## 1.1 The Vertebral Column [BOARD]

### 1.1.1 Overview and Regional Organization

The vertebral column serves as the axial skeleton of the trunk, providing structural support, protecting the spinal cord, and anchoring the musculature of the back and thorax. It consists of 33 vertebrae organized into five regions: 7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral (fused into the sacrum), and 4 coccygeal (variably fused into the coccyx). In the adult, only the upper 24 vertebrae remain mobile and are separated by intervertebral discs.

The column exhibits four physiologic curvatures. The thoracic and sacral curvatures are primary (kyphotic), present at birth, while the cervical and lumbar curvatures are secondary (lordotic), developing after birth with head control and upright ambulation, respectively. These curvatures increase the resilience and shock-absorbing capacity of the column.

### 1.1.2 Vertebral Structure

A typical vertebra consists of an anterior body (centrum) and a posterior vertebral (neural) arch. The body is the primary weight-bearing structure, composed of cancellous bone surrounded by a shell of cortical bone. The vertebral arch is formed by two pedicles and two laminae, which together with the posterior surface of the vertebral body create the vertebral foramen. The successive vertebral foramina form the vertebral (spinal) canal, housing the spinal cord, meninges, and associated vasculature.

Projecting from the vertebral arch are seven processes: one spinous process posteriorly, two transverse processes laterally, and four articular processes (two superior, two inferior) that form the zygapophyseal (facet) joints with adjacent vertebrae. The orientation of the facet joints varies by region and determines the predominant plane of motion at each spinal level.

**Cervical vertebrae (C1-C7)** are distinguished by their small bodies, bifid spinous processes (C2-C6), and the presence of transverse foramina transmitting the vertebral arteries (C1-C6). The atlas (C1) lacks a body and spinous process, consisting instead of anterior and posterior arches with two lateral masses. The axis (C2) features the dens (odontoid process), which projects superiorly from its body and articulates with the anterior arch of the atlas, permitting approximately 50% of cervical rotation.

**Thoracic vertebrae (T1-T12)** are characterized by costal facets on the bodies and transverse processes for articulation with the ribs. Their spinous processes are long and angled inferiorly. The facet joints are oriented coronally, favoring rotation.

**Lumbar vertebrae (L1-L5)** have the largest bodies, reflecting the increasing axial load borne at lower levels. Their spinous processes are short and horizontal, and their facet joints are oriented sagittally, favoring flexion and extension while limiting rotation.

### 1.1.3 Spinal Ligaments and Stability

The vertebral column is stabilized by a complex system of ligaments that constrain motion and protect the neural elements.

| Ligament | Location | Function | Clinical Significance |
|----------|----------|----------|----------------------|
| Anterior Longitudinal Ligament (ALL) | Anterior surface of vertebral bodies from occiput to sacrum | Resists hyperextension; strongest spinal ligament | Torn in extension injuries; calcification causes DISH |
| Posterior Longitudinal Ligament (PLL) | Posterior surface of vertebral bodies within spinal canal | Resists hyperflexion; reinforces disc posteriorly | Narrows from C2 caudally; thinnest at L5-S1; OPLL causes myelopathy |
| Ligamentum Flavum | Connects laminae of adjacent vertebrae | Resists separation of laminae in flexion; elastic | Hypertrophy contributes to spinal stenosis |
| Interspinous Ligament | Between adjacent spinous processes | Resists flexion and anterior shear | Disrupted in flexion injuries |
| Supraspinous Ligament | Posterior tips of spinous processes (C7 to sacrum) | Resists flexion | Becomes nuchal ligament in cervical region |
| Nuchal Ligament | Cervical midline from external occipital protuberance to C7 | Resists cervical flexion; origin for trapezius | Homologous to supraspinous ligament |
| Intertransverse Ligaments | Between adjacent transverse processes | Resists lateral flexion | Minor stabilizing role |
| Capsular Ligaments | Surround facet joints | Constrain facet joint motion | Disrupted in facet dislocations |

**Table 1-1. Major Spinal Ligaments**

The ALL is the only spinal ligament that limits extension and is the strongest of all spinal ligaments. It runs as a broad band from the anterior tubercle of the atlas and the basilar portion of the occiput down to the anterior sacrum, attaching firmly to the vertebral bodies and loosely to the intervertebral discs. The PLL runs within the vertebral canal along the posterior aspect of the vertebral bodies from the axis to the sacrum. Notably, it narrows progressively from cervical to lumbar levels, providing less reinforcement to the posterolateral disc at lower levels, which partly explains the higher frequency of posterolateral disc herniations in the lumbar spine.

The transverse ligament of the atlas is the strongest component of the cruciate ligament complex and is critical for atlantoaxial stability, preventing anterior displacement of C1 on C2. Disruption (as in rheumatoid arthritis or trauma) can result in life-threatening spinal cord compression.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The posterior ligamentous complex (PLC), consisting of the supraspinous ligament, interspinous ligament, ligamentum flavum, and facet capsules, is the key determinant of surgical versus nonsurgical management in thoracolumbar fractures. PLC disruption implies instability and typically warrants operative stabilization.

## 1.2 Gross Anatomy of the Spinal Cord [BOARD]

### 1.2.1 General Features

The spinal cord is a cylindrical structure of neural tissue continuous with the medulla oblongata at the foramen magnum. In the adult, the cord measures approximately 42-45 cm in length and terminates as the conus medullaris, typically at the lower border of the L1 vertebral body or the upper border of the L2 vertebral body (range: T12-L3). At birth, the conus extends to approximately the L3 level, ascending to its adult position by approximately 3 months of age due to differential growth between the vertebral column and the spinal cord.

The cord is anchored inferiorly by the filum terminale, a slender filament of pia mater extending from the tip of the conus to the posterior surface of the coccyx. The filum has two parts: the filum terminale internum (from conus to the caudal dural sac at approximately S2) and the filum terminale externum (coccygeal ligament, from S2 to the first coccygeal segment). Lateral anchoring is provided by the paired denticulate ligaments, extensions of pia mater that attach to the dura between the dorsal and ventral nerve roots, numbering 21 pairs on each side.

### 1.2.2 Enlargements

The spinal cord is not uniform in diameter. Two fusiform enlargements correspond to the segments innervating the limbs:

- **Cervical enlargement (C5-T1):** Corresponds to the brachial plexus and upper extremity innervation. This is the larger of the two enlargements and extends from approximately the C3 to T2 vertebral levels.
- **Lumbar enlargement (L1-S3):** Corresponds to the lumbosacral plexus and lower extremity innervation. It extends from approximately the T9 to T12 vertebral levels.

### 1.2.3 Conus Medullaris and Cauda Equina

Below the conus medullaris, the lumbar cistern (subarachnoid space from approximately L2 to S2) contains the cauda equina, a collection of dorsal and ventral nerve roots from lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal segments descending to their respective intervertebral foramina. The cauda equina consists of lower motor neuron (LMN) fibers and produces LMN signs when injured, in contrast to the upper motor neuron (UMN) signs associated with spinal cord lesions.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The conus medullaris ends at L1-L2 in adults. Lumbar puncture is therefore performed at the L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace to avoid spinal cord injury. The L4 spinous process is at the level of the iliac crest (Tuffier's line).

### 1.2.4 Spinal Nerves and Segmental Organization

There are 31 pairs of spinal nerves: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, and 1 coccygeal. In the cervical region, spinal nerves exit above their corresponding vertebra (e.g., the C5 nerve exits above the C5 vertebral body), except for C8, which exits below C7. Below the cervical region, all spinal nerves exit below their corresponding vertebra.

Each spinal nerve forms from the union of dorsal (sensory) and ventral (motor) roots at or near the intervertebral foramen. The dorsal root ganglion, located within the intervertebral foramen, contains the cell bodies of primary afferent (sensory) neurons.

### 1.2.5 Vertebral-Cord Level Discrepancy: The Rule of 1/2/3

Because the spinal cord is shorter than the vertebral column, a progressive discrepancy exists between vertebral (skeletal) and spinal cord (neurologic) levels. This relationship is clinically important for localizing cord lesions based on vertebral imaging.

The **Rule of 1/2/3** provides a clinically useful approximation:
- **Upper cervical spine (C1-C4):** Cord segments correspond approximately to the same vertebral level (add 0 to 1)
- **Lower cervical spine (C5-C7):** Add 1 to the vertebral level to estimate the cord segment (e.g., C6 vertebral body level contains approximately the C7 cord segment)
- **Upper thoracic spine (T1-T6):** Add 2 to the vertebral level
- **Lower thoracic spine (T7-T9):** Add 3 to the vertebral level
- **T10 vertebral body:** Contains L1-L2 cord segments
- **T11 vertebral body:** Contains L3-L4 cord segments
- **T12 vertebral body:** Contains L5 cord segment
- **L1 vertebral body:** Contains sacral and coccygeal cord segments (conus medullaris)

> **Clinical Pearl:** A fracture at the T10 vertebral body may produce a neurologic level of L1-L2 due to the vertebral-cord level discrepancy. Always correlate imaging findings with the clinical neurologic examination.

## 1.3 White Matter Tracts [BOARD]

The white matter of the spinal cord is organized into three bilateral funiculi (columns): dorsal (posterior), lateral, and anterior (ventral). Each contains specific ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) tracts with characteristic somatotopic organizations.

### 1.3.1 Ascending Tracts (Sensory)

#### Dorsal Column-Medial Lemniscus System

The dorsal columns transmit proprioception (joint position sense), vibration, fine (discriminative) touch, and two-point discrimination. They are composed of two fasciculi:

- **Fasciculus gracilis:** Located medially, carries fibers from the lower extremities and lower trunk (below T6). Present throughout the entire spinal cord.
- **Fasciculus cuneatus:** Located laterally, carries fibers from the upper extremities and upper trunk (above T6). Present only above T6.

First-order neurons have cell bodies in the dorsal root ganglia and send central processes through the dorsal root entry zone to ascend ipsilaterally in the dorsal columns. These fibers synapse on second-order neurons in the nucleus gracilis and nucleus cuneatus in the caudal medulla. Second-order neurons cross (decussate) as internal arcuate fibers and ascend as the medial lemniscus to the ventral posterolateral (VPL) nucleus of the thalamus. Third-order neurons project from the VPL to the primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus).

**Somatotopy:** Within the dorsal columns, fibers from sacral segments are most medial, while fibers from more rostral (cervical) segments are added progressively laterally. The mnemonic is: sacral segments are medial in the gracilis ("graceful inner position"), cervical segments are lateral in the cuneatus.

#### Spinothalamic Tracts

The spinothalamic tracts form the anterolateral system and transmit pain, temperature, and crude touch:

- **Lateral spinothalamic tract:** Carries pain and temperature sensation. First-order neurons synapse in the dorsal horn (primarily Rexed laminae I, II, and V). Second-order neurons cross the midline via the anterior white commissure within 1-2 segments above the entry level and ascend in the contralateral lateral funiculus to the VPL of the thalamus.
- **Anterior spinothalamic tract:** Carries crude touch and pressure. Follows a similar pathway but crosses more diffusely over several segments.

**Somatotopy:** Within the lateral spinothalamic tract, fibers are arranged with cervical segments most medial and sacral segments most lateral (the opposite arrangement from the corticospinal tract). This laminated arrangement has clinical significance: external compression of the cord (e.g., by a tumor) affects the outermost (sacral) fibers first, producing sacral-sparing patterns. Intramedullary lesions (e.g., syringomyelia) affect the innermost (cervical) fibers first.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The crossing of spinothalamic fibers in the anterior white commissure 1-2 segments above their entry level explains why lesions (such as a syrinx) affecting the central cord produce bilateral loss of pain and temperature at those dermatomes while preserving dorsal column function ("dissociated sensory loss" or "cape-like distribution").

#### Spinocerebellar Tracts

These tracts carry unconscious proprioceptive information from muscles and joints to the cerebellum:

- **Posterior (dorsal) spinocerebellar tract:** Carries proprioceptive input from the lower extremities. First-order neurons synapse in Clarke's nucleus (Rexed lamina VII, C8-L2). Second-order neurons ascend ipsilaterally in the lateral funiculus and enter the cerebellum via the inferior cerebellar peduncle.
- **Anterior (ventral) spinocerebellar tract:** Also carries proprioceptive input from the lower extremities but crosses the midline twice (once in the cord, once in the cerebellum), effectively remaining ipsilateral. Enters the cerebellum via the superior cerebellar peduncle.
- **Cuneocerebellar tract:** The upper extremity equivalent of the posterior spinocerebellar tract; relays from the accessory cuneate nucleus.

### 1.3.2 Descending Tracts (Motor)

#### Corticospinal Tracts

The corticospinal tract (CST) is the primary pathway for voluntary motor control. Upper motor neurons originate primarily in the primary motor cortex (Brodmann area 4, precentral gyrus), with contributions from the premotor cortex and supplementary motor area. Fibers descend through the corona radiata, posterior limb of the internal capsule, cerebral peduncle, basis pontis, and medullary pyramids.

- **Lateral corticospinal tract (LCST):** Contains approximately 85-90% of corticospinal fibers, which decussate at the pyramidal decussation in the caudal medulla. These fibers descend in the contralateral lateral funiculus and synapse on alpha motor neurons in the ventral horn (lamina IX) and interneurons in the intermediate zone (lamina VII). The LCST is the primary pathway for skilled, fractionated movements of the distal extremities.
- **Anterior corticospinal tract (ACST):** Contains approximately 10-15% of corticospinal fibers that remain uncrossed, descending in the ipsilateral anterior funiculus. Most fibers cross at segmental levels via the anterior white commissure before synapsing. The ACST primarily influences axial and proximal musculature.

**Somatotopy of the Lateral CST:** Fibers are laminated with cervical fibers most medial and sacral fibers most lateral. This is the reverse of the spinothalamic tract arrangement.

| Tract | Function | Crosses At | Cord Location | Somatotopy (Medial to Lateral) |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Dorsal Columns | Proprioception, vibration, fine touch | Medulla (internal arcuate fibers) | Posterior funiculus | Sacral → Cervical |
| Lateral Spinothalamic | Pain, temperature | Anterior white commissure (1-2 levels above entry) | Lateral funiculus | Cervical → Sacral |
| Anterior Spinothalamic | Crude touch, pressure | Anterior white commissure (diffuse, multi-level) | Anterior funiculus | Cervical → Sacral |
| Lateral Corticospinal | Voluntary motor (distal, skilled) | Pyramidal decussation (medulla) | Lateral funiculus | Cervical → Sacral |
| Anterior Corticospinal | Voluntary motor (axial, proximal) | Segmental anterior white commissure | Anterior funiculus | Not distinctly laminated |
| Posterior Spinocerebellar | Unconscious proprioception (LE) | Uncrossed | Lateral funiculus (posterior) | N/A |
| Anterior Spinocerebellar | Unconscious proprioception (LE) | Crosses twice (net ipsilateral) | Lateral funiculus (anterior) | N/A |

**Table 1-2. Major White Matter Tracts of the Spinal Cord**

#### Other Descending Tracts

- **Rubrospinal tract:** Originates from the red nucleus, crosses in the ventral tegmentum, descends in the lateral funiculus adjacent to the LCST. Facilitates flexor motor neurons; clinically significant in decerebrate/decorticate posturing.
- **Reticulospinal tracts:** The medullary (lateral) reticulospinal tract facilitates flexors and inhibits extensors. The pontine (medial) reticulospinal tract facilitates extensors and inhibits flexors. These tracts influence posture, muscle tone, and autonomic functions.
- **Vestibulospinal tracts:** The lateral vestibulospinal tract (from the lateral vestibular nucleus) facilitates extensor tone and is involved in postural reflexes. The medial vestibulospinal tract (from the medial vestibular nucleus) descends in the medial longitudinal fasciculus and influences neck musculature.
- **Tectospinal tract:** Originates from the superior colliculus, crosses in the dorsal tegmentum, descends in the anterior funiculus. Mediates reflex head turning toward visual or auditory stimuli.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The somatotopic arrangement of the lateral CST (cervical medial, sacral lateral) means that extramedullary (external) compression produces motor deficits starting in the lower extremities (sacral fibers compressed first), while intramedullary lesions tend to affect upper extremity function first (central cervical fibers affected first). This distinction is crucial for differentiating intramedullary versus extramedullary pathology.

## 1.4 Gray Matter Organization [BOARD]

### 1.4.1 General Organization

In cross-section, the spinal cord gray matter forms a butterfly-shaped (or H-shaped) structure surrounded by white matter. The gray matter is divided into dorsal (posterior) horns, ventral (anterior) horns, and, in the thoracic and upper lumbar cord, lateral horns. The gray commissure surrounds the central canal, which contains cerebrospinal fluid and is lined by ependymal cells.

### 1.4.2 Dorsal Horn

The dorsal horn is the primary site for processing sensory afferent information. It receives input from primary afferent neurons entering through the dorsal roots and contains interneurons and projection neurons that relay information to higher centers.

Key functional zones include:

- **Marginal zone (Rexed lamina I):** Receives nociceptive and thermoreceptive afferents (A-delta and C fibers). Contains projection neurons that give rise to the spinothalamic tract.
- **Substantia gelatinosa (Rexed lamina II):** A translucent band of small neurons receiving unmyelinated C fiber afferents. This is a critical integration site for pain modulation, containing interneurons that gate pain transmission according to the Melzack-Wall gate control theory. It is the site of action of endogenous opioids (enkephalin).
- **Nucleus proprius (Rexed laminae III-IV):** Receives light touch and proprioceptive afferents from A-beta fibers. Contains wide dynamic range neurons and projection neurons.
- **Neck of dorsal horn (Rexed lamina V):** Contains wide dynamic range neurons receiving convergent somatic and visceral afferents. This convergence underlies referred pain phenomena.
- **Base of dorsal horn (Rexed lamina VI):** Present mainly in the cervical and lumbar enlargements. Receives proprioceptive afferents from muscle spindles (group Ia) and Golgi tendon organs (group Ib).

### 1.4.3 Intermediate Zone and Lateral Horn

**Rexed lamina VII** occupies the intermediate zone and contains several functionally important nuclei:

- **Clarke's nucleus (nucleus dorsalis):** Located at the medial base of the dorsal horn from C8 to L2-L3. Receives proprioceptive afferents from the lower extremities and gives rise to the posterior spinocerebellar tract. Its limited segmental extent (C8-L2) means that proprioceptive fibers from below L2 must first ascend in the fasciculus gracilis to reach Clarke's nucleus.
- **Intermediolateral cell column (IML):** Located in the lateral horn at T1-L2 (thoracolumbar cord). Contains preganglionic sympathetic neurons. Their axons exit through the ventral roots as white rami communicantes to reach the sympathetic chain ganglia. This is the origin of the sympathetic outflow to the body.
- **Sacral parasympathetic nucleus:** Located in the lateral gray matter at S2-S4. Contains preganglionic parasympathetic neurons whose axons exit through the ventral roots as the pelvic splanchnic nerves (nervi erigentes) to innervate the descending colon, rectum, bladder, and reproductive organs.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The IML at T1-L2 is the origin of sympathetic preganglionic neurons. Spinal cord injuries above T6 disrupt descending supraspinal control of the IML, which is the anatomic basis for autonomic dysreflexia. The intact splanchnic sympathetic outflow below the lesion can generate massive, unopposed vasoconstriction in response to noxious stimuli below the injury level.

### 1.4.4 Ventral Horn

The ventral horn contains the motor neurons that directly innervate skeletal muscle:

- **Rexed lamina VIII:** Contains commissural interneurons and propriospinal neurons involved in coordinating bilateral motor activity and postural control.
- **Rexed lamina IX:** Contains the alpha motor neurons (innervating extrafusal muscle fibers for force generation), gamma motor neurons (innervating intrafusal muscle fibers of muscle spindles for proprioceptive sensitivity), and beta motor neurons (innervating both). The motor neurons are arranged somatotopically:
  - **Medial motor neuron pools:** Innervate axial (trunk and proximal) musculature. Present throughout the cord.
  - **Lateral motor neuron pools:** Innervate distal limb musculature. Present primarily in the cervical and lumbar enlargements.
  - **Flexor motor neurons:** Located posteriorly within the ventral horn.
  - **Extensor motor neurons:** Located anteriorly within the ventral horn.

**Rexed lamina X** surrounds the central canal and contains commissural neurons and decussating fibers.

## 1.5 Rexed Laminae: Functional Significance [COMPREHENSIVE]

The Rexed laminar system, first described by Swedish neuroanatomist Bror Rexed in 1952, replaced earlier inconsistent nomenclature with a uniform cytoarchitectonic framework. Understanding the functional significance of each lamina extends beyond basic anatomy into clinical neuroscience and pharmacology.

**Lamina I (marginal zone)** contains neurons that express the neurokinin-1 receptor (NK1R), which is the receptor for substance P released by nociceptive afferents. These neurons are key mediators of chronic pain pathways, and selective ablation of NK1R-expressing neurons in lamina I has been shown in animal models to attenuate hyperalgesia without affecting acute pain.

**Lamina II (substantia gelatinosa)** is the pharmacological target of many analgesic interventions. Opioid receptors (particularly mu and delta) are concentrated here, and intrathecal opioid delivery acts primarily at this level. The gate control theory proposes that non-nociceptive A-beta fiber input can inhibit pain transmission through lamina II interneurons, forming the theoretical basis for transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) and dorsal column stimulators.

**Lamina V** is the site of viscerosomatic convergence, where neurons receive input from both somatic structures and visceral organs sharing the same segmental innervation. This convergence explains referred pain patterns: cardiac ischemia produces left arm pain (C8-T1 convergence), renal calculi cause flank-to-groin pain (T10-L1), and gallbladder disease causes right scapular pain (C3-C5, via phrenic nerve afferents).

**Lamina VII** is functionally diverse. Beyond the autonomic nuclei described above, it contains the central pattern generators for locomotion, reciprocal inhibitory interneurons (Renshaw cells reside at the border with lamina IX), and descending reticulospinal and vestibulospinal tract terminations that regulate postural tone.

**Lamina IX** motor neuron pools follow a precise somatotopic map. In the cervical enlargement, motor neurons for hand intrinsics are located most laterally, while those for shoulder girdle muscles are more medial. Understanding this organization explains why central cord syndrome preferentially affects hand function (the most lateral motor neuron pools in the cervical enlargement are most vulnerable to central lesions affecting the surrounding white matter).

## 1.6 Autonomic Pathway Neuroanatomy [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 1.6.1 Sympathetic Nervous System

The sympathetic preganglionic neurons reside in the intermediolateral cell column (IML) from T1 to L2. These neurons receive descending input from hypothalamic, pontine, and medullary autonomic centers, particularly the rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM) and the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus. This descending input travels primarily in the dorsolateral funiculus.

Preganglionic sympathetic axons (myelinated B fibers) exit through the ventral roots and enter the sympathetic chain via white rami communicantes. They may synapse at the level of entry, ascend, or descend within the chain to synapse at a different level, or pass through the chain without synapsing to reach prevertebral ganglia (celiac, superior mesenteric, inferior mesenteric) via the splanchnic nerves.

Specific sympathetic outflows of clinical importance include:

- **T1-T4:** Cardioacceleratory fibers to the heart via the superior, middle, and inferior (stellate) cervical sympathetic ganglia and cardiac nerves
- **T1-T2:** Ciliospinal center of Budge; preganglionic neurons for the oculosympathetic pathway (pupillary dilation, eyelid elevation, facial sweating). Disruption produces Horner syndrome (miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis)
- **T5-L2:** Splanchnic outflow; major vasomotor control of the splanchnic vascular bed, containing approximately 25% of total blood volume. This is the anatomic basis for autonomic dysreflexia
- **T10-T12:** Innervation to the uterus, relevant for labor-induced autonomic dysreflexia
- **T11-L2:** Innervation of the lower urinary tract detrusor and sphincter mechanisms via the hypogastric nerve (promotes urine storage through detrusor relaxation and internal sphincter contraction)

### 1.6.2 Parasympathetic Nervous System

The spinal parasympathetic outflow originates from the sacral parasympathetic nucleus at S2-S4 (craniosacral outflow). Preganglionic fibers exit through the ventral roots and form the pelvic splanchnic nerves, which innervate:

- **Bladder detrusor muscle:** Parasympathetic stimulation causes detrusor contraction (voiding)
- **Rectum and distal colon:** Parasympathetic stimulation increases motility and relaxes the internal anal sphincter
- **Erectile tissue:** Parasympathetic stimulation mediates reflexogenic erections via nitric oxide-mediated vasodilation (S2-S4 "point and shoot" mnemonic: parasympathetic erection, sympathetic emission)

The Onuf nucleus (a specialized group of motor neurons in lamina IX at S2-S4) innervates the external urethral sphincter and external anal sphincter. Though somatically innervated, these muscles have unique properties: they are tonically active at rest (unlike other skeletal muscles) and are relatively resistant to motor neuron disease (preserving continence in ALS).

### 1.6.3 Descending Autonomic Pathways

Supraspinal autonomic control descends primarily through the lateral funiculus in a region overlapping with the lateral corticospinal tract. Key descending autonomic pathways include:

- **Hypothalamospinal tract:** From the paraventricular nucleus to the IML; involved in blood pressure regulation, thermoregulation, and water balance
- **Medullary reticulospinal projections:** From the RVLM (pressor) and caudal ventrolateral medulla (depressor) to the IML; tonically maintain sympathetic vasomotor tone
- **Pontine micturition center projections:** From Barrington's nucleus in the pons to the sacral parasympathetic nucleus; coordinate the micturition reflex by simultaneously contracting the detrusor and relaxing the external sphincter

> **Clinical Pearl:** The descending autonomic pathways run in the lateral funiculus, intermingled with the corticospinal tract. This anatomic proximity explains why patients with lateral cord lesions often have concurrent motor deficits and autonomic dysfunction (e.g., Horner syndrome with lateral medullary or cervical cord lesions).

## 1.7 Vascular Supply of the Spinal Cord [BOARD]

### 1.7.1 Arterial Supply

The spinal cord receives its arterial supply from three longitudinal arteries and segmental reinforcing vessels.

#### Longitudinal Arteries

- **Anterior spinal artery (ASA):** A single midline artery formed by the union of branches from the intracranial vertebral arteries at the level of the foramen magnum. It descends along the anterior median fissure and supplies the anterior two-thirds of the spinal cord, including the ventral horns (motor neurons), spinothalamic tracts, and the anterior portion of the lateral funiculus (including the corticospinal tracts).
- **Posterior spinal arteries (PSA):** Paired arteries originating from the vertebral arteries or the posterior inferior cerebellar arteries (PICA). They descend along the posterolateral sulci, medial to the dorsal roots, and supply the posterior one-third of the spinal cord, including the dorsal columns and the dorsal horns.

The ASA is not a single continuous vessel from top to bottom but rather a series of anastomotic channels that are dependent on segmental reinforcement. The PSAs similarly form a plexiform network (the vasocorona) with variable contributions from segmental arteries.

#### Segmental (Radicular) Arteries

The longitudinal arteries are reinforced by segmental arteries derived from the vertebral, ascending cervical, deep cervical, intercostal, lumbar, and sacral arteries. These segmental vessels enter the vertebral canal through the intervertebral foramina and divide into anterior and posterior radiculomedullary arteries that join the longitudinal system.

Not all segmental arteries contribute to the spinal cord blood supply. Most supply only the nerve roots (radicular arteries). Only 6-8 anterior and 10-20 posterior radiculomedullary arteries reach and reinforce the longitudinal system. The number and distribution of these reinforcing vessels are highly variable, contributing to the vulnerability of certain cord segments.

#### Artery of Adamkiewicz (Great Anterior Radiculomedullary Artery)

The artery of Adamkiewicz is the largest and most important segmental reinforcement to the anterior spinal artery. Key features include:

- **Origin:** Typically arises from the left side of the aorta (approximately 75-80% of individuals)
- **Level:** Most commonly arises between T9 and T12 (range T8-L3, with approximately 75% arising between T9 and T12)
- **Course:** Enters the intervertebral foramen, follows the ventral root, and joins the ASA. It has a characteristic "hairpin turn" as it ascends briefly then turns to descend and join the ASA
- **Clinical significance:** Supplies the lower two-thirds of the spinal cord. Compromise during aortic surgery (thoracoabdominal aneurysm repair), vertebral surgery, or by aortic dissection/occlusion can produce anterior cord syndrome

#### Intrinsic Cord Circulation

The ASA gives rise to central (sulcal/sulcocommissural) arteries that penetrate the anterior median fissure and supply the central gray matter and inner portions of the white matter. The PSAs and the vasocorona supply the peripheral white matter and the dorsal horns.

### 1.7.2 Watershed Zones

Watershed zones are regions at the boundary between two arterial territories where perfusion pressure is lowest, making them most susceptible to ischemic injury during systemic hypotension:

- **T4-T8 (mid-thoracic watershed, sometimes cited as T4-T6):** This is the most clinically significant watershed zone. The thoracic cord receives relatively few segmental reinforcements, lying between the cervical arterial territory (reinforced by vertebral and cervical arteries) and the lower thoracolumbar territory (reinforced by the artery of Adamkiewicz). This makes the mid-thoracic cord vulnerable to ischemia during aortic cross-clamping, hypotension, or aortic dissection.
- **Peripheral rim of the cord (border zone between central and peripheral circulations):** The boundary between the central sulcal arteries (from the ASA) and the pial arterial plexus (vasocorona) creates a circumferential watershed zone. This area is vulnerable in chronic compression myelopathy and may contribute to the pathogenesis of central cord syndrome.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The T4-T8 mid-thoracic watershed zone is the most vulnerable segment of the spinal cord to ischemic injury during periods of systemic hypoperfusion. Patients undergoing aortic surgery are at particular risk for paraplegia if the artery of Adamkiewicz is compromised. Intraoperative spinal cord monitoring (somatosensory and motor evoked potentials) and maintenance of adequate perfusion pressure are essential preventive measures.

### 1.7.3 Venous Drainage

The venous drainage of the spinal cord consists of intrinsic and extrinsic systems that ultimately drain into the epidural internal vertebral venous plexus (Batson plexus).

**Intrinsic veins** drain the cord parenchyma into the anterior and posterior spinal veins running along the midline. Six longitudinal venous channels form the external spinal venous plexus (coronal venous plexus) on the surface of the cord.

**The Batson venous plexus (internal vertebral venous plexus)** is a network of thin-walled, valveless veins located in the epidural space. Its characteristics include:

- **Valveless system:** Allows bidirectional flow depending on intra-abdominal and intrathoracic pressure changes. During Valsalva maneuver, coughing, or straining, increased abdominal/thoracic pressure shunts blood into the vertebral venous system.
- **Extensive anastomoses:** The plexus communicates freely with pelvic, abdominal, thoracic, and intracranial venous systems, providing an alternative route for venous return.
- **Clinical significance for metastasis:** The valveless nature of Batson plexus provides a pathway for hematogenous spread of malignancy from pelvic, abdominal, and thoracic organs to the vertebral bodies and epidural space, bypassing the portal and caval venous systems. This explains the predilection of prostatic, breast, lung, thyroid, and renal carcinomas for vertebral metastasis.
- **Epidural hematoma risk:** The thin-walled epidural veins are susceptible to rupture, particularly in the setting of anticoagulation, coagulopathy, or trauma, potentially producing spinal epidural hematoma with cord compression.

## 1.8 Meninges and Cerebrospinal Fluid Spaces [BOARD]

The spinal cord is surrounded by three meningeal layers continuous with the cranial meninges:

- **Dura mater (pachymeninx):** The outermost, toughest layer. The spinal dura is a single layer (unlike the two-layered cranial dura) and extends from the foramen magnum to approximately S2, where it tapers as the dural sac. Between the dura and the vertebral periosteum lies the epidural space, which contains fat, the internal vertebral venous plexus, and segmental arteries. This is the target space for epidural anesthesia.
- **Arachnoid mater:** A delicate, avascular membrane attached to the inner surface of the dura. The subarachnoid space between the arachnoid and pia contains CSF and is traversed by arachnoid trabeculae. The lumbar cistern (L2 to S2) within the subarachnoid space is the site of lumbar puncture and intrathecal drug delivery.
- **Pia mater:** The innermost layer, intimately adherent to the spinal cord surface. It gives rise to the denticulate ligaments laterally and the filum terminale inferiorly. The pia also forms the linea splendens, a thickened midline band over the anterior median fissure.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The epidural space is a true space containing fat and veins, whereas the subdural space is only a potential space. Epidural hematomas tend to be more focal and biconvex, while subdural hematomas spread more diffusely. Both can cause acute cord compression requiring emergent surgical evacuation.

## Summary

The spinal cord is a precisely organized neural structure protected within the vertebral column. Its white matter tracts are somatotopically arranged, with the lateral corticospinal tract exhibiting cervical-medial and sacral-lateral fiber organization (opposite to the spinothalamic tract arrangement). The gray matter contains functionally specialized Rexed laminae, with the intermediolateral cell column (T1-L2) providing sympathetic outflow and the sacral parasympathetic nucleus (S2-S4) controlling bladder, bowel, and sexual function. The anterior spinal artery supplies the anterior two-thirds of the cord, while the posterior spinal arteries supply the posterior one-third. The artery of Adamkiewicz, arising from the left side in approximately 80% of individuals between T9 and L2, is the dominant segmental reinforcement to the lower cord. The T4-T8 mid-thoracic region represents a critical watershed zone vulnerable to ischemia. The valveless Batson venous plexus provides a route for metastatic spread to the vertebral column. Understanding these anatomic relationships is essential for localizing spinal cord lesions and predicting clinical syndromes.
