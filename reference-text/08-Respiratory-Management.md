# Chapter 8: Respiratory Management in Spinal Cord Injury

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Identify the level-dependent innervation of the respiratory muscles and predict the respiratory impairment expected at each level of spinal cord injury.
2. Explain the pathophysiology of paradoxical breathing in cervical SCI and the mechanism by which spasticity improves respiratory function over time.
3. Describe the assessment and management of impaired cough, including manually assisted cough techniques and mechanical insufflation-exsufflation.
4. Outline the criteria and methodology for progressive ventilator-free breathing (PVFB) as a weaning strategy.
5. Identify candidacy criteria for phrenic nerve pacing and explain why lower motor neuron damage to C3-C5 is a contraindication.
6. Discuss the role of abdominal binders, glossopharyngeal breathing, and tracheostomy in respiratory management.
7. Recognize sleep-disordered breathing as a common comorbidity in cervical SCI and describe appropriate screening and management.

---

## 8.1 Introduction `[BOARD]`

Respiratory complications are the **leading cause of morbidity and mortality** in both the acute and chronic phases of spinal cord injury. Pneumonia is the number one cause of death in persons with SCI across all time periods, and respiratory failure is responsible for approximately 42% of deaths during the acute phase (Berlly and Shem, 2007). The severity of respiratory impairment is directly related to the level and completeness of injury: higher and more complete injuries produce greater respiratory muscle paralysis and more severe ventilatory compromise.

Understanding the segmental innervation of respiratory muscles is essential for predicting the respiratory consequences of SCI at any given level and for designing rational management strategies.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Respiratory complications are the number one killer in SCI --- both acutely and chronically. The clinician managing SCI patients must approach respiratory assessment and prevention with the same urgency as spinal stabilization and neurologic evaluation.

## 8.2 Respiratory Muscle Innervation `[BOARD]`

### 8.2.1 Primary Inspiratory Muscle: The Diaphragm

The diaphragm is the principal muscle of inspiration, responsible for generating approximately 65-75% of tidal volume during quiet breathing. It is innervated by the **phrenic nerve**, which arises from the **C3, C4, and C5** nerve roots (mnemonic: "C3, C4, C5 keeps the diaphragm alive"). The phrenic nerve is a pure motor nerve to the diaphragm, with sensory branches to the central diaphragm, pericardium, and peritoneal surfaces.

**Clinical Implications:**

- Injuries at C1-C2 result in complete diaphragmatic paralysis and ventilator dependence
- Injuries at C3-C4 produce partial diaphragmatic weakness; many patients require at least temporary ventilatory support
- Injuries at C5 and below generally spare diaphragm function, though accessory muscle loss and intercostal paralysis still impair respiratory mechanics significantly

### 8.2.2 Intercostal Muscles

The intercostal muscles are innervated segmentally by the intercostal nerves at **T1-T11**.

**External intercostals:** Elevate the ribs during inspiration, expanding the thoracic cavity. They function as **inspiratory** muscles. Their contribution is particularly important during deep inspiration and exercise.

**Internal intercostals (interosseous portion):** Depress the ribs during forced expiration. They function as **expiratory** muscles. The parasternal (interchondral) portion of the internal intercostals is inspiratory.

In cervical SCI, paralysis of the intercostal muscles results in loss of chest wall stabilization during inspiration, contributing to paradoxical breathing mechanics.

### 8.2.3 Abdominal Muscles

The abdominal muscles are the primary muscles of **forced expiration and cough**. They are innervated by thoracic nerve roots at **T6-T12** (with some contribution from T5):

- **Rectus abdominis:** T7-T12
- **External oblique:** T7-T12
- **Internal oblique:** T7-L1
- **Transversus abdominis:** T7-L1

These muscles generate the high intrathoracic pressures required for effective cough. Their paralysis in cervical and high thoracic SCI is the primary reason for impaired secretion clearance and susceptibility to atelectasis and pneumonia.

### 8.2.4 Accessory Muscles of Respiration

| Muscle | Innervation | Function | Preserved in Cervical SCI? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sternocleidomastoid (SCM) | Spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) + C2-C3 | Elevates sternum; accessory inspiration | Yes (cranial nerve + high cervical roots) |
| Scalenes (anterior, middle, posterior) | C3-C8 | Elevate ribs 1-2; accessory inspiration | Partially (depends on level) |
| Trapezius | Spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) + C3-C4 | Stabilizes scapula; assists respiration indirectly | Yes (CN XI + high cervical roots) |
| Pectoralis major | C5-T1 (lateral and medial pectoral nerves) | Accessory inspiration (when arms fixed) | Partially (depends on level) |

In high cervical SCI (C1-C4), the SCM and trapezius --- innervated primarily by the spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) --- become the principal available respiratory muscles. These accessory muscles alone are insufficient to sustain adequate ventilation in most patients with high cervical complete injuries.

### 8.2.5 Respiratory Muscle Innervation Summary

| Level of Injury | Muscles Available | Respiratory Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| C1-C2 | SCM, trapezius only | Complete ventilator dependence |
| C3-C4 | SCM, trapezius, partial diaphragm | Usually requires ventilatory support acutely; some may wean |
| C5-C8 | Diaphragm intact, accessory muscles, NO intercostals/abdominals | Impaired cough; paradoxical breathing; FVC reduced ~50% |
| T1-T5 | Diaphragm + accessory + partial intercostals | Moderately impaired cough; reduced FVC |
| T6-T12 | Diaphragm + intercostals + partial abdominals | Mildly impaired cough; near-normal FVC in lower thoracic |
| L1 and below | All respiratory muscles intact | No significant respiratory impairment from SCI |

## 8.3 Acute Respiratory Pathophysiology `[BOARD]`

### 8.3.1 Reduction in Forced Vital Capacity

In the acute phase of cervical SCI, FVC decreases by approximately **50%** from predicted values. This reduction results from the combined loss of intercostal and abdominal muscle function, which impairs both the inspiratory expansion and expiratory compression of the thorax.

### 8.3.2 Paradoxical Breathing

In normal breathing, the diaphragm descends (abdomen protrudes) and the chest wall expands (ribs elevate) simultaneously during inspiration. In cervical SCI with intercostal paralysis, a characteristic **paradoxical breathing pattern** develops:

- **Abdomen protrudes** during inspiration (normal diaphragmatic descent --- preserved)
- **Chest wall retracts** during inspiration (abnormal --- because the paralyzed intercostal muscles cannot stabilize the chest wall, and the negative intrathoracic pressure generated by diaphragmatic contraction pulls the flaccid chest wall inward)

This is the **opposite of normal breathing** mechanics and represents a highly inefficient breathing pattern that increases the work of breathing and reduces effective tidal volume.

### 8.3.3 Improvement with Spasticity

A paradoxical but clinically important phenomenon occurs as spinal shock resolves and spasticity develops in the weeks to months following injury: **FVC improves** as intercostal spasticity increases. The developing tonic contraction of the intercostal muscles re-stabilizes the chest wall, preventing the paradoxical inward collapse during inspiration. This spasticity-mediated improvement in chest wall mechanics can increase FVC by 10-30% over the acute phase values.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Paradoxical breathing (chest retracts while abdomen protrudes during inspiration) is the hallmark of cervical SCI respiratory mechanics. Counterintuitively, the development of intercostal spasticity over weeks to months actually IMPROVES respiratory function by re-stabilizing the chest wall.

### 8.3.4 Positional Effects on Breathing

In cervical SCI, respiratory mechanics are paradoxically **better in the supine position** than upright. This is because in the supine position, the abdominal viscera push the diaphragm cephalad into a more domed (mechanically advantageous) position, and the weight of the viscera provides passive resistance against which the diaphragm can contract more effectively. In the upright position, the abdominal contents fall away from the diaphragm, reducing its resting length and mechanical advantage. This is the opposite of the normal physiologic response (healthy individuals breathe more efficiently upright).

## 8.4 Cough Assessment and Management `[BOARD]`

### 8.4.1 Cough Effectiveness

Effective cough requires the generation of high expiratory flow rates to mobilize and expel secretions from the airways. The **peak cough flow (PCF)** threshold for effective secretion clearance is approximately **270 L/min** (some sources cite 160 L/min as the minimum for adequate clearance during respiratory infections).

Cough effectiveness depends primarily on the abdominal muscles (T6-T12), which generate the high intrathoracic pressures needed for the explosive expiratory phase of cough. In cervical SCI, where abdominal muscles are completely paralyzed, cough is severely impaired or absent.

### 8.4.2 Respiratory Complications

| Complication | Significance |
|---|---|
| Atelectasis | Most common acute respiratory complication; caused by mucus plugging + inability to take deep breaths + poor cough |
| Pneumonia | Leading cause of death in SCI (all time periods); results from retained secretions, aspiration, and impaired defense mechanisms |
| Mucus plugging | Thick secretions obstruct airways; exacerbated by dehydration, supplemental O2 without humidification, anticholinergic medications |
| Respiratory failure | Occurs acutely from muscle paralysis; may develop insidiously from fatigue in patients with marginal reserve |

### 8.4.3 Assisted Cough Techniques

**Manually Assisted Cough (Quad Cough):**

A caregiver or therapist places their hands over the patient's epigastrium or lateral costal margins and delivers a forceful inward and upward thrust synchronized with the patient's expiratory effort (similar to a Heimlich maneuver but applied as the patient attempts to cough). This substitutes for the paralyzed abdominal muscles, generating the intrathoracic pressure required for secretion clearance.

**Technique:**

1. Patient takes a maximal inspiration (or is given a deep breath via manual ventilation bag)
2. At the point of maximum inspiration, the patient closes the glottis briefly
3. As the patient opens the glottis to cough, the caregiver delivers a firm abdominal thrust
4. The combined diaphragmatic contraction (inspiration) and external abdominal compression (expiration) generates a more effective cough

**Self-Assisted Cough:** Patients with sufficient upper extremity function (C6-C7) can perform a self-assisted cough by pressing their forearm or fist into their epigastrium during attempted exhalation.

### 8.4.4 Mechanical Insufflation-Exsufflation (MI-E / CoughAssist)

MI-E devices (commonly known by the brand name CoughAssist) deliver alternating positive pressure (insufflation) followed by rapidly applied negative pressure (exsufflation), simulating the inspiratory and expiratory phases of a natural cough.

**Settings:**

- Insufflation pressure: +30 to +40 cmH2O
- Exsufflation pressure: -30 to -40 cmH2O
- Cycle: 2-3 seconds insufflation, 2-3 seconds exsufflation, pause
- Typically delivered via facemask or tracheostomy adapter
- Sessions of 4-5 cycles, repeated as needed

**Indications:** Patients with PCF <270 L/min, recurrent atelectasis, or mucus plugging despite manually assisted cough.

**Evidence:** MI-E has been shown to increase peak cough flow, reduce atelectasis, and decrease the incidence of pneumonia and respiratory hospitalizations in patients with neuromuscular weakness and SCI (Bach et al., 1993; Pillastrini et al., 2006).

> **Clinical Pearl:** A peak cough flow below 270 L/min indicates inadequate cough for secretion clearance. These patients require assisted cough techniques --- either manually assisted (quad cough) or mechanical insufflation-exsufflation (CoughAssist) --- to prevent atelectasis and pneumonia.

## 8.5 Ventilatory Support and Weaning `[BOARD]`

### 8.5.1 Indications for Mechanical Ventilation

- Vital capacity (VC) **less than 1 liter** (or <15 mL/kg) generally indicates the need for ventilatory support
- Respiratory failure (PaO2 <60 mmHg or PaCO2 >50 mmHg on room air)
- Clinical signs of respiratory distress or fatigue
- Complete cervical SCI at C4 or above nearly always requires at least temporary mechanical ventilation

### 8.5.2 Progressive Ventilator-Free Breathing (PVFB)

PVFB is the **standard approach** to weaning ventilator-dependent SCI patients. Unlike the gradual reduction of ventilator rate used in synchronized intermittent mandatory ventilation (SIMV) weaning, PVFB involves complete disconnection from the ventilator for progressively longer periods.

**Rationale:** PVFB trains the diaphragm and accessory muscles as endurance muscles through progressive loading, analogous to an exercise training program. Each period of spontaneous breathing serves as a training session.

**Protocol:**

1. **Readiness criteria:**
   - FVC greater than **15 mL/kg**
   - Negative inspiratory force (NIF) more negative than **-20 cmH2O**
   - SpO2 greater than **95%** on FiO2 ≤0.40
   - Hemodynamic stability
   - No active infection or metabolic derangement
   - Adequate nutrition

2. **Initial trials:** Begin with short periods off the ventilator --- typically **5-15 minutes** --- during which the patient breathes spontaneously through the tracheostomy with supplemental oxygen and continuous monitoring

3. **Progression:** Increase the duration of ventilator-free time by 5-15 minutes per session, with multiple sessions per day. Allow full ventilator rest between sessions to prevent fatigue

4. **Monitoring:** SpO2, respiratory rate, tidal volume, heart rate, and subjective comfort during each trial. Terminate the trial if SpO2 drops below 95%, respiratory rate exceeds 30/min, or the patient reports dyspnea or fatigue

5. **Advancement:** As tolerance builds, trials are extended to hours, then overnight, and eventually full 24-hour ventilator freedom

**Evidence:** PVFB has been shown to be more successful than SIMV weaning for patients with C3-C4 SCI. Approximately 40-60% of patients with C3-C4 motor complete SCI can eventually be weaned from mechanical ventilation using PVFB (Peterson et al., 1999).

### 8.5.3 Weaning Adjuncts

- **Adequate nutrition:** Respiratory muscles require adequate caloric and protein intake for endurance training; malnutrition impairs weaning success
- **Secretion management:** Aggressive pulmonary toilet (suctioning, MI-E, chest physiotherapy) prevents atelectasis that could complicate weaning
- **Psychological support:** Ventilator dependence causes significant anxiety; patient education, reassurance, and gradual exposure are essential for successful weaning
- **Optimal positioning:** Supine position may improve diaphragmatic mechanics during weaning trials (see Section 8.3.4)
- **Abdominal binder:** Improves diaphragmatic mechanics in the upright position (see Section 8.7)

> **Clinical Pearl:** PVFB (progressive ventilator-free breathing) --- not SIMV weaning --- is the standard approach for weaning ventilator-dependent SCI patients. Start with short trials (5-15 minutes), increase gradually, and allow full rest between sessions.

## 8.6 Phrenic Nerve Pacing `[BOARD]`

### 8.6.1 Principle

Phrenic nerve pacing (PNP) or diaphragm pacing stimulation (DPS) uses surgically implanted electrodes to deliver electrical stimulation to the phrenic nerve or directly to the diaphragm, producing rhythmic diaphragmatic contraction that generates ventilation without a mechanical ventilator.

### 8.6.2 Candidacy Criteria

The critical prerequisite for phrenic nerve pacing is an **intact phrenic nerve lower motor neuron pathway**. The phrenic nerve motor neurons reside at C3-C5; therefore:

- **Candidates:** Patients with ventilator-dependent SCI at **C1-C2** (injury ABOVE the phrenic motor neurons), where the phrenic nerve and diaphragm are intact but disconnected from brainstem respiratory centers
- **NOT candidates:** Patients with **LMN damage to C3-C5** (denervated diaphragm); if the phrenic motor neurons or the phrenic nerve itself is damaged, electrical stimulation cannot produce diaphragmatic contraction because the end organ (diaphragm) is denervated

### 8.6.3 Pre-Implantation Evaluation

Before implantation, the integrity of the phrenic nerve-diaphragm unit must be confirmed:

1. **Phrenic nerve conduction studies (PNCS):** Stimulation of the phrenic nerve in the neck with recording of the diaphragmatic compound muscle action potential (CMAP). Absent or severely reduced CMAP suggests phrenic nerve or diaphragm denervation.

2. **Diaphragm EMG:** Needle electromyography of the diaphragm to assess for evidence of denervation (fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves) versus viable motor units.

3. **Diaphragm fluoroscopy or ultrasound:** Assessment of diaphragmatic excursion during spontaneous or phrenic-stimulated breathing.

**Important caveat:** Recent evidence suggests that PNCS alone may not be sufficient to determine candidacy; diagnostic laparoscopy with direct diaphragm evaluation has been advocated to detect stimulable diaphragm muscle even in patients with abnormal PNCS results (Onders et al., 2020).

### 8.6.4 Implantation Approaches

**Phrenic Nerve Electrodes:** Surgically placed around the phrenic nerve in the neck or thorax (open or thoracoscopic approach). Stimulation is delivered to the nerve, producing contraction of the entire hemidiaphragm.

**Intramuscular Diaphragm Electrodes (NeuRx):** Placed laparoscopically directly into the diaphragm muscle at the motor point. This approach is less invasive and can be performed even in patients with incomplete phrenic nerve injuries, as it stimulates remaining viable motor units.

### 8.6.5 Outcomes

- Approximately 50% of appropriately selected patients achieve full-time (24-hour) ventilator independence with phrenic pacing
- An additional 20-30% achieve part-time ventilator independence (4-16 hours/day of pacing)
- Benefits over mechanical ventilation include: more natural breathing pattern, improved speech and swallowing, reduced pulmonary complications, increased mobility, and improved quality of life
- Phrenic pacing requires a gradual conditioning period (weeks to months) to build diaphragmatic endurance, analogous to PVFB

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phrenic nerve pacing requires an INTACT phrenic nerve LMN. Patients with C1-C2 SCI are the ideal candidates (injury above the phrenic motor neurons). Patients with C3-C5 LMN damage (denervated diaphragm) cannot benefit from phrenic pacing --- there is no muscle to stimulate.

## 8.7 The Abdominal Binder `[BOARD]`

### 8.7.1 Mechanism

An elastic abdominal binder wrapped around the abdomen compresses the abdominal contents, pushing them cephalad against the diaphragm. In cervical SCI patients who lack abdominal muscle tone, this serves two important functions:

1. **Improves FVC in the upright position:** By supporting the abdominal contents and pushing the diaphragm into a more domed (cephalad) starting position, the binder restores a mechanically advantageous length-tension relationship for diaphragmatic contraction. This can increase FVC by 10-15% in the upright position.

2. **Prevents abdominal visceral descent:** In the upright position, without abdominal muscle tone, the viscera fall away from the diaphragm, flattening it and reducing its excursion. The binder counteracts this gravitational effect.

### 8.7.2 The Positional Paradox

A critical clinical nuance:

- **Apply the binder when the patient is UPRIGHT** (sitting in wheelchair): Improves FVC by supporting abdominal contents and optimizing diaphragm mechanics
- **REMOVE the binder when the patient is SUPINE:** In the supine position, the binder compresses the abdominal contents against the diaphragm from below (gravity already positions the viscera cephalad), which restricts diaphragmatic descent during inspiration and actually REDUCES FVC

> **Clinical Pearl:** The abdominal binder is a respiratory aid that improves FVC when the patient is upright (by supporting the diaphragm in an optimal position) but MUST be removed when the patient is supine (where it paradoxically restricts diaphragmatic excursion and worsens ventilation).

## 8.8 Glossopharyngeal Breathing `[BOARD]`

### 8.8.1 Technique

Glossopharyngeal breathing (GPB), also known as "frog breathing," is a technique in which the patient uses the muscles of the mouth, pharynx, and larynx to "gulp" successive boluses of air into the lungs. Each gulp forces a small volume of air (60-200 mL) past the glottis and into the lungs; by performing 6-9 consecutive gulps, the patient can insufflate the lungs to near-total lung capacity.

### 8.8.2 Clinical Application

- Can provide **1-3 hours** of ventilator-free breathing for ventilator-dependent patients
- Serves as an emergency backup if the ventilator malfunctions
- Improves cough effectiveness (by achieving a larger pre-cough inspiration)
- Requires intact bulbar (oral/pharyngeal) musculature and a functioning glottis
- Cannot be performed through a cuffed tracheostomy (cuff must be deflated or tracheostomy must be capped/fenestrated)
- Requires extensive training and practice; not all patients can master the technique

## 8.9 Tracheostomy `[BOARD]`

### 8.9.1 Indications

Tracheostomy is generally considered when a patient is expected to remain ventilator-dependent for more than **2 weeks** or has failed extubation attempts.

### 8.9.2 Advantages Over Endotracheal Intubation

- Improved patient comfort and reduced sedation requirements
- Facilitates ventilator weaning (reduced dead space, reduced airway resistance)
- Improved secretion management (direct suctioning access)
- Enables speech (with speaking valve or cuff deflation)
- Allows oral nutrition
- Reduced risk of laryngeal injury compared to prolonged endotracheal intubation

### 8.9.3 Speech with Tracheostomy

**Speaking Valve (Passy-Muir Valve):** A one-way valve placed on the tracheostomy hub that allows air to enter through the tracheostomy during inspiration but redirects exhalation upward through the vocal cords, enabling phonation. Requirements: cuff must be fully deflated (or cuffless tracheostomy), adequate expiratory flow above the cuff to phonate, and intact laryngeal function.

**Cuff Deflation:** Simply deflating the tracheostomy cuff allows some air to pass around the tracheostomy tube and through the larynx during expiration, enabling speech. However, this reduces the effectiveness of positive-pressure ventilation.

## 8.10 Sleep-Disordered Breathing `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 8.10.1 Prevalence

Sleep-disordered breathing (SDB) is remarkably common in cervical SCI, with obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) reported in up to **60%** of patients with tetraplegia. This prevalence far exceeds that of the general population (estimated 2-9%).

### 8.10.2 Risk Factors Specific to SCI

- **Supine sleeping position:** Most SCI patients sleep supine (cannot easily reposition), which promotes airway collapse
- **Neck anatomy:** Cervical orthoses and surgical hardware may alter neck positioning and airway mechanics
- **Obesity:** Weight gain is common after SCI due to reduced physical activity and altered metabolism
- **Medications:** Sedatives, muscle relaxants (baclofen, diazepam), and opioids suppress respiratory drive and reduce upper airway muscle tone
- **Upper airway edema:** May occur in the acute phase from surgical positioning or fluid resuscitation
- **Reduced lung volumes:** Lower FRC in cervical SCI increases the propensity for airway closure during sleep

### 8.10.3 Types of Sleep-Disordered Breathing

**Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA):** Repetitive collapse of the upper airway during sleep despite continued respiratory effort. Characterized by snoring, witnessed apneas, and excessive daytime sleepiness.

**Central Sleep Apnea (CSA):** Cessation of respiratory effort during sleep due to impaired central respiratory drive. May occur in cervical SCI patients due to disruption of brainstem-spinal cord respiratory pathways, particularly during REM sleep when intercostal and accessory muscle tone is physiologically suppressed.

**Hypoventilation:** Chronic nocturnal hypoventilation (elevated PaCO2 during sleep) without discrete apneic events; common in patients with marginal respiratory reserve who maintain adequate ventilation while awake but hypoventilate during sleep.

### 8.10.4 Screening and Diagnosis

- **Screening questionnaires:** Epworth Sleepiness Scale, STOP-Bang Questionnaire (may have reduced sensitivity in SCI due to altered symptom presentation)
- **Polysomnography (PSG):** Gold standard for diagnosis; measures sleep architecture, airflow, respiratory effort, oxygen saturation, and body position
- **Nocturnal oximetry:** Simple screening tool; repetitive oxygen desaturations suggest SDB

### 8.10.5 Treatment

- **CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure):** First-line treatment for OSA; pneumatic splinting of the upper airway prevents collapse
- **BiPAP (Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure):** Provides separate inspiratory and expiratory pressures; useful for patients with hypoventilation or CSA who need inspiratory pressure support in addition to airway patency
- **Positional therapy:** If feasible, elevation of the head of bed or lateral positioning
- **Weight management:** Dietary counseling and exercise programs adapted for SCI
- **Medication review:** Minimize sedating medications, particularly at bedtime
- **Tracheostomy:** May be required for refractory SDB in ventilator-dependent patients

## 8.11 Long-Term Respiratory Surveillance `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 8.11.1 Routine Monitoring

- **Pulmonary function tests (PFTs):** Annual spirometry (FVC, FEV1) to track respiratory function over time
- **Oximetry:** Periodic resting and nocturnal oximetry
- **Chest imaging:** As clinically indicated (symptoms, recurrent infections)
- **Sleep studies:** Screen for SDB, particularly in cervical SCI patients with daytime sleepiness, morning headaches, or nocturnal desaturations

### 8.11.2 Preventive Strategies

- **Influenza vaccination:** Annually
- **Pneumococcal vaccination:** Per CDC guidelines
- **COVID-19 vaccination:** Per current recommendations; SCI patients are at elevated risk for severe respiratory illness
- **Smoking cessation:** Essential; smoking compounds the respiratory impairment of SCI
- **Weight management:** Obesity further restricts lung volumes and worsens SDB
- **Pulmonary rehabilitation:** Inspiratory muscle training (IMT) using threshold loading devices has been shown to improve inspiratory muscle strength, FVC, and respiratory endurance in some studies (Berlowitz et al., 2016)

### 8.11.3 Respiratory Considerations During Aging

As SCI patients age, they may experience gradual decline in respiratory function from:

- Progressive deconditioning of remaining respiratory muscles
- Chest wall stiffening (age-related reduction in compliance)
- Increasing body weight
- Superimposed pulmonary disease (COPD, asthma)
- Post-polio-like syndrome of progressive motor neuron loss

Ongoing respiratory surveillance and proactive management of modifiable risk factors are essential to maintaining respiratory health in the aging SCI population.

## 8.12 Respiratory Management by Injury Level: Quick Reference `[BOARD]`

| Injury Level | Diaphragm | Intercostals | Abdominals | FVC (% predicted) | Cough | Ventilation | Key Interventions |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| C1-C2 | Paralyzed | Paralyzed | Paralyzed | 5-10% | Absent | Ventilator dependent | Phrenic pacing (if intact LMN), GPB, full-time MV |
| C3-C4 | Weak | Paralyzed | Paralyzed | 15-30% | Absent | Usually requires MV acutely; may wean | PVFB weaning, MI-E, assisted cough, consider pacing |
| C5-C8 | Intact | Paralyzed | Paralyzed | 30-50% | Severely impaired | Usually spontaneous | Assisted cough, MI-E, abdominal binder, aggressive pulmonary toilet |
| T1-T6 | Intact | Partially intact | Paralyzed | 50-75% | Impaired | Spontaneous | Assisted cough PRN, abdominal binder if needed |
| T7-T12 | Intact | Intact | Partially intact | 75-90% | Mildly impaired | Spontaneous | Cough augmentation for upper thoracic; minimal for lower |
| L1+ | Intact | Intact | Intact | >90% | Normal | Spontaneous | No respiratory interventions typically needed |
