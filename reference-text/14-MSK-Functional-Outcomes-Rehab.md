## 14.1 Heterotopic Ossification

**[BOARD]**

### Definition and Pathophysiology

Heterotopic ossification (HO) is the formation of mature, lamellar bone within periarticular soft tissues (muscle, connective tissue) in locations where bone does not normally exist. HO occurs exclusively below the neurologic level of injury, in tissues that have lost normal suprasegmental neural control.

The pathogenesis of neurogenic HO remains incompletely understood. The prevailing theory involves aberrant differentiation of mesenchymal stem cells within soft tissues into osteoblastic lineages, driven by an interplay of local and systemic factors including:

- Local inflammatory mediators released from injured or remodeling tissues
- Altered vascular permeability and tissue edema below the injury level
- Loss of normal sympathetic neural regulation of bone metabolism
- Circulating osteogenic growth factors and bone morphogenetic proteins
- Venous stasis and microthrombi in paralyzed limbs

### Epidemiology

- **Incidence:** 16 to 53 percent of individuals with SCI, depending on diagnostic criteria, imaging modality, and study population. The wide range reflects the inclusion of both clinically significant HO and incidental radiographic findings.
- **Anatomic distribution:**
  - Hip: 70 to 80 percent of cases (by far the most common location)
  - Knee: second most common
  - Shoulder: less common
  - Elbow: least common
- HO invariably occurs below the level of injury. It is characteristically periarticular, forming in the soft tissues surrounding joints rather than within the joint itself.
- Onset is typically 1 to 4 months after injury, with a peak incidence at approximately 2 months.

### Risk Factors

- **Complete injury (AIS A):** The strongest risk factor; motor-complete injuries have significantly higher rates than incomplete injuries.
- **Spasticity:** Presence and severity of spasticity correlate with HO risk, potentially through repetitive microtrauma to periarticular tissues.
- **Pressure injuries:** Active pressure injuries, particularly in the pelvic region, are associated with increased HO incidence.
- **Urinary tract infections:** Systemic inflammation from recurrent UTIs may contribute to osteogenic stimulation.
- **Deep vein thrombosis:** DVT and HO share common risk factors (immobility, inflammation, venous stasis) and frequently coexist. The causal relationship is debated -- DVT may predispose to HO, or both may result from shared underlying pathology.
- **Aggressive passive range of motion:** Overly forceful joint mobilization may cause microtrauma that promotes HO development. This is an important clinical consideration, as well-intentioned but aggressive physiotherapy may paradoxically contribute to the problem.

### Clinical Presentation

HO typically presents with:

- **Swelling** of the affected extremity, often periarticular
- **Warmth** and erythema over the affected area
- **Decreased range of motion** as the ectopic bone restricts joint mobility
- **Low-grade fever** may occur during the active inflammatory phase

**Critical differential diagnosis:** The clinical presentation of early HO -- with swelling, warmth, erythema, and edema of an extremity -- closely mimics deep vein thrombosis (DVT). DVT must be excluded in any patient presenting with unilateral extremity swelling, as the two conditions can coexist and require different treatments.

### Diagnostic Evaluation

**Laboratory studies:**
- **Alkaline phosphatase (ALP):** Serum ALP elevation is the earliest laboratory marker of HO, reflecting increased osteoblastic activity. However, ALP is nonspecific and may be elevated from other causes (hepatobiliary disease, fracture healing, physiologic bone remodeling). Serial trending of ALP is more useful than a single measurement.

**Imaging:**
- **Triple-phase bone scan (technetium-99m MDP):** The most sensitive early diagnostic imaging study. All three phases (flow, blood pool, delayed) are positive in active HO, distinguishing it from other causes of increased tracer uptake. The bone scan becomes positive 2 to 4 weeks before plain radiographic changes are evident.
- **Plain radiographs:** Demonstrate ectopic bone formation but are a lagging indicator, becoming positive only 4 to 6 weeks after clinical onset when sufficient mineralization has occurred for radiographic visualization.
- **CT scan:** Provides superior anatomic detail for surgical planning, delineating the extent of ectopic bone, its relationship to neurovascular structures, and joint involvement.
- **MRI:** Less commonly used for HO diagnosis but may be helpful in early stages to demonstrate soft tissue edema and inflammatory changes before mineralization.

**Table 14.1: Diagnostic Studies for Heterotopic Ossification**

| Study | Timing of Positivity | Sensitivity | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Serum alkaline phosphatase | Earliest (days to weeks) | High but nonspecific | Screening, monitoring activity |
| Triple-phase bone scan | 2-4 weeks after onset | Most sensitive early test | Early diagnosis, monitoring maturity |
| Plain radiographs | 4-6 weeks after onset | Lower early sensitivity | Confirmation, extent assessment |
| CT scan | When mineralization present | Excellent anatomic detail | Surgical planning |

### Treatment

**Conservative management:**
- **Gentle range of motion** -- Maintain joint mobility with gentle, pain-free ROM exercises. Aggressive stretching or forceful manipulation should be avoided, as this may worsen HO by causing further soft tissue microtrauma and inflammatory stimulation. The balance between preserving ROM and avoiding exacerbation requires clinical judgment.
- **Etidronate disodium (EHDP)** -- A first-generation bisphosphonate that inhibits hydroxyapatite crystal formation and mineralization of the osteoid matrix. Etidronate does not prevent osteoid formation but inhibits its mineralization, potentially reducing the functional impact of HO. Typical dosing is 20 mg/kg/day for 2 weeks followed by 10 mg/kg/day for 10 weeks. Long-term use may cause osteomalacia.
- **Indomethacin** -- An NSAID that inhibits prostaglandin-mediated bone formation and is used for HO prophylaxis. Typical dosing is 75 mg/day for 6 weeks. Indomethacin has demonstrated efficacy in reducing HO incidence after hip arthroplasty and is extrapolated to the SCI setting, though direct evidence in SCI is more limited.
- **Radiation prophylaxis** -- A single dose of external beam radiation (typically 700 to 800 cGy) delivered to the affected area within 24 to 72 hours of injury or surgery can significantly reduce HO formation. Used more commonly after hip arthroplasty than in SCI, but may be considered in high-risk SCI patients.

**Surgical excision:**
- Indicated for HO that significantly restricts joint ROM, impairs function (sitting, positioning, transfers), causes skin breakdown over the bony prominence, or results in neurovascular compression.
- **Timing:** Surgery should be delayed until HO has matured, typically 12 to 18 months after onset. Premature excision of immature HO carries a high risk of recurrence.
- **Confirmation of maturity:**
  - Normalized serum alkaline phosphatase level (return to baseline)
  - Cold (inactive) bone scan showing no increased uptake in the delayed phase
  - Both criteria should ideally be met before proceeding with surgery
- **Recurrence:** Even with mature HO and appropriate timing, recurrence rates after surgical excision remain significant. Perioperative prophylaxis with indomethacin, radiation, or etidronate may reduce recurrence risk.

**Clinical Pearl 14.1:** When a patient with SCI presents with a swollen, warm extremity, always consider both HO and DVT in the differential diagnosis. The two conditions can coexist, and DVT should be excluded with duplex ultrasound before attributing symptoms solely to HO.

## 14.2 Osteoporosis After SCI

**[BOARD]**

### Pathophysiology

Sublesional osteoporosis is an inevitable consequence of SCI, resulting from the loss of mechanical loading and gravitational stress on the skeleton below the injury level. Bone loss begins within days to weeks of injury and follows a characteristic pattern:

- **Most rapid bone loss** occurs during the first 2 years after injury, with sublesional bone mineral density (BMD) declining by approximately 4 percent per month in the first year.
- **Primary sites of bone loss:** The distal femur and proximal tibia are the most severely affected sites. Trabecular (cancellous) bone is preferentially lost compared to cortical bone, reflecting the greater metabolic activity and surface area of trabecular bone.
- **Steady state:** Bone loss gradually decelerates after the first 2 years, reaching a new (lower) steady state at approximately 3 to 7 years post-injury. At this point, sublesional BMD may be reduced by 50 to 70 percent compared to pre-injury values.
- **Irreversibility:** Unlike postmenopausal or glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis, SCI-related sublesional bone loss is largely irreversible with current therapies. Standing programs, FES cycling, and pharmacologic agents (bisphosphonates, denosumab) have shown limited ability to reverse established bone loss, though they may slow further decline.

### DEXA Scanning

**[BOARD]**

Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) is the gold standard for bone density measurement. However, standard DEXA scanning protocols (which measure BMD at the lumbar spine and proximal femur) are inappropriate for the SCI population for several reasons:

- **Spine and proximal hip BMD may be falsely elevated** due to:
  - Heterotopic ossification (ectopic bone in periarticular tissues increases measured BMD)
  - Degenerative disc disease and osteophytes (common in chronic SCI)
  - Compression fractures with vertebral body sclerosis
  - Limited weight bearing on the spine (in wheelchair users, the spine is loaded more than the lower extremities but less than in ambulatory individuals)

- **Recommended DEXA sites in SCI:** The distal femur and proximal tibia are the preferred measurement sites, as these locations:
  - Reflect the true magnitude of sublesional bone loss
  - Correspond to the most common fracture sites in SCI
  - Are not confounded by HO, degenerative changes, or artifact

Specialized DEXA protocols for distal femur and proximal tibia scanning have been developed and validated for the SCI population.

### Fragility Fractures

**[BOARD]**

Fragility fractures (low-trauma fractures) are a major clinical concern in chronic SCI, occurring from minimal trauma such as transfers, passive range of motion, or minor falls from the wheelchair.

**Epidemiology:**
- Lifetime fracture incidence in SCI is estimated at 25 to 46 percent.
- Most common sites: distal femur, proximal tibia, femoral shaft.

**Clinical presentation:**
Fragility fractures in SCI present atypically due to the absence of protective sensation:

- **Swelling, warmth, and erythema** of the affected extremity -- mimicking DVT or infection (cellulitis, osteomyelitis)
- Pain may be absent (in complete injuries) or atypical
- **Autonomic dysreflexia** may be triggered by a fracture below the injury level (in injuries T6 and above)
- Deformity may be the presenting feature, particularly in shaft fractures
- Crepitus on examination

**The diagnostic challenge:** The presentation of fragility fractures (swollen, warm, erythematous extremity) overlaps significantly with DVT, cellulitis, HO, and other conditions. A high index of suspicion and appropriate imaging (radiographs of the affected area) are essential.

**Treatment:**
- **Conservative management is preferred** in the majority of SCI fragility fractures.
- **Padded splints** rather than circumferential casts -- this is a critical principle in SCI. Circumferential casting creates a closed space around insensate tissue, placing the patient at high risk for pressure injury from the cast itself. Padded splints, bivalved casts, or pillow splints allow skin inspection and pressure relief.
- Fracture healing is typically adequate despite the osteoporotic bone, though healing time may be prolonged.
- Surgical fixation may be considered for displaced fractures, fractures involving the joint surface, or fractures that cannot be adequately managed with splinting, but is generally avoided when possible due to the poor bone quality and risks of surgery.

**Clinical Pearl 14.2:** Never apply a circumferential cast to an insensate extremity in a patient with SCI. The risk of pressure injury within the cast is extremely high. Use padded splints, bivalved casts, or pillow splints that allow regular skin inspection.

## 14.3 Upper Extremity Overuse: Shoulder Pain and Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

**[BOARD]**

### Shoulder Pain

(See also Chapter 11, Section 11.3 for detailed discussion of shoulder pain mechanisms and management.)

Shoulder pain is the most common musculoskeletal complaint in the SCI population, affecting 30 to 70 percent of manual wheelchair users. The prevalence increases with duration of wheelchair use.

**Etiology:**
- **Rotator cuff pathology and impingement** -- The most common cause. MRI studies demonstrate rotator cuff abnormalities in 65 to 75 percent of individuals with chronic SCI.
- **Bicipital tendinitis** -- Inflammation of the long head of the biceps tendon from repetitive shoulder flexion during propulsion and transfers.
- **Acromioclavicular (AC) joint disease** -- Degenerative changes from repetitive loading.

**Prevention:**
The Consortium for Spinal Cord Medicine guidelines emphasize:
1. Proper wheelchair setup:
   - **Axle position:** Rear axle positioned directly below or slightly anterior to the shoulder joint when the hand is at the top-dead-center of the push rim. This position minimizes the magnitude and frequency of propulsion forces.
   - **Seat height:** Allows 100 to 120 degrees of elbow flexion when the hand rests on the top of the push rim. Appropriate seat height reduces the shoulder joint loading during propulsion.
2. **Transfer technique:** Lead with the stronger arm; use a sliding board when appropriate; minimize total daily transfers; avoid shoulder impingement during the transfer motion.
3. **Strengthening program:** Focus on rotator cuff external rotators and scapular stabilizers (lower trapezius, serratus anterior, rhomboids) to maintain muscular balance against the dominant internal rotators and pectoral muscles that are strengthened by daily wheelchair propulsion.

### Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

**[BOARD]**

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) -- compression of the median nerve within the carpal tunnel at the wrist -- is highly prevalent in manual wheelchair users, affecting 40 to 66 percent of this population compared to approximately 3 to 6 percent of the general population.

**Mechanism:** Repetitive wrist extension loading during wheelchair propulsion (the wrist is extended as the hand grips the push rim) and weight-bearing through the extended wrist during transfers creates sustained and repetitive compression of the median nerve within the carpal tunnel.

**Symptoms:**
- Numbness and paresthesias in the median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle, and radial half of ring finger) -- though these symptoms may be confounded by concurrent neuropathic pain at or below the injury level
- Weakness of thenar muscles (opponens pollicis, abductor pollicis brevis) -- functionally significant as this impacts grip and pinch strength
- Nocturnal symptoms (waking with numbness/tingling)

**Diagnosis:** Electrodiagnostic studies (nerve conduction studies and electromyography) confirm the diagnosis and grade severity. Clinical testing (Phalen test, Tinel sign) may be less reliable in patients with pre-existing sensory deficits.

**Management:** Wrist splinting (neutral position during sleep), activity modification, ergonomic wheelchair adjustments, corticosteroid injection, and surgical carpal tunnel release for refractory cases.

## 14.4 Functional Outcomes by Neurologic Level

**[BOARD]**

Predicting functional outcomes based on neurologic level of injury is a cornerstone of SCI rehabilitation medicine. While individual outcomes vary based on factors including completeness, age, body habitus, motivation, comorbidities, and available resources, the following represents expected outcomes for individuals with motor-complete (AIS A or B) injuries who receive comprehensive rehabilitation.

### C1-C4: High Tetraplegia

**Key muscle function:** No voluntary upper extremity motor function. C3-C4 may have partial diaphragm function (diaphragm innervated by C3-C5 via phrenic nerve).

**Respiratory:**
- C1-C2: Ventilator dependent (complete phrenic nerve denervation). May benefit from phrenic nerve pacing if the phrenic nerve lower motor neurons (C3-C5) are intact.
- C3: May wean from ventilator with intensive respiratory rehabilitation; many remain ventilator-dependent or require nocturnal ventilation.
- C4: Often ventilator-independent for daytime breathing but may require nocturnal ventilatory support. Reduced vital capacity and cough effectiveness.

**ADLs:** Dependent for all activities of daily living (feeding, grooming, bathing, dressing, bowel/bladder management).

**Mobility:** Power wheelchair with specialized controls:
- **Sip-and-puff control:** Pneumatic system activated by oral sipping (inhaling) or puffing (exhaling) through a straw-like device. For individuals without any voluntary extremity movement.
- **Head array:** Proximity sensors mounted behind and beside the head detect head movements to control wheelchair direction and speed.
- **Chin control:** Joystick operated by chin movement.

**Equipment needs:** Hospital bed, mechanical lift for all transfers, environmental control units (ECU) for operating electronic devices, 24-hour attendant care.

### C5: Deltoid, Biceps

**Key muscle function:** Deltoid (shoulder abduction/flexion), biceps (elbow flexion), brachioradialis (elbow flexion with forearm in neutral). NO wrist extension, NO finger function.

**ADLs:**
- **Feeding:** Modified independent to independent after setup with a universal cuff (utensil holder strapped to the hand) and plate guard. Mobile arm supports or balanced forearm orthosis (BFO) may be needed to assist weak shoulder musculature.
- **Grooming:** Moderate to maximal assistance. With adaptive equipment and setup, may achieve some grooming tasks (brushing teeth, combing hair) using universal cuff.
- **Dressing:** Dependent to maximal assistance for upper and lower body.
- **Bathing:** Dependent.
- **Bowel/bladder:** Dependent.

**Mobility:** Power wheelchair primary mode of mobility. Some individuals may propel a manual wheelchair with rim projections (projection or peg rims that allow grasping with flexed elbows) on flat, smooth surfaces for short distances, but a power wheelchair is required for functional community mobility.

**Transfers:** Dependent; mechanical lift or sliding board with maximal assistance.

### C6: Wrist Extension -- THE TENODESIS GRASP

**[BOARD]**

**Key muscle function:** Extensor carpi radialis longus and brevis (wrist extension). C6 is defined by the critical addition of active wrist extension to the C5 motor profile.

**The Tenodesis Grasp:**

The tenodesis grasp is the key functional milestone at C6 and one of the most important concepts in SCI rehabilitation. Active wrist extension produces passive finger flexion through tenodesis (tightening of the flexor tendons across the wrist and finger joints as the wrist extends). Conversely, wrist flexion produces passive finger extension.

This passive hand opening and closing, driven by active wrist extension and gravity-assisted wrist flexion, creates a functional grasp pattern that allows C6 tetraplegic individuals to pick up, hold, and release objects. The tenodesis grasp is not as strong or dexterous as active finger flexion but is sufficient for many functional tasks.

**Critical rehabilitation principle:** The flexor tendons must be maintained at appropriate tension (not overstretched) for the tenodesis grasp to function. Aggressive finger extension stretching, particularly with the wrist in extension, elongates the finger flexor tendons and weakens or eliminates the tenodesis grasp. This is one of the most common and functionally devastating therapeutic errors in C6 tetraplegia.

**ADLs:**
- **Feeding:** Modified independent to independent with adaptive equipment.
- **Grooming:** Contact guard to modified independent with setup and adaptive equipment.
- **Dressing:** Contact guard to modified independent for upper body; maximal to moderate assistance for lower body.
- **Bathing:** Moderate assistance to modified independent with equipment.
- **Bowel/bladder:** Moderate assistance; some may achieve independence with digital stimulation using adaptive equipment.
- **Transfers:** Contact guard transfers on level surfaces using a sliding board. Cannot perform depression transfers (no triceps).

**Mobility:** Independent manual wheelchair mobility with coated or friction-enhanced push rims on flat surfaces. Power wheelchair may be needed for community distances, outdoor terrain, and energy conservation.

**Independence potential:** C6 is the highest complete injury level at which independent living is possible, though this requires an extremely motivated individual, appropriate adaptive equipment, and an accessible environment. Published data suggest the following rates of independence for C6 complete SCI: feeding 16%, upper extremity dressing 13%, lower extremity dressing 3%, grooming 19%, bathing 9%, bowel management 3%, transfers 6%, wheelchair mobility 88%.

### C7: Triceps -- Elbow Extension

**[BOARD]**

**Key muscle function:** Triceps (elbow extension). The addition of elbow extension to the motor profile is transformative for functional independence.

**Why C7 = "The Usual Level for Independence":**

Elbow extension provides:
- The ability to perform depression transfers (push-up transfers) without a sliding board
- Effective wheelchair push-ups for pressure relief
- Greater reach and upper extremity functional range
- Ability to propel a manual wheelchair up inclines, ramps, and over curbs
- Enhanced ability to perform overhead activities

**ADLs:**
- **Feeding:** Independent.
- **Grooming:** Independent.
- **Dressing:** Independent (upper and lower body) with adaptive techniques.
- **Bathing:** Independent with equipment (tub bench, handheld shower).
- **Bowel/bladder:** Independent with adaptive equipment.

**Mobility:** Independent manual wheelchair mobility including curbs, ramps, and uneven terrain. Community distances achievable with manual wheelchair.

**Transfers:** Independent on level surfaces (depression transfers). May need minimal assistance for uneven surface transfers (car, bed at different height).

### C8-T1: Finger Flexion and Intrinsic Hand Muscles

**Key muscle function:** Finger flexors (C8), finger intrinsics (T1), thumb opposition and flexion. Near-normal hand function with full grasp and release.

**ADLs:** Independent in all ADLs. Hand function approaches normal, though fine motor dexterity may be mildly reduced compared to able-bodied individuals.

**Mobility:** Fully independent manual wheelchair mobility. Independent transfers in all situations.

### Paraplegia: T2-T9

**Key muscle function:** Full upper extremity function. Progressive trunk stability with lower levels (abdominal musculature innervated T7-T12; intercostals T1-T11).

**ADLs:** Independent in all ADLs, bowel and bladder management.

**Mobility:**
- Manual wheelchair is the primary mode of mobility.
- **Ambulation:** Exercise-only ambulation is possible with bilateral knee-ankle-foot orthoses (KAFOs) and a walker or forearm crutches. Ambulation at thoracic levels is not practical for community mobility due to the extreme energy cost (6 to 12 times normal energy expenditure) and slow speed.

**Trunk stability:** Improves progressively from T2 to T9 as more intercostal and abdominal musculature is innervated. Individuals with higher thoracic injuries have less trunk stability, making seated balance, transfers, and wheelchair skills more challenging than those with lower thoracic injuries.

### Paraplegia: T10-L2

**Key muscle function:** Full trunk control (abdominal muscles complete), variable hip flexor function (iliopsoas L1-L2).

**Mobility:**
- **Household ambulation** is achievable with bilateral KAFOs and forearm crutches for some individuals.
- Community ambulation may be attempted with good endurance, motivation, and appropriate orthoses, though a wheelchair remains the primary mobility device for most.
- Energy cost of ambulation remains elevated but less than at higher thoracic levels.

### Paraplegia: L3-S5

**Key muscle function:** Hip flexors, knee extensors (quadriceps, L3-L4), ankle dorsiflexors (L4-L5), ankle plantarflexors (S1-S2). Progressive lower extremity motor function with descending levels.

**Mobility:**
- **Community ambulation** is a realistic goal with appropriate orthoses (AFOs, forearm crutches, or canes) for many individuals.
- L3-L4: KAFOs or AFOs with forearm crutches for community ambulation.
- L5-S1: AFOs with canes or crutches; some may ambulate without assistive devices on level surfaces.
- S2-S5: Near-normal ambulation; may use no assistive device or a single cane for balance.

## 14.5 Orthotic Devices

**[BOARD]**

### Types of Lower Extremity Orthoses

- **Hip-Knee-Ankle-Foot Orthosis (HKAFO):** Controls the hip, knee, and ankle joints. Rarely used for functional ambulation due to extreme energy cost and impracticality. May be used for therapeutic standing.
- **Knee-Ankle-Foot Orthosis (KAFO):** Controls the knee and ankle joints. Used for individuals with L1-L3 level injuries who lack adequate quadriceps strength to maintain knee extension during stance. Bilateral KAFOs are typically required. Ambulation with bilateral KAFOs requires a four-point or swing-through gait pattern using forearm crutches or a walker.
- **Ankle-Foot Orthosis (AFO):** Controls the ankle joint. Used for individuals with L4-S1 level injuries with adequate quadriceps but weak or absent dorsiflexors and/or plantarflexors. Various designs include solid-ankle AFO (most restrictive), hinged AFO (allows dorsiflexion), and posterior leaf spring AFO (flexible, assists dorsiflexion during swing phase).
- **Reciprocating Gait Orthosis (RGO):** A specialized HKAFO with a reciprocal mechanism linking the two hip joints. When one hip extends, the mechanism assists flexion of the contralateral hip, producing a reciprocal gait pattern. Designed for individuals with thoracic-level paraplegia. More energy-efficient than standard HKAFO ambulation but still requires excessive energy for practical community use. Most commonly used for therapeutic standing and exercise ambulation.

## 14.6 Wheelchair and Seating

**[BOARD]**

### Wheelchair Types

- **Rigid-frame manual wheelchairs:** One-piece welded or folded rigid frame that does not collapse. Lighter weight, more durable, and more energy-efficient for propulsion than folding frames. Preferred for active users with paraplegia and low-level tetraplegia.
- **Folding-frame manual wheelchairs:** Cross-brace frame that folds laterally for transport. Heavier and less efficient than rigid frames but more compact for storage in a car trunk. May be appropriate for part-time users or those with transportation constraints.
- **Power wheelchairs:** Motor-driven wheelchairs controlled by joystick (standard), head array, sip-and-puff, or chin control. Essential for individuals with high-level tetraplegia (C1-C5) and beneficial for some C6 individuals for community mobility and energy conservation.

### Cushion Types

- **ROHO air-cell cushions:** Interconnected air cells that conform to body contours, equalizing pressure distribution. Require regular inflation checks. Available in low-, mid-, and high-profile versions.
- **Jay gel cushions:** Gel-based with solid foam base for stability. Provide contouring and pressure relief with a more stable seating base than air-only cushions.
- **Hybrid cushions:** Combine air, gel, and foam elements to optimize pressure redistribution, stability, and comfort.
- **Foam cushions:** Contoured or flat foam. Least expensive; less effective for pressure redistribution. Adequate for lower-risk individuals (incomplete injuries, ambulatory users).

### Standing Frames and Standing Wheelchairs

Standing frames and standing wheelchairs allow supported upright positioning for individuals who cannot bear weight independently. Benefits include psychological well-being, bone loading (though evidence for meaningful osteoporosis prevention is limited), improved bowel function, reduced spasticity, and pressure redistribution.

### Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES) Cycling

FES cycling uses surface electrical stimulation of paralyzed lower extremity muscles to produce cycling movements on a stationary ergometer. Benefits include cardiovascular conditioning, muscle hypertrophy (which may improve tissue protection over bony prominences), improved circulation, and psychological benefits. Evidence for significant improvement in BMD is mixed.

## 14.7 The Functional Independence Measure (FIM)

**[BOARD]**

### Overview

The Functional Independence Measure (FIM) is the most widely used standardized functional assessment instrument in rehabilitation medicine. It measures the level of disability and the burden of care required for an individual to perform daily activities.

### Structure

The FIM consists of 18 items organized into two domains:

**Motor domain (13 items):**
- Self-care (6 items): eating, grooming, bathing, upper body dressing, lower body dressing, toileting
- Sphincter control (2 items): bladder management, bowel management
- Transfers (3 items): bed/chair/wheelchair, toilet, tub/shower
- Locomotion (2 items): walk/wheelchair, stairs

**Cognitive domain (5 items):**
- Communication (2 items): comprehension, expression
- Social cognition (3 items): social interaction, problem solving, memory

### Scoring

Each item is scored on a 7-point ordinal scale:

**Table 14.4: FIM Scoring Scale**

| Score | Level | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 7 | Complete independence | Performs task safely, without modification, assistive device, or helper, in reasonable time |
| 6 | Modified independence | Performs task independently but requires assistive device, extra time, or safety concern |
| 5 | Supervision/setup | Requires standby assistance, cueing, coaxing, or setup of equipment |
| 4 | Minimal contact assistance | Individual performs 75% or more of the task |
| 3 | Moderate assistance | Individual performs 50-74% of the task |
| 2 | Maximal assistance | Individual performs 25-49% of the task |
| 1 | Total assistance | Individual performs less than 25% of the task |

### Score Ranges

- **Motor subscale:** 13 items x 7 points = maximum 91 points
- **Cognitive subscale:** 5 items x 7 points = maximum 35 points
- **Total FIM score:** 18 items x 7 points = maximum 126 points
- **Minimum possible score:** 18 (score of 1 on all items)

### Helper Threshold

The FIM score provides a practical indicator of the need for human assistance:

- **FIM 1-2 (Total to Maximal Assistance):** The individual requires a helper for most of the task. This represents a high burden of care.
- **FIM 3-5 (Moderate Assistance to Supervision):** The individual needs a helper present but performs a significant portion of the task independently. Intermediate burden of care.
- **FIM 6-7 (Modified Independence to Complete Independence):** The individual does not require a helper. The task is performed independently, though assistive devices or extra time may be needed (score of 6).

### Clinical Application

The FIM is administered by trained clinicians at admission to rehabilitation, at discharge, and at follow-up. The change in FIM score (admission to discharge) reflects the functional gains achieved during rehabilitation and is used for outcomes measurement, program evaluation, and prognostication.

**Limitations in SCI:**
- The FIM has a ceiling effect for individuals with lower-level injuries (paraplegia T10 and below), who often achieve maximum or near-maximum scores despite residual functional limitations.
- The Spinal Cord Independence Measure (SCIM) was developed specifically for the SCI population and may be more sensitive to functional changes, particularly for respiratory management, sphincter function, and mobility in this population.

## 14.8 Driving

**[BOARD]**

Return to driving is a critical functional goal that directly impacts independence, employment, and community participation.

### Driving by Injury Level

**Table 14.5: Driving Adaptations by Injury Level**

| Level | Vehicle Type | Adaptations |
|---|---|---|
| Paraplegia | Standard car | Hand controls (push-pull or push-right/angle type) for gas/brake; steering spinner knob; transfer into driver's seat with manual wheelchair stowed in back seat or trunk |
| Low tetraplegia (C6-C8) | Car with adaptations or adapted van | Hand controls with additional modifications; may need steering column modifications; adapted van with lowered floor, ramp, and wheelchair lockdown for driving from wheelchair |
| High tetraplegia (C4-C5) | Adapted van | Drive from power wheelchair; joystick or other adapted steering/acceleration/braking systems; voice-activated controls; high-tech driving adaptations |
| C1-C3 | Generally not feasible | Limited by ventilator dependence and severely restricted motor function; rare exceptions with highly specialized technology |

### Driver Evaluation and Training

Comprehensive driver evaluation by a certified driver rehabilitation specialist (CDRS) includes:
- Vision, cognition, and reaction time assessment
- Physical capacity evaluation (ROM, strength, coordination)
- Behind-the-wheel evaluation with appropriate adaptations
- Vehicle modification recommendations
- On-road training with prescribed equipment

## 14.9 Vocational Rehabilitation and Community Reintegration

**[BOARD]**

### Employment After SCI

Return to employment after SCI is achievable but rates remain suboptimal. Employment rates in the SCI population range from 25 to 35 percent in most studies, compared to over 70 percent in the age-matched general population. Factors associated with return to employment include:

- Pre-injury education and occupation
- Level and completeness of injury (higher levels and complete injuries associated with lower employment rates)
- Time since injury (employment rates improve over the first 5 to 10 years post-injury)
- Access to vocational rehabilitation services
- Transportation availability
- Psychological factors (depression, self-efficacy, motivation)

### Vocational Rehabilitation Services

State vocational rehabilitation (VR) agencies provide comprehensive services including:
- Vocational assessment and interest inventories
- Career counseling and job placement assistance
- Educational and training programs
- Assistive technology and workplace accommodations
- Job coaching and supported employment

### Community Reintegration

Successful community reintegration encompasses:
- **Housing:** Accessible home modifications (ramps, widened doorways, roll-in showers, lowered countertops, accessible kitchen and bathroom)
- **Transportation:** Accessible vehicle, paratransit services, public transit accessibility
- **Recreation and leisure:** Adaptive sports programs (wheelchair basketball, rugby, tennis, hand cycling, sailing, skiing), recreation therapy, community fitness programs
- **Social participation:** Peer mentoring, SCI support groups, disability advocacy organizations

### Adaptive Sports

Participation in adaptive sports provides physical, psychological, and social benefits:
- Cardiovascular conditioning and weight management
- Improved upper extremity strength and endurance
- Enhanced psychological well-being and self-efficacy
- Social networking and community connection
- Competitive opportunities up to and including the Paralympic Games

## 14.10 Aging with Spinal Cord Injury

**[BOARD]**

Individuals with SCI experience accelerated physiologic aging, with secondary complications emerging earlier and progressing faster than in the age-matched non-disabled population. Key considerations include:

### Musculoskeletal Overuse

- **Progressive shoulder pain:** Increasing prevalence and severity with duration of wheelchair use. Decades of wheelchair propulsion and transfers take a cumulative toll on the shoulder girdle.
- **Progressive wrist and hand pathology:** Carpal tunnel syndrome, wrist arthritis, and tendinopathy worsen with age and duration of use.
- **Loss of independence:** Progressive upper extremity overuse injuries may necessitate transition from manual to power wheelchair, increased attendant care, and modification of transfer techniques.

### Other Age-Related Changes

- **Increased fatigue:** Multifactorial (deconditioning, sleep disorders, pain, depression, medication side effects, physiologic aging).
- **Bowel and bladder changes:** Bowel transit may further slow with age; bladder compliance and capacity may change.
- **Cardiovascular disease:** SCI populations have increased prevalence of cardiovascular risk factors (physical inactivity, altered lipid profiles, insulin resistance). Cardiovascular disease is a leading cause of death in chronic SCI.
- **Metabolic syndrome:** Increased visceral adiposity, dyslipidemia, glucose intolerance, and hypertension.
- **Respiratory decline:** Age-related reduction in pulmonary function superimposed on SCI-related restrictive lung disease.
- **Skin changes:** Decreased skin elasticity and subcutaneous tissue with age increases pressure injury vulnerability.

### Clinical Implications

- Regular reassessment of functional status, equipment needs, and caregiver requirements
- Proactive management of overuse injuries
- Cardiovascular risk factor screening and management
- Lifelong nutritional and weight management counseling
- Mental health screening for depression and adjustment difficulties

## 14.11 Nontraumatic Spinal Cord Injury

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Nontraumatic spinal cord injury (NT-SCI) accounts for a significant and growing proportion of spinal cord disorders admitted to inpatient rehabilitation. The etiology, prognosis, and rehabilitation considerations differ in important ways from traumatic SCI.

### Spondylotic Myelopathy

**The most common cause of nontraumatic SCI admitted to inpatient rehabilitation.**

Degenerative cervical spondylosis produces myelopathy through chronic compression of the spinal cord by osteophytes, hypertrophied ligamentum flavum, disc herniation, and canal stenosis. The natural history is typically one of stepwise neurologic deterioration, though acute deterioration can occur from minor trauma superimposed on a critically stenotic canal.

**Clinical features:**
- Insidious onset of gait ataxia, bilateral hand clumsiness (myelopathy hand with loss of finger dexterity and power grip)
- UMN signs in the lower extremities (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign) with mixed UMN/LMN signs in the upper extremities at the level of compression
- Lhermitte sign (electric shock sensation radiating down the spine or into the extremities with neck flexion)
- Central cord syndrome pattern may occur with hyperextension injury in the setting of pre-existing spondylotic stenosis

**Rehabilitation considerations:** Similar to traumatic incomplete SCI, with attention to the typically slower, older patient profile. Functional prognosis is generally favorable for motor-incomplete presentations.

### Spinal Tumors

**The second most common cause of NT-SCI admitted to inpatient rehabilitation.**

Spinal tumors causing myelopathy include:
- **Extradural tumors:** Metastatic disease (breast, lung, prostate most common primary sites), primary vertebral body tumors
- **Intradural extramedullary tumors:** Meningiomas, schwannomas (nerve sheath tumors)
- **Intramedullary tumors:** Ependymomas, astrocytomas (rare)

**Clinical features:** Progressive myelopathy with pain often as the presenting symptom. Epidural metastatic disease classically presents with back pain (often worse at night or with recumbency), followed by progressive motor weakness and bowel/bladder dysfunction. MRI with contrast is the diagnostic study of choice.

**Rehabilitation considerations:** Prognosis depends on tumor type, extent of cord compression, and response to treatment. Rehabilitation goals must be integrated with oncologic management (radiation, chemotherapy, surgery). Bone fragility from metastatic disease and treatment-related osteoporosis requires particular attention to fracture prevention.

### Transverse Myelitis

An inflammatory disorder causing acute or subacute segmental demyelination and necrosis of the spinal cord. May be idiopathic, post-infectious, or associated with systemic autoimmune diseases.

**Clinical features:**
- Acute or subacute onset (hours to days) of bilateral weakness, sensory level, and bowel/bladder dysfunction
- Distinct sensory level on examination
- MRI demonstrates intramedullary T2 hyperintensity, often extending over 1 to 2 vertebral segments (short-segment in MS-associated transverse myelitis) or 3 or more segments (longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis, suggestive of NMOSD)
- CSF may show pleocytosis and elevated protein

**Rehabilitation considerations:** Recovery is variable. Approximately one-third recover well, one-third have moderate residual disability, and one-third have severe persistent deficits. Rehabilitation follows the same principles as traumatic SCI, tailored to the functional level of impairment.

### Neuromyelitis Optica Spectrum Disorder (NMOSD)

NMOSD is a severe autoimmune astrocytopathy characterized by attacks of optic neuritis and longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis (LETM). The disease is mediated by pathogenic autoantibodies against aquaporin-4 (AQP4), a water channel protein expressed on astrocyte foot processes.

**Key features:**
- **AQP4-IgG antibodies** are highly specific for NMOSD (>99% specificity)
- Longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis (3 or more vertebral segments) is characteristic, distinguishing it from MS-associated myelitis
- Attacks tend to be more severe than MS relapses, with poorer recovery
- Area postrema syndrome (intractable nausea, vomiting, hiccups) is a characteristic presentation
- Treatment is directed at preventing relapses with immunosuppressive therapy (rituximab, eculizumab, inebilizumab, satralizumab)

**Rehabilitation considerations:** NMOSD attacks often produce severe residual deficits. Rehabilitation follows SCI principles, with awareness that additional attacks may occur and that visual impairment from optic neuritis may compound functional limitations.

### Multiple Sclerosis

MS is a chronic autoimmune demyelinating disease of the central nervous system. Spinal cord involvement produces myelopathic symptoms that may require inpatient rehabilitation.

**Rehabilitation considerations:**
- Fatigue management is paramount (Uhthoff phenomenon -- heat sensitivity with worsening of symptoms)
- Cognitive impairment may affect rehabilitation participation
- Disease-modifying therapy should be continued during rehabilitation
- Spasticity management is frequently needed
- Exercise programs must be tailored to avoid overheating and excessive fatigue

### Vitamin B12 Deficiency (Subacute Combined Degeneration)

B12 deficiency causes demyelination of the posterior columns (loss of proprioception, vibration sense) and corticospinal tracts (UMN signs). The combination of posterior column and lateral column involvement gives rise to the name "subacute combined degeneration."

**Clinical features:**
- Sensory ataxia (loss of proprioception)
- Peripheral neuropathy (concurrent)
- UMN signs (corticospinal tract involvement)
- Cognitive impairment
- Macrocytic anemia (may be absent in early deficiency)
- MRI may show T2 hyperintensity in the posterior columns

**Rehabilitation considerations:** B12 replacement is the primary treatment. Neurologic recovery depends on the duration and severity of deficiency before treatment. Established myelin damage may be only partially reversible. Rehabilitation focuses on gait training, balance, and compensatory strategies for proprioceptive loss.

### Epidural Abscess

Epidural abscess is a neurosurgical emergency causing myelopathy through direct compression and vascular compromise of the spinal cord.

**Clinical features:**
- Classic triad (present in minority): fever, spinal pain, progressive neurologic deficit
- Risk factors: IV drug use, diabetes, immunosuppression, spinal procedures, bacteremia
- Most common organism: Staphylococcus aureus
- MRI with contrast is the diagnostic study of choice
- Emergent surgical drainage plus IV antibiotics is the standard treatment

**Rehabilitation considerations:** Outcome depends on the severity and duration of cord compression before decompression. Patients with complete motor deficits preoperatively have the worst prognosis for recovery.

### Radiation Myelopathy

Radiation myelopathy is a delayed complication of spinal radiation therapy, occurring months to years after treatment. It results from radiation-induced vascular damage and demyelination of the spinal cord.

**Clinical features:**
- Delayed onset (typically 6 months to several years after radiation)
- Progressive myelopathy affecting the radiation field
- Lhermitte sign may be an early manifestation (transient radiation myelopathy)
- MRI demonstrates T2 hyperintensity and possible enhancement within the radiation field

**Rehabilitation considerations:** No specific treatment exists. The myelopathy may stabilize or slowly progress. Rehabilitation follows standard SCI principles based on the functional level of impairment.

## 14.12 Life Care Planning

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Life care planning is a dynamic document that comprehensively outlines the anticipated needs and associated costs of lifetime care for an individual with SCI. It is commonly used in medicolegal contexts (personal injury litigation, workers' compensation) but is also valuable for clinical care planning.

### Components of a Life Care Plan

1. **Medical care:** Physician visits, hospitalizations, diagnostic testing, preventive care
2. **Rehabilitation services:** Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, psychology
3. **Medications:** Chronic medications, acute treatment medications, supplements
4. **Durable medical equipment:** Wheelchair (replacement every 3-5 years), cushion (replacement every 1-3 years), mattress, standing frame, adaptive equipment, orthotics
5. **Supplies:** Catheters, bowel program supplies, skin care products
6. **Attendant care:** Hours and level of daily personal care assistance needed
7. **Home modifications:** Accessible bathroom, ramp, widened doorways, environmental controls
8. **Transportation:** Vehicle modifications, accessible vehicle replacement
9. **Vocational/educational:** Vocational assessment, job training, college tuition
10. **Psychological support:** Counseling, peer support, psychiatric medication management
11. **Complications and contingencies:** Projected hospitalizations for pressure injuries, UTIs, respiratory infections, equipment breakdown

### Life Expectancy Considerations

Life expectancy after SCI is reduced compared to the general population, with the degree of reduction dependent on injury level, completeness, ventilator dependence, and age at injury. Life care plans must account for the projected lifespan using SCI-specific actuarial data (National Spinal Cord Injury Statistical Center data). Advances in medical care have improved SCI life expectancy over recent decades, but it remains reduced, particularly for high-level complete injuries and ventilator-dependent individuals.

---

## Summary

Musculoskeletal complications, functional outcomes, and rehabilitation after SCI encompass a broad spectrum of clinical considerations. Heterotopic ossification occurs in 16 to 53 percent of individuals, predominantly at the hip, and surgical excision should be delayed until maturity is confirmed by normalization of alkaline phosphatase and a cold bone scan. Sublesional osteoporosis is universal, with DEXA scanning appropriately performed at the distal femur and proximal tibia rather than standard spine/hip sites. Fragility fractures present atypically and should be managed with padded splints rather than circumferential casts. Functional outcomes are predictable by neurologic level, with C6 representing the highest level of potential independent living (via tenodesis grasp), C7 being the usual level for independence (with triceps enabling depression transfers), and energy cost of ambulation increasing dramatically at thoracic levels. The FIM provides a standardized 18-item assessment scored on a 7-point scale across motor and cognitive domains. Nontraumatic SCI, led by spondylotic myelopathy and spinal tumors, requires disease-specific rehabilitation approaches integrated with the underlying medical or oncologic management.
