## 11.1 Introduction and Epidemiology

**[BOARD]**

Pain is among the most prevalent, persistent, and functionally disabling secondary complications of spinal cord injury (SCI). Unlike many other sequelae of SCI that can be managed with structured rehabilitation protocols, pain frequently proves refractory to intervention and exerts a profound negative effect on quality of life, mood, sleep, and functional participation.

### Prevalence and Severity

Epidemiologic studies consistently demonstrate that 65 to 80 percent of individuals with SCI report chronic pain, defined as pain persisting beyond three months from injury. Among those with chronic pain, approximately 30 to 40 percent rate their pain as severe (numerical rating scale 7 or greater out of 10). Pain intensity does not reliably correlate with injury level or completeness, though specific pain subtypes show differential associations with neurologic characteristics. Longitudinal cohort data suggest that SCI-related pain tends to persist or worsen over time rather than spontaneously resolve, with the majority of individuals still reporting significant pain five or more years after injury.

The economic burden of SCI-related pain is substantial, encompassing direct healthcare costs (medications, procedures, clinic visits) and indirect costs (lost productivity, caregiver burden, reduced community participation). Pain is consistently cited as one of the top factors limiting return to employment and social reintegration.

### Impact on Function and Quality of Life

Chronic pain after SCI is independently associated with depression, anxiety, sleep disturbance, fatigue, and reduced participation in rehabilitation activities. Pain interference -- the degree to which pain disrupts daily activities, relationships, and enjoyment of life -- is often a more clinically meaningful outcome measure than pain intensity alone. Even moderate pain, when persistent, can erode functional gains achieved during acute rehabilitation and contribute to a downward spiral of deconditioning, social withdrawal, and psychological distress.

## 11.2 The ISCIP Classification System

**[BOARD]**

The International Spinal Cord Injury Pain (ISCIP) classification was developed through an international expert consensus process involving the International Association for the Study of Pain (IASP), the International Spinal Cord Society (ISCoS), and the American Spinal Injury Association (ASIA). Published in 2012, this taxonomy provides a standardized framework for classifying pain after SCI across three hierarchical tiers.

### Tier 1: Pain Type

Tier 1 divides all pain into three broad categories:

1. **Nociceptive pain** -- Pain arising from activation of peripheral nociceptors in response to actual or threatened tissue damage, with a normally functioning somatosensory nervous system.
2. **Neuropathic pain** -- Pain caused by a lesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system (central or peripheral).
3. **Other pain** -- Pain that cannot be classified as nociceptive or neuropathic, or pain with unknown etiology.

### Tier 2: Pain Subtype

Tier 2 further categorizes pain into clinically relevant subtypes:

**Nociceptive subtypes:**
- Musculoskeletal pain
- Visceral pain
- Other nociceptive pain

**Neuropathic subtypes:**
- At-level neuropathic pain (SCI-related)
- Below-level neuropathic pain (SCI-related)
- Other neuropathic pain (not SCI-related, e.g., peripheral neuropathy, carpal tunnel syndrome)

### Tier 3: Primary Pain Source

Tier 3 specifies the primary anatomic source or pathology responsible for the pain (e.g., rotator cuff tear for musculoskeletal pain; constipation for visceral pain; central sensitization for below-level neuropathic pain). This tier requires the greatest degree of clinical evaluation and diagnostic testing.

**Table 11.1: ISCIP Classification Summary**

| Tier 1 (Pain Type) | Tier 2 (Pain Subtype) | Tier 3 Examples (Primary Source) |
|---|---|---|
| Nociceptive | Musculoskeletal | Shoulder impingement, fracture, spasticity-related, heterotopic ossification |
| Nociceptive | Visceral | Constipation, urinary tract infection, renal calculi, cholecystitis |
| Nociceptive | Other nociceptive | Pressure injury, autonomic dysreflexia headache |
| Neuropathic | At-level SCI | Nerve root compression, syringomyelia, spinal cord damage at NLI |
| Neuropathic | Below-level SCI | Central neuropathic pain, spinal cord damage below NLI |
| Neuropathic | Other neuropathic | Carpal tunnel syndrome, diabetic peripheral neuropathy |
| Other | Unknown | Pain not fitting nociceptive or neuropathic criteria |

### Validation and Clinical Utility

Validation studies have demonstrated moderate inter-rater reliability for the ISCIP classification, with clinicians achieving approximately 79 percent accuracy for identifying nociceptive pain and 77 percent for neuropathic pain at Tier 1. Musculoskeletal pain was the most reliably classified subtype at Tier 2 (84 percent accuracy). The classification has facilitated standardized communication across clinical and research settings, although clinicians should recognize that individual patients frequently experience multiple simultaneous pain types, requiring separate classification of each distinct pain problem.

## 11.3 Nociceptive Musculoskeletal Pain

**[BOARD]**

Musculoskeletal pain is the most common pain type after SCI, arising from overuse, mechanical stress, degenerative changes, and altered biomechanics imposed by the injury and subsequent reliance on the upper extremities for mobility, transfers, and pressure relief.

### Shoulder Pain

Shoulder pain is the single most prevalent musculoskeletal complaint in persons with SCI, affecting 30 to 70 percent of manual wheelchair users depending on the population studied, chronicity of injury, and assessment methods. The prevalence increases with duration of wheelchair use, and by 20 years post-injury, up to 72 percent of individuals report shoulder pain.

**Etiology and Pathomechanics:**

The upper extremity was not designed for weight-bearing locomotion. In paraplegia and low-level tetraplegia, the shoulder joint complex assumes a dramatically altered functional role, serving as the primary engine for wheelchair propulsion, transfers, pressure relief maneuvers, and overhead reaching. Active manual wheelchair users complete an estimated 2,700 push-rim rotations per day, subjecting the shoulder to repetitive loading that far exceeds physiologic norms.

The principal diagnostic entities contributing to shoulder pain in SCI include:

- **Rotator cuff pathology (impingement, tendinopathy, and tears)** -- This is the most common cause of shoulder pain in SCI. Supraspinatus tendinopathy and subacromial impingement predominate, with MRI studies demonstrating rotator cuff abnormalities in 65 to 75 percent of individuals with chronic SCI. The combination of repetitive overhead activity, muscle imbalance (strong internal rotators, weak external rotators), and altered scapular kinematics creates a biomechanical environment favoring impingement.
- **Bicipital tendinitis** -- Inflammation of the long head of the biceps tendon, often coexisting with rotator cuff pathology.
- **Acromioclavicular (AC) joint degenerative disease** -- Resulting from repetitive loading through the AC joint during transfers and wheelchair propulsion.
- **Glenohumeral instability** -- Particularly in individuals with muscle imbalance around the shoulder girdle.

**Prevention Strategies:**

The Consortium for Spinal Cord Medicine (Paralyzed Veterans of America) published clinical practice guidelines emphasizing the following preventive measures:

1. **Wheelchair fitting optimization** -- Proper axle position (slightly anterior to the shoulder), seat height adjustment (elbow flexion of 100 to 120 degrees when the hand is at the top of the push rim), and appropriate camber to minimize propulsion effort and shoulder joint loading.
2. **Propulsion technique** -- Use of long, smooth semicircular strokes that allow the hand to drift below the push rim during recovery, rather than short choppy strokes. Minimizing the total number of strokes per day is a key goal.
3. **Transfer technique** -- Leading with the stronger arm, using a transfer board when appropriate, minimizing the total number of transfers per day, and maintaining proper body mechanics.
4. **Strengthening programs** -- Targeted strengthening of the rotator cuff (especially external rotators) and scapular stabilizers (lower trapezius, serratus anterior, rhomboids) to restore muscular balance. Shoulder stretching programs to maintain flexibility of the anterior shoulder girdle.
5. **Activity modification** -- Reducing overall repetitive upper extremity demand through use of power-assist wheels, power wheelchairs for community distances, and ergonomic adaptations.

**Clinical Pearl 11.1:** In patients with SCI presenting with new shoulder pain, always consider heterotopic ossification, fracture (especially in osteoporotic bone), and referred visceral pain in the differential diagnosis alongside mechanical overuse etiologies.

### Other Musculoskeletal Pain Sources

- **Cervical and thoracic spine pain** -- Degenerative disc disease and spondylosis proximal to the injury level, exacerbated by altered posture and compensatory movement patterns.
- **Wrist and hand pain** -- Including carpal tunnel syndrome, which affects 40 to 66 percent of manual wheelchair users due to repetitive wrist extension loading during propulsion and weight-bearing during transfers. (See Chapter 14 for detailed discussion.)
- **Spasticity-related pain** -- Muscle spasms, contractures, and spasticity-induced joint deformity can produce significant nociceptive pain, though spasticity may also trigger neuropathic pain through central mechanisms.
- **Overuse syndromes** -- Lateral and medial epicondylitis, de Quervain tenosynovitis, and other repetitive strain injuries of the upper extremity.

## 11.4 Nociceptive Visceral Pain

**[BOARD]**

Visceral pain arises from internal organ pathology below the level of injury. Visceral afferents travel with autonomic fibers, and disruption of sensory pathways by SCI often results in atypical or absent pain perception from abdominal and pelvic viscera.

### Common Sources

1. **Constipation** -- Perhaps the most common source of visceral discomfort in SCI. Neurogenic bowel dysfunction leads to prolonged transit time, fecal impaction, and colonic distention that may cause vague abdominal discomfort or, in injuries at T6 and above, autonomic dysreflexia (AD) as the sole clinical manifestation.

2. **Urinary tract infections (UTIs)** -- Neurogenic bladder predisposes to recurrent UTIs, which in individuals with impaired sensation may present with increased spasticity, malaise, autonomic dysreflexia, or cloudy/malodorous urine rather than classic dysuria and suprapubic pain.

3. **Renal and ureteral calculi** -- Higher incidence in SCI due to hypercalciuria (especially in the first year), urinary stasis, and recurrent infections with urease-producing organisms. May present with flank pain (if sensation is preserved), hematuria, AD, or increased spasticity.

4. **Acute abdomen** -- Appendicitis, cholecystitis, bowel obstruction, and perforated viscus may present atypically. In high-level injuries, AD may be the only sign of acute abdominal pathology. Delayed diagnosis contributes to increased morbidity and mortality from intra-abdominal emergencies in SCI.

**Clinical Pearl 11.2:** In any individual with SCI at T6 or above who presents with unexplained autonomic dysreflexia, visceral pathology (including acute abdomen) must be considered and systematically excluded. AD may be the sole herald of a surgical emergency.

### Evaluation

Assessment of visceral pain in SCI requires a high index of suspicion and systematic evaluation, as conventional pain-based symptom assessment is unreliable. Serial abdominal examinations may be insensitive. Imaging studies (CT abdomen/pelvis, renal ultrasound), laboratory studies (CBC, metabolic panel, urinalysis), and a careful review of bowel and bladder function are essential components of the evaluation.

## 11.5 Neuropathic Pain: At-Level

**[BOARD]**

At-level neuropathic pain occurs within the zone of injury, defined as pain perceived within three dermatomes above and/or below the neurologic level of injury (NLI). This pain type reflects damage to neural structures at or near the lesion epicenter, including nerve roots, dorsal horn neurons, and ascending sensory tracts.

### Clinical Features

At-level neuropathic pain characteristically presents with:

- **Quality:** Burning, shooting, electric shock-like, stabbing, or lancinating character.
- **Distribution:** Dermatomal or band-like pattern corresponding to the zone of injury (NLI plus or minus three dermatomes).
- **Temporal pattern:** Often constant with superimposed paroxysms. May be spontaneous or evoked.
- **Associated phenomena:** Allodynia (pain from normally non-painful stimuli such as light touch or clothing contact), hyperalgesia (exaggerated pain response to mildly painful stimuli), and dysesthesia (unpleasant abnormal sensation).

### Pathophysiology

At-level neuropathic pain involves both peripheral and central mechanisms:

- **Peripheral nerve root injury** -- Direct damage to dorsal root ganglion neurons and their central projections at the level of vertebral injury, producing ectopic discharge and peripheral sensitization.
- **Dorsal horn hyperexcitability** -- Injury-induced changes in dorsal horn neuronal excitability, including upregulation of voltage-gated sodium channels, loss of inhibitory interneurons, and altered neurotransmitter release.
- **Transitional zone changes** -- The border zone between intact and damaged spinal cord tissue represents a region of particular vulnerability to aberrant neuronal reorganization and pain generation.

### Differential Diagnosis

At-level neuropathic pain must be distinguished from:

- Radiculopathy from foraminal stenosis or hardware-related nerve root compression
- Post-surgical neuropathic pain at the incision site
- Syringomyelia (which may produce or exacerbate at-level pain as the syrinx expands)
- Complex regional pain syndrome (rare but reported after SCI)

## 11.6 Neuropathic Pain: Below-Level

**[BOARD]**

Below-level neuropathic pain is a form of central neuropathic pain arising from damage to the spinal cord itself, perceived in regions corresponding to dermatomes more than three levels below the NLI. This is the most challenging and refractory pain type after SCI.

### Epidemiology

Below-level neuropathic pain affects approximately 40 to 50 percent of individuals with SCI. It is more common in complete injuries than incomplete injuries, though it occurs in both. Onset may be immediate or delayed by months to years after injury. Once established, below-level neuropathic pain tends to be persistent and is the pain type most strongly associated with decreased quality of life and functional impairment.

### Clinical Features

- **Quality:** Burning, tingling, pins-and-needles, squeezing, freezing, or aching character. Often described as constant background pain with intermittent sharp or shooting exacerbations.
- **Distribution:** Diffuse, poorly localized, perceived in regions below the NLI that lack normal sensation. The pain may involve the entire body below the injury level or be more prominent in specific regions (legs, feet, perineum).
- **Paradox of pain in anesthetic regions:** A hallmark of central neuropathic pain is the perception of pain in body regions with absent or severely impaired sensation to external stimuli -- pain existing in the context of numbness.

### Pathophysiology

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

The pathogenesis of below-level central neuropathic pain involves multiple interacting mechanisms of central sensitization:

1. **Deafferentation hyperexcitability** -- Loss of normal sensory input to supraspinal structures leads to spontaneous hyperexcitability of thalamic and cortical neurons that previously processed sensory information from the now-denervated body regions.

2. **Spinothalamic tract damage** -- Injury to the lateral spinothalamic tract disrupts normal pain and temperature signaling while paradoxically promoting abnormal pain generation through disinhibition and reorganization of thalamic relay neurons.

3. **Maladaptive cortical reorganization** -- Functional neuroimaging studies demonstrate reorganization of the primary somatosensory cortex (S1) following SCI, with expansion of cortical representations for intact body regions into deafferented cortical territory. The degree of cortical reorganization correlates with neuropathic pain severity.

4. **Glial activation and neuroinflammation** -- Activated microglia and astrocytes in the spinal cord and brain release proinflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6), chemokines, and glutamate that sustain central sensitization. Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) released by activated microglia shifts the chloride reversal potential in dorsal horn neurons, converting inhibitory GABAergic signaling to excitatory signaling.

5. **Loss of descending inhibition** -- Disruption of descending serotonergic and noradrenergic inhibitory pathways from the brainstem (periaqueductal gray, rostral ventromedial medulla, locus coeruleus) removes tonic inhibition of spinal nociceptive processing.

6. **Thalamic changes** -- The ventroposterolateral (VPL) nucleus shows neuronal hyperexcitability, bursting discharge patterns, and expanded receptive fields following SCI, contributing to central pain generation.

7. **Ion channel alterations** -- Upregulation of voltage-gated sodium channel subtypes (Nav1.3) and calcium channel subtypes (Cav3.2 T-type) in dorsal horn neurons and thalamic relay neurons promotes hyperexcitability and spontaneous discharge.

## 11.7 Pharmacologic Management of Neuropathic Pain

**[BOARD]**

Treatment of neuropathic pain after SCI follows a stepped approach guided by the best available evidence, though high-quality randomized controlled trial data remain limited in this population. International consensus guidelines generally recommend the following hierarchy.

### First-Line Agents: Gabapentinoids

**Gabapentin** and **pregabalin** are alpha-2-delta calcium channel ligands that reduce neurotransmitter release from hyperexcitable presynaptic terminals. They are the most extensively studied medications for SCI neuropathic pain.

- **Pregabalin:** 75 to 600 mg per day in two to three divided doses. Pregabalin is the only medication with FDA approval specifically for neuropathic pain associated with SCI, based on results from multicenter randomized controlled trials demonstrating statistically significant pain reduction compared to placebo. A systematic review and meta-analysis found a standardized mean difference of -0.40 (95% CI: -0.78 to -0.01) in favor of pregabalin versus placebo for SCI neuropathic pain.
- **Gabapentin:** 300 to 3,600 mg per day in three divided doses. Titrate gradually (start 300 mg at bedtime, increase by 300 mg every 3 to 7 days) to minimize sedation and dizziness. Multiple trials demonstrate efficacy for SCI neuropathic pain, with no statistically significant difference from pregabalin in head-to-head network meta-analysis.

**Adverse effects:** Somnolence, dizziness, peripheral edema, weight gain, cognitive slowing. Dose adjustment required in renal impairment.

**Table 11.2: Pharmacologic Algorithm for SCI Neuropathic Pain**

| Line | Agent | Dose Range | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| First | Pregabalin | 75-600 mg/day | FDA-approved for SCI neuropathic pain; start 75 mg BID, titrate weekly |
| First | Gabapentin | 300-3,600 mg/day | Start 300 mg QHS, titrate by 300 mg q3-7 days; renal dose adjustment |
| Second | Amitriptyline | 25-150 mg/day | Anticholinergic effects; avoid in cardiac disease; monitor for urinary retention |
| Second | Nortriptyline | 25-150 mg/day | Fewer anticholinergic effects than amitriptyline; better tolerated |
| Second | Duloxetine | 30-120 mg/day | SNRI; dual pain and mood benefits; hepatic metabolism |
| Third | Tramadol | 50-400 mg/day | Weak opioid + SNRI properties; seizure risk; serotonin syndrome risk with SSRIs/SNRIs |
| Adjunctive | Topical lidocaine | 5% patch/cream | For localized at-level pain; minimal systemic absorption |
| Adjunctive | Capsaicin | 8% high-concentration patch | For localized pain; requires clinic application |
| Last resort | Opioids | Varies | Avoid chronic use; risk of tolerance, dependence, hyperalgesia, constipation |

### Second-Line Agents

**Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs):**
- **Amitriptyline** (25 to 150 mg per day) and **nortriptyline** (25 to 150 mg per day) inhibit reuptake of serotonin and norepinephrine, enhancing descending inhibitory pain modulation. Nortriptyline is generally preferred due to a more favorable anticholinergic side effect profile.
- **Cautions in SCI:** TCAs may exacerbate neurogenic bladder dysfunction (urinary retention from anticholinergic effects), constipation (worsening neurogenic bowel), orthostatic hypotension, and sedation. Cardiac conduction abnormalities require screening with baseline ECG. These medications are relatively contraindicated in patients with pre-existing cardiac conduction disease.

**Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs):**
- **Duloxetine** (30 to 120 mg per day) has demonstrated efficacy for various neuropathic pain conditions, though direct evidence in SCI populations is more limited. The dual mechanism of action may provide concurrent benefits for comorbid depression.
- **Venlafaxine** (75 to 225 mg per day extended release) is an alternative SNRI with pain-modulating properties at higher doses where noradrenergic effects predominate.

### Third-Line and Adjunctive Agents

**Tramadol** (50 to 400 mg per day) combines weak mu-opioid receptor agonism with serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibition. Evidence from randomized controlled trials in SCI supports short-term efficacy for neuropathic pain. However, tramadol carries risks of seizure (especially in combination with other serotonergic medications), respiratory depression, constipation, and dependence.

### Avoidance of Chronic Opioids

Strong opioids (morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, hydromorphone) should be avoided for chronic neuropathic pain management after SCI. Evidence for long-term efficacy is lacking, and the SCI population faces heightened risks from chronic opioid therapy:

- **Constipation** exacerbating neurogenic bowel dysfunction
- **Respiratory depression** in patients with compromised respiratory mechanics (cervical and high thoracic injuries)
- **Tolerance and opioid-induced hyperalgesia** potentially worsening central sensitization
- **Sedation** interfering with rehabilitation participation and cognitive function
- **Substance use disorders** -- prevalence of pre-injury substance use disorder is elevated in the SCI population
- **Endocrine effects** including hypogonadism and osteoporosis in a population already at risk

**Clinical Pearl 11.3:** Pregabalin remains the only FDA-approved medication specifically indicated for SCI neuropathic pain. In clinical practice, gabapentin is equally effective and often preferred as the initial agent due to lower cost, with pregabalin reserved for patients who do not tolerate gabapentin or prefer twice-daily dosing.

## 11.8 Non-Pharmacologic Management of Pain

**[BOARD]**

Non-pharmacologic interventions form an essential component of multimodal pain management after SCI. These approaches may be used as primary treatments for mild to moderate pain or as adjuncts to pharmacotherapy for more severe pain.

### Physical and Rehabilitation Approaches

- **Exercise and physical activity** -- Aerobic exercise, aquatic therapy, and structured resistance training programs have demonstrated modest but consistent benefits for pain reduction in SCI. Exercise may modulate pain through endogenous opioid release, improved mood, and functional conditioning.
- **Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS)** -- Modest evidence supports TENS for localized nociceptive and at-level neuropathic pain. Electrode placement in regions with preserved sensation is necessary for efficacy.
- **Acupuncture** -- Limited evidence in SCI, with some studies suggesting benefit for musculoskeletal and at-level neuropathic pain. Quality of available trials is generally low.
- **Massage therapy** -- May provide short-term relief of musculoskeletal pain and spasticity-related discomfort.

### Psychological Interventions

- **Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)** -- The most evidence-supported psychological intervention for chronic pain in SCI. CBT addresses maladaptive pain cognitions (catastrophizing, fear-avoidance), promotes self-management skills, and has demonstrated efficacy for reducing pain interference and improving mood.
- **Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)** -- An emerging approach focusing on psychological flexibility and valued-based living despite pain. Preliminary evidence supports ACT for chronic pain after SCI.
- **Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR)** -- Mindfulness meditation practices may reduce pain catastrophizing and improve pain coping in SCI, though large-scale trials are lacking.

### Interventional Procedures

- **Nerve blocks and epidural injections** -- Subacromial corticosteroid injections for shoulder impingement, trigger point injections for myofascial pain, and epidural steroid injections for radicular at-level pain may provide temporary relief.
- **Botulinum toxin injections** -- For spasticity-related pain, focal injection of botulinum toxin type A can reduce painful muscle spasms and improve range of motion.
- **Intrathecal drug delivery** -- Intrathecal baclofen pumps primarily manage spasticity but may concurrently reduce spasticity-associated pain. Intrathecal morphine or ziconotide (a voltage-gated calcium channel blocker) may be considered for severe, refractory pain.

## 11.9 Emerging and Advanced Therapies

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

### Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS)

Transcranial direct current stimulation delivers low-amplitude direct current (1 to 2 mA) through scalp electrodes to modulate cortical excitability. Anodal stimulation of the primary motor cortex (M1) has shown the most consistent analgesic effects for central neuropathic pain, presumably through activation of descending inhibitory circuits projecting to thalamic and brainstem structures.

Multiple randomized sham-controlled trials in SCI populations have demonstrated statistically significant pain reduction with repeated sessions of M1 anodal tDCS, though effect sizes are generally modest and the optimal stimulation parameters (intensity, duration, number of sessions, maintenance protocols) remain to be defined. A key advantage of tDCS is its favorable safety profile and potential for home-based self-administration after appropriate training, which may facilitate long-term maintenance therapy.

### Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS)

Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation uses focused electromagnetic pulses to modulate cortical excitability. High-frequency rTMS (5 to 20 Hz) applied to the M1 cortex produces analgesic effects through mechanisms believed to overlap with those of tDCS, including activation of corticothalamic and corticobrainstem descending modulatory pathways.

Evidence from randomized controlled trials in SCI neuropathic pain supports short-term analgesic efficacy of M1-targeted rTMS, with some studies demonstrating pain reduction persisting for weeks after a course of treatment. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) has also been investigated as a stimulation target, with potential benefits for the affective-emotional dimension of pain and comorbid depression.

Practical limitations of rTMS include the need for specialized equipment and trained operators, the requirement for repeated sessions, and variable individual response. Theta burst stimulation (TBS), a more time-efficient rTMS protocol, is under investigation as an alternative approach.

### Virtual Reality (VR) Therapy

Immersive virtual reality environments offer a novel approach to pain modulation in SCI by exploiting the brain's capacity for multisensory integration and body schema representation. VR-based interventions may include:

- **Visual walking illusions** -- Providing visual feedback of walking or body movement in a virtual environment, potentially modulating maladaptive cortical reorganization associated with central neuropathic pain.
- **Distraction-based VR** -- Engaging patients in immersive virtual environments to redirect attentional resources away from pain processing.
- **VR-enhanced motor imagery** -- Combining motor imagery with visual feedback in virtual reality to promote cortical reorganization.

Preliminary studies suggest that VR interventions may produce short-term reductions in neuropathic pain intensity, though the evidence base remains early-stage and optimal VR protocols for SCI pain have not been established.

### Dorsal Root Entry Zone (DREZ) Lesioning

DREZ lesioning is a neurosurgical procedure involving selective destruction of the dorsal horn (Rexed laminae I through V) and Lissauer tract at the level of injury. The goal is to ablate hyperexcitable second-order nociceptive neurons and disrupt aberrant pain signaling at the zone of injury.

DREZ lesioning has demonstrated the greatest efficacy for segmental at-level neuropathic pain with a dermatomal distribution, with reported success rates of 60 to 80 percent for significant pain reduction in appropriately selected patients. It is considerably less effective for diffuse below-level central neuropathic pain. Patient selection is critical, and the procedure carries risks of additional neurologic deficit, particularly motor weakness if the ventral horn is inadvertently involved.

### Spinal Cord Stimulation (SCS)

Conventional spinal cord stimulation, involving epidural placement of stimulating electrodes over the dorsal columns, has historically shown disappointing results for central neuropathic pain after SCI, with response rates substantially lower than those achieved for peripheral neuropathic conditions such as failed back surgery syndrome.

However, emerging evidence suggests that newer stimulation paradigms may offer improved outcomes:

- **High-frequency (10 kHz) stimulation** -- Paresthesia-free stimulation that may engage different analgesic mechanisms than conventional tonic stimulation.
- **Burst stimulation** -- Patterned stimulation designed to modulate both the lateral (sensory-discriminative) and medial (affective-motivational) pain pathways.
- **Dorsal root ganglion (DRG) stimulation** -- Targeted stimulation of the DRG at the affected level, which may be particularly effective for at-level neuropathic pain with a segmental distribution.

These newer modalities remain under active investigation in SCI populations, and evidence is currently limited to case series and small trials.

## 11.10 Special Considerations in Pain Assessment

**[BOARD]**

### Pain Assessment in Individuals with Cognitive or Communication Impairments

Concurrent traumatic brain injury (present in up to 60 percent of traumatic SCI cases) may impair the ability to self-report pain using standard instruments. In such cases, behavioral pain assessment tools and proxy reporting may be necessary, though their validity in the SCI-TBI dual diagnosis population is not well established.

### Distinguishing Pain from Spasticity

Pain and spasticity frequently coexist and interact after SCI. Distinguishing between painful spasticity (nociceptive pain generated by forceful muscle contraction) and neuropathic pain exacerbated by spasticity requires careful clinical assessment. Treatment of underlying spasticity may reduce associated pain, while neuropathic pain typically persists despite spasticity management.

### Pain and Autonomic Dysreflexia

In individuals with injuries at T6 and above, noxious stimuli below the injury level -- including pain-generating pathology -- may trigger autonomic dysreflexia rather than conscious pain perception. Conversely, autonomic dysreflexia itself may produce headache, which is a form of nociceptive pain. Clinicians must recognize the bidirectional relationship between pain and AD and address both systematically.

### Multidimensional Pain Assessment

Comprehensive pain assessment in SCI should include:

- **Pain intensity** -- Numerical rating scale (NRS 0-10), visual analog scale (VAS)
- **Pain quality** -- Neuropathic Pain Symptom Inventory (NPSI), Douleur Neuropathique 4 (DN4), or Leeds Assessment of Neuropathic Symptoms and Signs (LANSS) to characterize neuropathic features
- **Pain interference** -- Brief Pain Inventory (BPI) interference subscale, Multidimensional Pain Inventory (MPI)
- **Psychological comorbidity** -- Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) for depression, Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) for anxiety
- **Sleep quality** -- Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) or Insomnia Severity Index (ISI)

| Feature | Musculoskeletal | Visceral | At-Level Neuropathic | Below-Level Neuropathic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quality | Aching, dull, movement-related | Cramping, colicky, dull | Burning, shooting, electric | Burning, tingling, diffuse |
| Location | Specific joint/muscle | Abdominal/pelvic | Within 3 dermatomes of NLI | Diffuse below NLI |
| Provocation | Movement, loading | Visceral distention, eating | Touch (allodynia), position | Often spontaneous |
| Temporal | Intermittent, activity-related | Variable, related to function | Constant with paroxysms | Constant |
| Response to analgesics | NSAID, acetaminophen helpful | Treat underlying cause | Gabapentinoids, TCAs | Often refractory |
| Physical examination | Tenderness, reduced ROM | Distention, guarding (if sensation preserved) | Allodynia, hyperalgesia at NLI | Sensory loss with pain |

## 11.11 Multimodal Pain Management Framework

**[BOARD]**

Effective pain management after SCI requires an integrated multimodal approach that addresses the biological, psychological, and social dimensions of the pain experience.

### Principles of Multimodal Management

1. **Accurate classification first** -- Use the ISCIP taxonomy to classify each distinct pain problem, as treatment strategies differ fundamentally between pain types.
2. **Treat reversible causes** -- Before initiating symptomatic pain therapy, identify and treat nociceptive pain sources (shoulder impingement, UTI, constipation, pressure injury, hardware failure).
3. **Combine modalities** -- Utilize pharmacologic, physical, psychological, and interventional approaches in combination rather than sequentially exhausting single modalities.
4. **Set realistic expectations** -- Complete pain elimination is rarely achievable for central neuropathic pain. Realistic goals focus on reducing pain intensity by 30 to 50 percent, improving function, enhancing sleep quality, and reducing pain interference.
5. **Monitor outcomes** -- Track not only pain intensity but also functional outcomes, mood, sleep, and adverse effects using validated patient-reported outcome measures.
6. **Reassess periodically** -- Pain presentations change over time. New pain complaints warrant fresh evaluation for treatable nociceptive causes rather than automatic escalation of neuropathic pain medications.

### Interdisciplinary Team Approach

Optimal management involves coordinated care from physiatrists, pain medicine specialists, psychologists, physical therapists, occupational therapists, rehabilitation nurses, and social workers. Interdisciplinary pain rehabilitation programs modeled on those used in the general chronic pain population have demonstrated efficacy for improving function and reducing pain-related disability in SCI.

**Clinical Pearl 11.4:** When evaluating a patient with SCI and chronic pain, always maintain a high index of suspicion for new nociceptive pain generators (syringomyelia, hardware failure, heterotopic ossification, occult fracture, visceral pathology) before attributing worsening pain to progression of neuropathic pain.

---

## 11.12 Pain and Substance Use Considerations

**[BOARD]**

The prevalence of pre-injury substance use disorders is elevated in the SCI population, as alcohol and drug intoxication are contributing factors in a significant proportion of traumatic SCIs. This history creates particular challenges for pain management:

- Pre-existing opioid use disorder complicates the use of opioid-containing analgesics
- Alcohol use disorder may interact with gabapentinoids and other CNS-depressant medications
- Cannabis use is increasingly common for pain self-management in SCI, though evidence for efficacy in SCI neuropathic pain is limited and complicated by legal variability across jurisdictions
- Risk stratification using validated tools (Opioid Risk Tool, SOAPP-R) is recommended before initiating any opioid therapy

Behavioral health integration within the rehabilitation team is essential for addressing the complex intersection of chronic pain, substance use, depression, and functional limitation in this population.

---

## Summary

Pain after spinal cord injury is a highly prevalent, multifactorial, and often treatment-resistant condition that demands systematic classification, multimodal management, and ongoing reassessment. The ISCIP classification provides a standardized framework for categorizing pain across three tiers, distinguishing nociceptive from neuropathic mechanisms and guiding targeted treatment. Gabapentinoids represent the first-line pharmacotherapy for neuropathic pain, with pregabalin holding the distinction of FDA approval for SCI neuropathic pain specifically. Shoulder pain prevention through wheelchair optimization and strengthening programs is a cornerstone of long-term musculoskeletal health. Emerging neuromodulatory approaches offer hope for refractory central neuropathic pain, though evidence remains early-stage for most modalities.
