# Chapter 7: Neurogenic Bowel Management in Spinal Cord Injury

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Describe the enteric, parasympathetic, sympathetic, and somatic neural pathways governing gastrointestinal motility and continence.
2. Differentiate the internal anal sphincter (IAS) and external anal sphincter (EAS) by muscle type, innervation, and function.
3. Explain the normal sequence of defecation, including the role of the gastrocolic reflex.
4. Classify neurogenic bowel into upper motor neuron (reflex) and lower motor neuron (areflexic) patterns based on lesion level and clinical features.
5. Design and implement a comprehensive bowel program incorporating timing, positioning, pharmacologic stimulants, digital stimulation, and manual evacuation.
6. Manage acute gastrointestinal complications of SCI, including adynamic ileus, pseudo-obstruction, superior mesenteric artery syndrome, and sigmoid volvulus.

## 7.1 Introduction `[BOARD]`

Neurogenic bowel dysfunction affects virtually every person with a clinically significant spinal cord injury. Surveys consistently identify bowel management as one of the most distressing aspects of life with SCI, often ranking above bladder dysfunction and mobility limitations in its impact on quality of life (Coggrave et al., 2009). The goals of bowel management are to achieve predictable, complete evacuation within a reasonable timeframe, prevent incontinence between bowel programs, and avoid complications such as impaction, megacolon, hemorrhoids, and autonomic dysreflexia.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Patients frequently rate neurogenic bowel dysfunction as the most distressing complication of SCI, often more so than bladder problems or even loss of mobility. Effective bowel management demands the same systematic attention as any other organ-system complication.

## 7.2 Neuroanatomy of the Gastrointestinal Tract `[BOARD]`

### 7.2.1 The Enteric Nervous System (ENS)

The ENS is an intrinsic neural network embedded within the walls of the gastrointestinal tract, extending from the esophagus to the anus. It contains approximately 100 million neurons --- sometimes called the "second brain" --- and is capable of coordinating peristalsis, secretion, and absorption independent of extrinsic innervation. The ENS comprises two principal plexuses:

**Auerbach's (Myenteric) Plexus:** Located between the longitudinal and circular smooth muscle layers of the gut wall. Its primary function is **motor** --- coordinating the peristaltic contractions that propel luminal contents aborally. It regulates both the amplitude and frequency of smooth muscle contractions.

**Meissner's (Submucosal) Plexus:** Located within the submucosa, deep to the circular muscle layer. Its primary function is **sensory and secretory** --- detecting luminal distension and chemical stimuli and modulating mucosal blood flow, fluid secretion, and absorption.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The ENS is critical to understanding neurogenic bowel. Even after complete SCI, the ENS continues to coordinate local peristalsis. This is why patients with LMN (areflexic) bowel still experience slow transit and can eventually evacuate stool --- the ENS provides a baseline level of motility independent of the spinal cord.

### 7.2.2 Parasympathetic Innervation

Parasympathetic innervation to the GI tract is divided anatomically:

**Vagus Nerve (CN X):** Provides parasympathetic innervation from the esophagus to approximately the **mid-transverse colon** (splenic flexure). The vagus nerve increases motility, relaxes sphincters, and stimulates secretion throughout its distribution. Importantly, vagal innervation is unaffected by SCI because the vagus nerve arises from the brainstem and travels outside the spinal cord.

**Pelvic Nerve (S2-S4):** Provides parasympathetic innervation to the **distal colon (descending colon, sigmoid) and rectum**. These sacral parasympathetic fibers increase motility in the distal colon and rectum and relax the internal anal sphincter (IAS), facilitating defecation. Pelvic nerve parasympathetic function is disrupted in lesions involving the S2-S4 segments (LMN bowel).

### 7.2.3 Sympathetic Innervation

Sympathetic innervation to the GI tract arises from the thoracolumbar cord (T12-L2 for the colon) and reaches the bowel via the hypogastric nerve and the superior and inferior mesenteric ganglia. Sympathetic activation exerts effects that promote fecal storage:

- **Inhibits motility** (slows peristalsis and reduces propulsive contractions)
- **Contracts the IAS** (increases sphincter tone, maintaining continence)
- **Reduces secretion** (decreases fluid delivery to the lumen)

The net sympathetic effect on the bowel is to slow transit, firm stool consistency, and maintain anal closure --- functions that support storage and continence.

### 7.2.4 Somatic Innervation

**Pudendal Nerve (S2-S4):** Provides somatic motor innervation to the **external anal sphincter (EAS)**, which is composed of striated (skeletal) muscle under voluntary control. The pudendal nerve also carries sensory afferents from the perianal skin and anal canal, providing conscious awareness of rectal fullness and impending defecation.

### 7.2.5 Anal Sphincter Anatomy

| Feature | Internal Anal Sphincter (IAS) | External Anal Sphincter (EAS) |
|---|---|---|
| Muscle type | Smooth muscle | Striated (skeletal) muscle |
| Innervation | Sympathetic contracts, parasympathetic relaxes | Somatic (pudendal nerve, S2-S4) |
| Control | Involuntary (autonomic) | Voluntary |
| Resting tone | Provides ~70-85% of resting anal pressure | Provides ~15-30% of resting anal pressure |
| Function | Tonic contraction maintains continence at rest | Voluntary contraction provides additional guard |
| Reflex | Relaxes reflexively with rectal distension (RAIR) | Can be voluntarily contracted to defer defecation |

## 7.3 Normal Defecation Sequence `[BOARD]`

Understanding normal defecation is essential for designing bowel programs that leverage preserved physiologic mechanisms. The sequence proceeds as follows:

1. **Mass movement / high-amplitude propagating contractions** propel fecal material from the sigmoid colon into the rectum
2. **Rectosigmoid distension** activates intrinsic (ENS) and extrinsic (pelvic nerve) afferents
3. **Rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR):** Rectal distension triggers reflexive relaxation of the IAS via an intrinsic reflex arc mediated by the myenteric plexus and inhibitory neurotransmitters (nitric oxide, VIP)
4. **Sampling reflex:** A small amount of rectal content contacts the highly sensitive anal canal epithelium, allowing discrimination between gas, liquid, and solid stool
5. **Cortical awareness:** Afferent signals reaching the cerebral cortex produce the conscious sensation of rectal fullness and the urge to defecate
6. **Decision to defecate:** If socially appropriate, the individual voluntarily relaxes the EAS and puborectalis muscle (straightening the anorectal angle) and increases intra-abdominal pressure via Valsalva maneuver
7. **Rectal propulsive contractions** and increased intra-abdominal pressure evacuate the rectum
8. **Post-evacuation:** The EAS contracts, the puborectalis resumes its resting tone, and the anorectal angle is restored

### 7.3.1 Gastrocolic Reflex

The **gastrocolic reflex** is an increase in colonic motility triggered by gastric distension following food intake. It is mediated by both neural (vagal and intrinsic ENS pathways) and hormonal (cholecystokinin, gastrin, serotonin) mechanisms. The gastrocolic reflex is:

- **Strongest after the first meal of the day** (after a prolonged overnight fast)
- **Enhanced by warm beverages** and high-fat or high-calorie meals
- **Clinically leveraged** in bowel program design by scheduling the bowel program 30 minutes after a meal (typically breakfast) to capitalize on the reflexive increase in colonic motility

> **Clinical Pearl:** The gastrocolic reflex is strongest after the first meal of the day and is enhanced by warm beverages. Scheduling the bowel program 30 minutes after breakfast with a warm drink exploits this reflex to facilitate evacuation.

## 7.4 Classification of Neurogenic Bowel `[BOARD]`

### 7.4.1 Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) Reflex Bowel

Lesions above the conus medullaris (above approximately T12-L1 vertebral level) produce a reflex bowel pattern. The sacral reflex arc (S2-S4) remains intact, but descending cortical control is lost.

**Key Features:**

- **Intact sacral reflex arc:** The myenteric plexus, pelvic nerve afferents and efferents, and sacral cord segments are intact; reflexive peristalsis and defecation reflexes are preserved
- **Spastic EAS:** Loss of cortical inhibition results in increased EAS tone; the patient cannot voluntarily relax the EAS to permit defecation
- **Loss of voluntary control:** The patient cannot sense rectal fullness or voluntarily initiate or defer defecation
- **ENS function preserved:** Intrinsic peristalsis continues; sacral reflex-mediated colonic contractions can be triggered
- **Primary problem: Constipation and fecal impaction** --- stool is propelled through the colon by ENS and reflex activity but is retained by the spastic EAS
- **Digital stimulation is effective:** Gentle circular stretching of the anal canal triggers the sacral defecation reflex (rectal contraction with IAS relaxation), facilitating evacuation

### 7.4.2 Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) Areflexic Bowel

Lesions at or below the conus medullaris (conus medullaris or cauda equina) destroy the sacral reflex arc.

**Key Features:**

- **Absent sacral reflex arc:** Parasympathetic and somatic sacral segments are destroyed or denervated; no reflex-mediated defecation
- **Flaccid EAS:** Denervated striated muscle with reduced tone; poor voluntary sphincter control
- **ENS provides sole motility:** The enteric nervous system continues to coordinate slow peristalsis, but without sacral parasympathetic augmentation, transit is markedly slowed
- **Primary problem: Fecal incontinence** --- the flaccid sphincter cannot maintain continence, and stool leaks passively, especially with soft or liquid consistency
- **Manual evacuation is the primary technique:** Digital stimulation is NOT effective (no intact sacral reflex to trigger); gentle manual removal of stool from the rectum is required
- **Stool consistency management is critical:** Firmer stool is easier to retain; bulking agents and dietary fiber are used to increase stool consistency

| Feature | UMN Reflex Bowel | LMN Areflexic Bowel |
|---|---|---|
| Lesion level | Above conus medullaris | At or below conus/cauda equina |
| Sacral reflex arc | Intact | Absent |
| EAS tone | Spastic (increased) | Flaccid (decreased) |
| Primary problem | Constipation/impaction | Incontinence |
| ENS function | Preserved | Preserved (but sole driver) |
| Digital stimulation | Effective (triggers sacral reflex) | NOT effective |
| Evacuation method | Digital stimulation + suppository | Manual evacuation |
| Stool goal | Soft formed | Firm formed |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The cardinal distinction between UMN and LMN bowel is the integrity of the sacral reflex arc. If intact (UMN), digital stimulation triggers reflex evacuation and is the cornerstone of the bowel program. If absent (LMN), digital stimulation is futile, and manual evacuation is required.

## 7.5 The Bowel Program `[BOARD]`

A structured bowel program is the foundation of neurogenic bowel management. The program should be individualized based on bowel type (UMN vs. LMN), level of injury, functional abilities, lifestyle, and patient preference.

### 7.5.1 Timing

- Perform the bowel program at the **same time each day** (or every other day) to establish a predictable pattern
- Schedule **30 minutes after a meal** to exploit the gastrocolic reflex
- The first meal of the day produces the strongest gastrocolic reflex
- **Warm beverages** (coffee, tea, warm water) taken with or before the meal further stimulate the reflex
- Consistency is paramount --- the colon responds to regularity

### 7.5.2 Positioning

- **Seated on a commode** is preferred when possible: gravity assists descent of stool, and the sitting position straightens the anorectal angle, facilitating evacuation
- **Left side-lying** (left lateral decubitus) position is used when the patient cannot sit: this positions the sigmoid colon and rectum in a gravity-dependent orientation
- Avoid performing the bowel program supine in bed if at all possible, as this position is least efficient for evacuation

### 7.5.3 Chemical Stimulation (Suppositories)

**Bisacodyl Suppository:**

- A contact irritant/stimulant laxative that acts directly on colonic mucosa to increase peristaltic contractions and water/electrolyte secretion into the lumen
- Dose: 10 mg rectally
- Must be placed **against the rectal wall** (not in the center of the stool bolus) for mucosal contact and activation
- Two formulations:
  - **Polyethylene glycol (PEG)-based** (hydrogenated vegetable oil-free): Dissolves in 15-20 minutes; faster onset; generally preferred
  - **Vegetable oil/hydrogenated oil-based:** Requires body heat to melt; dissolves in 30-60 minutes; slower but still effective
- Inserted prior to digital stimulation to provide a chemical stimulus while the mechanical reflex is triggered

**Mini-enema (Docusate/Glycerin):** Provides osmotic and lubricating effect; can be used as an alternative or supplement to bisacodyl suppository.

### 7.5.4 Digital Stimulation (UMN Bowel)

Digital stimulation is the primary mechanical technique for triggering reflex evacuation in patients with UMN (reflex) bowel.

**Technique:**

1. Apply water-soluble lubricant to a gloved finger
2. Insert the finger into the rectum gently
3. Perform a slow, circular motion against the rectal wall for **15-20 seconds**
4. Withdraw the finger and wait for stool to be expelled reflexively
5. Repeat the stimulation every **5-10 minutes** until evacuation is complete
6. **Completion criteria:** No stool is expelled after two consecutive stimulations, or only mucus is returned

**Mechanism:** The circular stretching of the anal canal and rectal wall activates sacral parasympathetic afferents, triggering the defecation reflex --- coordinated rectal contraction with IAS relaxation --- via the intact sacral reflex arc.

**Important:** Digital stimulation is effective ONLY in UMN (reflex) bowel where the sacral arc is intact. It is NOT effective in LMN (areflexic) bowel.

### 7.5.5 Manual Evacuation (LMN Bowel)

For patients with LMN (areflexic) bowel, manual evacuation is the primary technique.

**Technique:**

1. Apply water-soluble lubricant to a gloved finger
2. Insert the finger into the rectum and gently hook and remove stool
3. Use a slow, careful technique to avoid mucosal trauma
4. Continue until the rectum is empty

**Cautions:** Vigorous manual evacuation can cause mucosal abrasion, hemorrhoids, anal fissures, and --- in patients with injuries at T6 or above --- autonomic dysreflexia. A gentle, unhurried approach is essential.

### 7.5.6 Program Goals

- Total bowel program time: **less than 60 minutes** (ideally 30-45 minutes)
- Frequency: Daily or every other day (individualized)
- Complete evacuation with no incontinence between programs
- Minimal or no use of digital stimulation beyond the scheduled program
- Patient and caregiver comfort with the technique

### 7.5.7 Sample UMN Bowel Program

| Step | Action | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Warm beverage + meal (gastrocolic reflex) | T = 0 minutes |
| 2 | Transfer to commode or position in left lateral | T = 20-30 minutes |
| 3 | Insert bisacodyl suppository against rectal wall | T = 30 minutes |
| 4 | Wait for suppository to dissolve | 15-20 minutes (PEG-based) |
| 5 | Begin digital stimulation (15-20 sec, circular) | T = 45-50 minutes |
| 6 | Repeat digital stimulation every 5-10 min as needed | Until complete |
| 7 | Completion: no stool after 2 consecutive stimulations | Goal <60 min total |

## 7.6 Diet and Medications `[BOARD]`

### 7.6.1 Dietary Fiber

- Recommended intake: **15-30 grams per day** of dietary fiber
- Sources: whole grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes, psyllium supplements
- Fiber increases stool bulk, promotes peristalsis, and normalizes transit time
- Must be accompanied by adequate fluid intake (2 liters/day minimum) to prevent impaction
- Excessive fiber without adequate fluid can worsen constipation

### 7.6.2 Fluid Intake

- Minimum **2 liters per day** of oral fluids
- Must be balanced against bladder management constraints (fluid restriction for CIC volumes)
- Warm beverages stimulate the gastrocolic reflex

### 7.6.3 Regular Meal Patterns

- Eating at consistent times each day promotes regular colonic motility
- Skipping meals diminishes the gastrocolic reflex and disrupts bowel regularity

### 7.6.4 Medications

| Medication | Class | Dose | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Docusate sodium | Stool softener | 100-300 mg/day | Surfactant; allows water and fat to penetrate stool | Does not stimulate motility; softens stool |
| Senna | Stimulant laxative | 1-4 tablets at bedtime | Anthraquinone; stimulates myenteric plexus → increases peristalsis | Take at bedtime for morning bowel program |
| Bisacodyl (oral) | Stimulant laxative | 5-15 mg PO | Contact irritant; stimulates colonic mucosa | Supplement to suppository program |
| Bisacodyl (suppository) | Stimulant laxative | 10 mg PR | Direct mucosal stimulation | Place against rectal wall |
| Polyethylene glycol (MiraLAX) | Osmotic laxative | 17 g/day in 8 oz liquid | Osmotic effect; retains water in colon | Does not cause electrolyte disturbances |
| Glycerin suppository | Osmotic/lubricant | 1 suppository PR | Osmotic draw + lubrication | Milder than bisacodyl; LMN bowel |

**Oral stimulant laxatives** (senna, bisacodyl) are typically administered at bedtime (HS) so that their effect --- which has an onset of 6-12 hours --- coincides with the morning bowel program.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Senna and oral bisacodyl taken at bedtime "prime" the colon overnight, so that by morning, stool has been propelled into the rectosigmoid region and is ready for evacuation by the bowel program.

## 7.7 Acute Gastrointestinal Complications `[BOARD]`

### 7.7.1 Adynamic (Paralytic) Ileus

**Epidemiology:** Occurs in approximately **50% of acute SCI patients**, particularly those with cervical and high thoracic injuries.

**Onset:** Typically develops within **24-48 hours** of injury.

**Resolution:** Usually resolves within **1 week** (3-7 days) with conservative management.

**Pathophysiology:** Loss of sympathetic and parasympathetic coordination, systemic inflammatory response, opioid administration, immobility, and electrolyte disturbances all contribute to temporary cessation of effective peristalsis.

**Clinical Features:**

- Abdominal distension
- Absent bowel sounds
- Nausea, vomiting
- Inability to tolerate oral feeding
- No passage of flatus or stool

**Management:**

| Intervention | Details |
|---|---|
| NPO (nil per os) | No oral intake until bowel function returns |
| Nasogastric tube (NGT) | Decompression; low intermittent suction |
| IV fluids | Maintain hydration and electrolyte balance |
| Electrolyte correction | Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia can perpetuate ileus |
| Total parenteral nutrition (TPN) | If ileus persists >3 days and enteral feeding not possible |
| Metoclopramide | Prokinetic agent; dopamine antagonist; enhances gastric emptying and small bowel motility |
| Erythromycin | Motilin receptor agonist; prokinetic; useful for persistent gastroparesis/ileus |
| Neostigmine | Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor; for acute colonic pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie syndrome); use with cardiac monitoring |

**Transition to enteral feeding** should occur as soon as bowel function returns, evidenced by passage of flatus, reduction in NGT output, and return of bowel sounds.

### 7.7.2 Acute Colonic Pseudo-Obstruction (Ogilvie Syndrome) `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Ogilvie syndrome is a functional obstruction of the colon without a mechanical cause, resulting in massive colonic dilation. It occurs in critically ill patients, including those with acute SCI, and is attributed to an imbalance between sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation to the colon.

**Critical threshold:** Cecal diameter greater than **12 cm** on abdominal radiograph is associated with a significant risk of **cecal perforation**, a surgical emergency with high mortality.

**Management:**

- Conservative: NGT decompression, correction of electrolytes, discontinuation of offending medications (opioids, anticholinergics), patient repositioning (prone or lateral to redistribute gas)
- Pharmacologic: **Neostigmine** 2 mg IV over 3-5 minutes with continuous cardiac monitoring (risk of bradycardia; atropine at bedside); effective in approximately 80-90% of cases
- Endoscopic: Colonoscopic decompression if pharmacologic treatment fails
- Surgical: Cecostomy or colectomy for refractory cases or perforation

> **Clinical Pearl:** In Ogilvie syndrome, a cecal diameter exceeding 12 cm on plain radiograph signals imminent perforation risk and demands urgent intervention --- neostigmine, colonoscopic decompression, or surgical consultation.

### 7.7.3 Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA) Syndrome `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

SMA syndrome (Wilkie syndrome) results from compression of the third (horizontal) portion of the duodenum between the superior mesenteric artery anteriorly and the aorta/vertebral column posteriorly. In SCI patients, rapid weight loss and prolonged supine positioning reduce the mesenteric fat pad that normally maintains the aortomesenteric angle, predisposing to duodenal compression.

**Clinical Features:**

- Bilious vomiting (proximal obstruction)
- Epigastric pain and distension
- Postprandial fullness
- Weight loss

**Diagnosis:** CT angiography or upper GI series demonstrating extrinsic compression of the duodenum at the aortomesenteric angle (normally >25 degrees, narrowed to <22 degrees in SMA syndrome).

**Management:**

- Nutritional support (parenteral or post-obstruction enteral feeding via nasojejunal tube)
- Positioning: Left lateral decubitus or prone positioning may relieve compression
- Weight gain to restore mesenteric fat pad
- Surgical: Duodenojejunostomy (bypass) for refractory cases

### 7.7.4 Sigmoid Volvulus `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Sigmoid volvulus --- twisting of the sigmoid colon around its mesentery --- occurs with increased frequency in the SCI population, particularly in patients with chronic constipation, redundant sigmoid colon, and prolonged immobility.

**Clinical Features:**

- Acute abdominal distension
- Obstipation (inability to pass stool or flatus)
- "Bent inner tube" or "coffee bean" sign on abdominal radiograph

**Management:**

- Endoscopic detorsion via flexible sigmoidoscopy (successful in 70-80% of cases)
- Rectal tube placement after detorsion to prevent recurrence
- Surgical resection (sigmoidectomy) for recurrent volvulus, failed endoscopic reduction, or signs of bowel ischemia/perforation

## 7.8 Transanal Irrigation `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Transanal irrigation (TAI) involves instillation of water into the rectum and distal colon via a rectal catheter or balloon-tipped device, followed by controlled drainage of the irrigant and stool. It is a promising intermediate therapy for patients whose conventional bowel programs are inadequate.

**Indications:**

- Neurogenic bowel refractory to conventional bowel program
- Fecal incontinence despite dietary and pharmacologic optimization
- Excessive bowel program duration (>60 minutes regularly)

**Evidence:** Systematic reviews indicate that TAI reduces constipation severity, decreases fecal incontinence episodes, and improves quality of life in patients with neurogenic bowel from SCI. A Cochrane review and the PVA Consortium guidelines identify TAI as a reasonable step before considering surgical options (Emmanuel et al., 2013).

**Complications:** Bowel perforation (rare, estimated 1 per 50,000 irrigations), autonomic dysreflexia, abdominal discomfort.

## 7.9 Surgical Options for Refractory Neurogenic Bowel `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 7.9.1 Malone Antegrade Continence Enema (MACE/ACE Procedure)

The Malone procedure creates a continent catheterizable channel (typically using the appendix or a cecal flap) connecting the skin surface to the cecum. Antegrade enemas are administered through this channel, allowing irrigation of the entire colon from proximal to distal, producing a more complete and efficient evacuation than retrograde (transanal) approaches.

**Outcomes:** High patient satisfaction; reduced time for bowel care; improved continence; primarily used in pediatric populations but applicable to adults with SCI.

### 7.9.2 Colostomy

Colostomy (typically sigmoid or descending colon) is considered a last-resort option for patients with intractable neurogenic bowel dysfunction that has failed all conservative, pharmacologic, and less invasive surgical measures.

**Considerations:**

- Patients frequently report improved quality of life and reduced time spent on bowel care after colostomy, despite initial reluctance
- Colostomy does not eliminate bowel management but simplifies it
- Complications include parastomal hernia, stomal prolapse, and skin irritation
- Shared decision-making is essential, and patient perspective must guide the decision

## 7.10 Long-Term Bowel Complications `[BOARD]`

### 7.10.1 Hemorrhoids

Chronic straining, prolonged sitting on the commode, and repeated digital stimulation/manual evacuation predispose SCI patients to hemorrhoids. Conservative management includes stool softeners, topical treatments, and minimizing time on the commode. Severe cases may require surgical hemorrhoidectomy.

### 7.10.2 Rectal Prolapse

Chronic straining and denervation of pelvic floor muscles can lead to rectal prolapse, particularly in patients with LMN bowel and a flaccid pelvic floor.

### 7.10.3 Autonomic Dysreflexia

Bowel distension, constipation, impaction, hemorrhoids, anal fissures, and the bowel program itself (digital stimulation, suppository insertion) are all potential triggers for autonomic dysreflexia in patients with injuries at T6 or above. Lidocaine jelly applied to the rectum prior to digital stimulation may reduce the risk.

### 7.10.4 Colorectal Cancer Screening

SCI patients should undergo colorectal cancer screening according to the same guidelines as the general population (colonoscopy beginning at age 45 or earlier based on risk factors). Bowel preparation and colonoscopy may require special accommodations (positioning, anesthesia, AD monitoring).

## 7.11 Emerging Therapies `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 7.11.1 Newer Pharmacologic Agents

Several medications approved for chronic constipation or irritable bowel syndrome with constipation (IBS-C) are being explored for neurogenic bowel:

- **Prucalopride:** A selective serotonin 5-HT4 receptor agonist that accelerates colonic transit; emerging evidence supports its use in neurogenic bowel
- **Lubiprostone:** A chloride channel activator that increases intestinal fluid secretion and accelerates transit
- **Linaclotide:** A guanylate cyclase-C agonist that increases intestinal fluid secretion

These agents may serve as adjuncts to the conventional bowel program in patients with refractory slow-transit constipation.

### 7.11.2 Sacral Nerve Stimulation

Sacral neuromodulation is being investigated as a potential treatment for neurogenic bowel dysfunction, with preliminary evidence suggesting improvements in bowel function and continence in selected patients with incomplete SCI.

### 7.11.3 Electrical Stimulation

Abdominal surface electrical stimulation and transcutaneous posterior tibial nerve stimulation are being studied as noninvasive modalities to improve colonic transit and bowel function in SCI.

## 7.12 Integrated Bowel Management Algorithm `[BOARD]`

**Step 1: Classify Bowel Type**
- UMN (reflex) bowel: lesion above conus, intact sacral arc, spastic EAS
- LMN (areflexic) bowel: lesion at/below conus, absent sacral arc, flaccid EAS

**Step 2: Establish Bowel Program**
- Timing: 30 min after meal, same time daily
- Positioning: Seated on commode if possible, left lateral if not
- UMN: Bisacodyl suppository + digital stimulation
- LMN: Manual evacuation + glycerin suppository

**Step 3: Optimize Diet and Medications**
- Fiber 15-30 g/day, fluid 2 L/day, regular meals
- Stool softener (docusate), stimulant laxative (senna) HS, osmotic laxative (MiraLAX) as needed

**Step 4: Escalate if Needed**
- If bowel program duration >60 min or persistent incontinence: increase medication doses, adjust timing/frequency
- Consider prokinetic agents (prucalopride, metoclopramide)

**Step 5: Intermediate Interventions**
- Transanal irrigation for refractory cases

**Step 6: Surgical Options**
- Malone/ACE procedure
- Colostomy (last resort, but often highly satisfactory for patients)
