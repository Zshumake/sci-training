# Chapter 2: Epidemiology and Etiology of Spinal Cord Injury

## Learning Objectives

1. State the incidence, prevalence, and demographic trends of traumatic spinal cord injury in the United States and worldwide
2. Identify the leading etiologies of SCI and how they vary by age group
3. Describe the distribution of neurologic injury level and ASIA Impairment Scale grade at discharge
4. Discuss associated injuries and their frequency in SCI patients
5. Outline marital status, employment, and hospitalization trends following SCI
6. Interpret life expectancy tables based on age at injury and injury severity
7. Identify leading causes of mortality after SCI and changes over time
8. Estimate lifetime costs associated with SCI by severity and age

---

## 2.1 Incidence and Prevalence [BOARD]

### 2.1.1 United States Incidence

The annual incidence of traumatic spinal cord injury (tSCI) in the United States is approximately 54 cases per million population, translating to roughly 17,700-18,000 new cases per year (not including those who die at the scene of injury). When prehospital deaths are included, the total number rises to an estimated 54 per million, or approximately 18,400 per year. These figures have remained relatively stable over recent decades, though subtle increases reflect improved survival at the scene and during acute care.

The National Spinal Cord Injury Statistical Center (NSCISC) database, maintained at the University of Alabama at Birmingham, has collected data on persons treated at federally funded Model Spinal Cord Injury Care Systems since 1973 and remains the most comprehensive longitudinal SCI dataset in the world. As of the most recent data, it encompasses information on over 36,000 individuals.

### 2.1.2 International Incidence

Global incidence varies substantially by region and reflects differences in etiologic patterns, road safety infrastructure, violence rates, and data collection methodologies. Worldwide, approximately 250,000-500,000 new cases of SCI occur annually. The estimated global incidence is 13 cases per 100,000 population per year, though reported rates range from as low as 2.5 per 100,000 in some European countries to over 80 per 100,000 in certain regions with high rates of violence or traffic injuries.

Low- and middle-income countries bear a disproportionate burden due to limited road safety measures, higher rates of occupational injury, and less developed trauma and rehabilitation systems. Prevention efforts in high-income countries have successfully reduced certain etiologies (particularly violence-related injuries), while falls have increased with aging populations.

### 2.1.3 Prevalence

The estimated prevalence of persons living with SCI in the United States ranges from approximately 282,000 to 308,000 persons. This figure has increased over time as survival rates have improved, reflecting advances in acute care, rehabilitation, and chronic disease management. The prevalence is expected to continue rising as the population ages and long-term survival improves.

## 2.2 Demographics [BOARD]

### 2.2.1 Sex

SCI disproportionately affects males, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 4:1. Since 2015, roughly 79% of new tSCI cases have been male. This ratio has remained relatively stable over time, though a modest trend toward increasing female representation has been noted (from approximately 82% male prior to 1980 to approximately 79% male since 2015), likely attributable to the rising proportion of fall-related injuries among the elderly of both sexes.

### 2.2.2 Age

The mean age at injury has increased substantially over the decades, rising from 29 years in the 1970s to approximately 44 years since 2015. This shift reflects two interrelated trends: the aging of the general population and the increasing proportion of fall-related injuries (which predominantly affect older adults). Over 11.5% of new SCI cases now occur in persons aged 60 years and older, a dramatic increase from prior decades.

The age distribution is bimodal, with a primary peak in young adults aged 16-30 years (predominantly from motor vehicle crashes, violence, and sports) and a secondary peak in adults aged 65 and older (predominantly from falls). The bimodal distribution has flattened as the second peak has grown relative to the first.

### 2.2.3 Racial and Ethnic Distribution

Among persons injured since 2015, approximately 59.5% are non-Hispanic White, 22.6% are non-Hispanic Black, 13.5% are Hispanic, 2.6% are Asian, and 1.8% are other or unknown. Non-Hispanic Black individuals are overrepresented relative to their share of the general population, largely due to higher rates of violence-related injuries.

### 2.2.4 Temporal Patterns

SCI demonstrates seasonal and temporal clustering. Injuries are most common during summer months, with July being the peak month. Injuries are more frequent on weekends, particularly Saturday, and during nighttime hours. These patterns correlate with increased recreational activities, alcohol use, and high-risk behaviors during these periods.

## 2.3 Etiology [BOARD]

### 2.3.1 Leading Causes

The etiologic spectrum of SCI has shifted over the decades tracked by the NSCISC:

| Etiology | Percentage (since 2015) | Trend |
|----------|------------------------|-------|
| Motor vehicle crashes (MVC) | 38.3% | Declining from >46% pre-1980 |
| Falls | 31.6% | Increasing from 17% pre-1980 |
| Acts of violence (primarily gunshot wounds) | 13.8% | Peaked at 24.8% in 1990s; declining |
| Sports/recreation | 8.2% | Declining from 14.2% pre-1980 |
| Medical/surgical | 4.2% | Stable |
| Other/unknown | 3.9% | Stable |

**Table 2-1. Etiology of Traumatic SCI by Era**

**Motor vehicle crashes** remain the leading cause overall, encompassing automobile, motorcycle, and other vehicular collisions. Implementation of seatbelt legislation, airbag technology, and impaired driving enforcement has contributed to their relative decline.

**Falls** have risen to the second leading cause and are now the leading cause of SCI in persons aged 45 and older. Falls from heights (rooftop, ladder, scaffolding) predominate in younger adults, while ground-level falls are more common in the elderly, often in the setting of preexisting cervical spondylosis producing central cord syndrome.

**Acts of violence** are predominantly gunshot wounds (GSW), with stab wounds representing a smaller proportion. Violence-related SCI peaked during the 1990s (reaching nearly 25% of all cases) and has since declined. GSW-related SCI is more common in urban settings and disproportionately affects young males and minority populations.

**Sports and recreation injuries** account for 8-9% of cases, with diving being the most common sporting mechanism. Water-related sports (diving into shallow water) account for the majority of sport-related cervical SCI. Other at-risk activities include football, cycling, equestrian sports, skiing, and all-terrain vehicle use.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Falls are now the leading cause of SCI in persons over age 45, and typically produce cervical injuries -- particularly central cord syndrome from hyperextension in the setting of pre-existing cervical spondylosis. The clinician should suspect SCI in any elderly patient who falls and presents with disproportionate upper extremity weakness.

### 2.3.2 Age-Specific Patterns

- **Children (0-14 years):** MVC is the leading cause, including pedestrian and bicycle-versus-motor-vehicle incidents. Non-accidental trauma (child abuse) is a significant cause in infants and toddlers. Pediatric SCI incidence is approximately 1.99 per 100,000, with a mean age of 14.6 years and a male predominance of approximately 2:1.
- **Young adults (15-29 years):** MVC predominates, followed by violence and sports. This age group has the highest incidence of complete injuries.
- **Middle-aged adults (30-44 years):** MVC remains leading, with increasing contribution from falls (particularly occupational).
- **Older adults (45+ years):** Falls become the leading cause, surpassing MVC. Injuries tend to be incomplete (especially central cord syndrome) and cervical in location.

### 2.3.3 International Etiologic Variation

Etiologic patterns vary substantially by country and region. In sub-Saharan Africa, falls and motor vehicle collisions are predominant causes, while violence-related SCI is particularly high in regions such as South Africa and certain Latin American countries. In South Asia, falls from trees (particularly during agricultural work) represent a unique mechanism. In developed East Asian countries, falls among the elderly are increasingly common, mirroring trends in North America and Europe.

## 2.4 Injury Level and Category Distribution [BOARD]

### 2.4.1 Level Distribution

Cervical injuries are the most common, accounting for approximately 58% of all traumatic SCI. The most common single neurologic level is C5, followed by C4 and C6. For paraplegia, T12 is the most common level.

The distribution by region since 2015 is approximately:
- Cervical: 58%
- Thoracic: 32%
- Lumbar: 10%

### 2.4.2 Category at Discharge

The distribution of neurologic categories at discharge has shifted dramatically over time, reflecting improved survival of patients with more severe injuries and better classification systems:

| Category | Percentage at Discharge (since 2015) |
|----------|--------------------------------------|
| Incomplete tetraplegia | 47.4% |
| Incomplete paraplegia | 20.0% |
| Complete paraplegia | 19.7% |
| Complete tetraplegia | 12.3% |

**Table 2-2. Neurologic Category at Discharge**

Incomplete tetraplegia has become the most common category, replacing complete paraplegia, which was historically most frequent. This shift reflects the aging population (older patients more likely to sustain incomplete cervical injuries from falls) and improved acute survival of patients with high cervical injuries.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The most common neurologic category at discharge is now incomplete tetraplegia (approximately 47%), largely driven by the increasing frequency of central cord syndrome in elderly patients who fall. Complete tetraplegia is the least common category at approximately 12.3%, in part because these patients have historically had lower acute survival rates.

## 2.5 Associated Injuries and Concomitant Conditions [BOARD]

### 2.5.1 Concomitant Injuries

SCI rarely occurs in isolation. Approximately 47% of patients with traumatic SCI have at least one significant concomitant injury. Among these, the most common associated injuries include:

- Traumatic brain injury (TBI): Present in up to 60% of patients with cervical SCI, and 40-50% overall
- Chest injuries: Rib fractures, pneumothorax, hemothorax
- Long bone fractures: Extremity fractures, which may be occult in the insensate patient
- Abdominal injuries: Solid organ and hollow visceral injuries, particularly in high-velocity mechanisms
- Facial fractures
- Pelvic fractures

The combination of SCI and TBI (dual diagnosis) presents particular rehabilitation challenges, as cognitive impairments may limit the patient's ability to participate in and benefit from SCI-specific rehabilitation programs.

### 2.5.2 Noncontiguous Vertebral Fractures

Noncontiguous vertebral fractures occur in 12-28% of patients with spinal fractures. This high incidence mandates complete spinal imaging in all patients with an identified vertebral fracture. Failure to image the entire spine risks missing a second unstable injury at a remote level, potentially leading to secondary neurologic injury during mobilization.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Noncontiguous vertebral fractures occur in 12-28% of patients with spinal fractures. All patients with one identified vertebral fracture should undergo imaging of the entire spine to exclude additional injuries.

## 2.6 Marital Status and Social Outcomes [BOARD]

### 2.6.1 Marital Status

At the time of injury, approximately 51.4% of persons with SCI are single (never married), reflecting the young demographic most commonly injured. Following injury, the likelihood of marriage decreases and the divorce rate increases relative to the general population. However, marriages that occur after SCI tend to have lower divorce rates than those existing at the time of injury, possibly reflecting greater partner awareness and commitment.

### 2.6.2 Employment

Employment status following SCI represents one of the most significant long-term quality-of-life indicators:

| Time Post-Injury | Employed (%) |
|-------------------|-------------|
| At time of injury | 58.1% |
| 1 year post-injury | 12-18% |
| 5 years post-injury | 25.8% |
| 10 years post-injury | 35% |
| 20 years post-injury | 35% |

**Table 2-3. Employment Rates Following SCI**

The dramatic decline in employment at 1 year post-injury reflects the acute rehabilitation period, medical instability, and the time required for psychosocial adjustment. The gradual increase over subsequent years reflects return to previous employment or retraining.

**Predictors of return to employment** (in order of importance):
1. **Education level** -- the single strongest predictor; higher education confers greater likelihood of employment
2. Younger age at injury
3. Male sex
4. White/Caucasian race
5. AIS D (motor incomplete, functional) grade
6. Nonviolent etiology
7. Ability to drive
8. Pre-injury employment history
9. Fewer medical complications

Education functions as a powerful mediator because it facilitates transition to sedentary and cognitive occupations that accommodate physical limitations. Vocational rehabilitation services, assistive technology, and reasonable workplace accommodations further enhance employment potential.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Education is the number one predictor of return to employment after SCI. Early engagement of vocational rehabilitation services and support for continuing education should be integral components of the rehabilitation plan.

## 2.7 Hospitalization Trends [BOARD]

Length of stay for both acute care and inpatient rehabilitation has decreased substantially over the decades, driven by improvements in acute management, changes in healthcare reimbursement, and evolving rehabilitation practices:

| Era | Acute Care (days) | Rehab (days) | Total (days) |
|-----|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| 1970s | 24 | 98 | 122 |
| 2015-present | 11 | 35 | 46 |

**Table 2-4. Average Length of Stay Trends**

The reduction in rehabilitation length of stay from nearly 100 days to approximately 35 days has raised concerns about the adequacy of inpatient rehabilitation for achieving optimal functional outcomes, particularly for patients with high tetraplegia who require extensive adaptive training. However, this trend also reflects the shift toward outpatient and community-based rehabilitation models.

## 2.8 Life Expectancy [BOARD]

### 2.8.1 Overview

Life expectancy following SCI, while improved compared to earlier decades, remains significantly lower than for the general population, and the reduction is proportional to injury severity. Life expectancy estimates are based on survival data from the NSCISC Model Systems and Shriners Hospitals.

### 2.8.2 Life Expectancy by Severity and Age

The following table provides estimated remaining years of life for persons who survive the first year after injury:

| Age at Injury | No SCI | Motor Functional (Any Level) | Paraplegia | Low Tetra (C5-C8) | High Tetra (C1-C4) | Ventilator Dependent |
|--------------|--------|------------------------------|------------|--------------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| 20 years | 59.6 | 53.4 | 46.4 | 41.3 | 35.3 | 18.1 |
| 40 years | 40.5 | 35.0 | 28.9 | 24.7 | 20.2 | 10.3 |
| 60 years | 22.8 | 18.0 | 13.5 | 10.8 | 8.6 | 3.5 |

**Table 2-5. Estimated Life Expectancy (Remaining Years) for Persons Surviving First Year Post-Injury**

Key observations:
- Even patients with motor-functional incomplete injuries (AIS D) lose approximately 6 years of life expectancy compared to the general population at age 20
- High-level tetraplegia (C1-C4) reduces life expectancy by approximately 24 years at age 20
- Ventilator-dependent patients lose more than 40 years of life expectancy at age 20
- The relative reduction in life expectancy diminishes with increasing age at injury
- Life expectancy for all SCI categories has improved over time, but remains substantially below the general population

### 2.8.3 First-Year Mortality

The highest mortality risk occurs during the first year after injury, driven by complications of the acute injury, respiratory failure, and thromboembolic events. First-year mortality has declined by approximately 40% over the past three decades due to improvements in prehospital care, emergency medicine, critical care, and acute rehabilitation. However, mortality during the first two years after injury remains many-fold higher than in subsequent years.

## 2.9 Mortality and Causes of Death [BOARD]

### 2.9.1 Leading Causes of Death

The causes of mortality after SCI have evolved dramatically as medical management has improved:

**Current leading causes of death (post-first year):**
1. **Respiratory diseases** (primarily pneumonia and influenza) -- leading cause overall (~19.6% of deaths)
2. **Heart disease** (hypertensive and ischemic) -- second leading cause
3. **Septicemia** (secondary to urinary tract infections and pressure injury infections) -- third leading cause
4. **Cancer**
5. **Unintentional injuries**

**Historical context:** Prior to the widespread implementation of intermittent catheterization and improved urologic management, genitourinary disease (renal failure from chronic pyelonephritis, amyloidosis, and nephrolithiasis) was the leading cause of death after SCI for over 40 years. The shift to respiratory causes as the primary killer reflects both the success of urologic management advances and the persistent challenge of respiratory complications in SCI.

### 2.9.2 Respiratory Mortality

Respiratory complications remain the most significant threat to long-term survival. Factors contributing to respiratory vulnerability include:

- Impaired respiratory mechanics (paralysis of intercostals, abdominals, and diaphragm depending on level)
- Impaired cough effectiveness, leading to retained secretions and atelectasis
- Increased susceptibility to aspiration
- Reduced mobility promoting pulmonary deconditioning
- Sleep-disordered breathing

### 2.9.3 Suicide

The suicide rate among persons with SCI is approximately 3 times that of the age- and sex-matched general population. Suicide risk is highest in younger males during the first 6 years post-injury, particularly those with a history of substance use, depression, or social isolation. Screening for depression and suicidal ideation should be a routine component of SCI follow-up care.

### 2.9.4 Cardiovascular Disease

Cardiovascular disease has emerged as an increasingly important cause of mortality in the chronic SCI population. Persons with SCI have accelerated cardiovascular aging, with higher rates of metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, and physical deconditioning. The cardiometabolic risk profile is exacerbated by reduced mobility, altered body composition (increased adiposity, decreased lean mass), and neurogenic factors (altered autonomic cardiovascular regulation).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Respiratory disease (especially pneumonia) is now the leading cause of death in SCI patients, having replaced genitourinary disease, which was historically the leading cause for over 40 years. This shift reflects the success of intermittent catheterization and improved urologic care. Aggressive pulmonary hygiene, vaccination, and respiratory muscle training are essential preventive strategies.

## 2.10 Lifetime Costs [BOARD]

### 2.10.1 Direct Costs

The economic burden of SCI is substantial and correlates directly with injury severity:

| Severity | First-Year Cost (2024 USD) | Each Subsequent Year | Estimated Lifetime Cost (Age 25) |
|----------|---------------------------|---------------------|----------------------------------|
| High tetraplegia (C1-C4) | $1,163,962 | $209,795 | $5,100,542 |
| Low tetraplegia (C5-C8) | $830,708 | $128,276 | $3,732,181 |
| Paraplegia | $578,072 | $78,136 | $2,544,860 |
| Incomplete motor functional (any level) | $383,346 | $50,066 | $1,798,482 |

**Table 2-6. Estimated Direct Costs of SCI by Severity**

These estimates include direct healthcare costs (hospitalization, rehabilitation, physician services, durable medical equipment, medications, personal care attendants, home modifications, and transportation) but do not include indirect costs.

### 2.10.2 Indirect Costs

Average annual indirect costs (lost wages, fringe benefits, productivity losses) are estimated at approximately $95,309 per year (2024 dollars). Over a lifetime, indirect costs may equal or exceed direct medical costs, particularly for younger, higher-functioning individuals who would otherwise have been employed.

### 2.10.3 Cost Drivers

The major cost drivers in SCI care include:
- Personal care attendant services (particularly for tetraplegia)
- Durable medical equipment (power wheelchairs, hospital beds, lift systems)
- Home modifications for accessibility
- Rehospitalizations for complications (pressure injuries, UTIs, respiratory infections)
- Medications (including spasticity management, pain, neurogenic bowel/bladder)
- Outpatient therapy and specialist visits

> **Clinical Pearl:** High-level tetraplegia (C1-C4) carries first-year costs exceeding $1 million and lifetime costs approaching $5 million at age 25. These figures underscore the importance of primary prevention programs and the economic justification for investment in acute care and rehabilitation resources that optimize outcomes and reduce long-term complications.

## 2.11 Historical Context and Evolution of SCI Care [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 2.11.1 Early History

Prior to World War II, SCI was considered uniformly fatal within a few years, with most patients succumbing to urinary sepsis or pressure injury complications. Sir Ludwig Guttmann established the National Spinal Injuries Centre at Stoke Mandeville Hospital in 1944 and pioneered the concept of comprehensive SCI rehabilitation, including regular turning for pressure injury prevention, intermittent catheterization for bladder management, and sport as therapy. His work transformed SCI from a death sentence to a manageable chronic condition.

The development of the Model Spinal Cord Injury Care System in the United States in 1970 established a network of specialized centers providing coordinated acute care, rehabilitation, and lifelong follow-up. These centers became the foundation for SCI research and the source of the NSCISC database.

### 2.11.2 Key Advances

Major advances that have shaped modern SCI epidemiology include:
- **1970s:** Introduction of clean intermittent catheterization (Lapides), dramatically reducing urologic mortality
- **1980s:** Improved prehospital spinal immobilization protocols; CT and MRI imaging
- **1990s:** Seat belt and airbag legislation; decline in violence-related SCI
- **2000s:** Improved critical care for high cervical injuries; advances in respiratory management
- **2010s-present:** Aging population shifts in demographics; increasing fall-related injuries; reduced length of stay

## 2.12 International Epidemiology [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 2.12.1 Regional Variation

Global SCI epidemiology reflects local trauma patterns, healthcare infrastructure, and cultural factors:

- **North America:** Incidence 39-54 per million; MVC leading cause overall, falls increasing
- **Western Europe:** Incidence 10-30 per million; falls increasingly predominant with aging populations
- **Sub-Saharan Africa:** Limited data; falls and road traffic injuries predominate; high mortality from limited acute care
- **South Asia:** Falls from heights (agricultural work, construction) represent a unique mechanism
- **East Asia:** Rapidly increasing elderly fall-related SCI in Japan, South Korea, and China
- **Latin America:** High rates of violence-related SCI in select countries (Brazil, Colombia)
- **Middle East:** Road traffic injuries predominate; variable violence-related SCI

### 2.12.2 Low-Resource Settings

In low- and middle-income countries, SCI outcomes remain substantially worse due to:
- Delayed presentation to definitive care
- Limited access to spinal surgery
- Absence of specialized rehabilitation facilities
- Higher rates of pressure injuries and urologic complications
- Limited availability of wheelchair and assistive technology
- Social stigma and inadequate community reintegration support

International initiatives such as the WHO International Perspectives on Spinal Cord Injury report (2013) and the International Spinal Cord Society (ISCoS) have advocated for improved global standards of SCI care.

## 2.13 Prevention Programs [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 2.13.1 Primary Prevention

Effective primary prevention strategies target the major etiologies:

- **Motor vehicle crashes:** Seatbelt legislation, graduated licensing, impaired driving enforcement, vehicle safety technology (airbags, electronic stability control, autonomous emergency braking)
- **Falls in elderly:** Balance and strength training programs, home safety modifications, medication review (particularly sedatives and antihypertensives), vision correction, hip protectors
- **Violence:** Community violence intervention programs, gun safety legislation, substance abuse treatment
- **Sports/recreation:** Rule changes (e.g., spearing penalties in football), protective equipment, water safety education (the "Feet First, First Time" diving safety campaign), helmet legislation
- **Occupational:** Fall protection regulations (OSHA), workplace safety training

### 2.13.2 Impact of Prevention

Prevention efforts have had measurable effects on SCI epidemiology. Seatbelt and airbag legislation contributed to the decline in MVC-related SCI. Community violence prevention programs and declining crime rates are associated with the drop in violence-related SCI from its 1990s peak. Diving safety campaigns have reduced sport-related cervical SCI. However, the rise in fall-related SCI in the elderly population represents an ongoing challenge requiring targeted geriatric fall prevention programs.

---

## Summary

Traumatic SCI in the United States has an incidence of approximately 54 per million per year (approximately 17,700-18,000 cases annually) with a prevalence of 282,000-308,000 persons. The demographics have shifted toward older age at injury (mean 44 years, up from 29 in the 1970s) with a persistent 4:1 male predominance. Motor vehicle crashes remain the leading cause (38%), but falls have risen to second (31%) and are now the leading cause in persons over 45. Violence-related SCI peaked in the 1990s and has declined. Incomplete tetraplegia is now the most common category at discharge (47%), reflecting the aging population. Life expectancy is significantly reduced relative to the general population, with losses proportional to injury severity. Respiratory disease has replaced genitourinary disease as the leading cause of death. Suicide risk is 3 times the general population. Education is the strongest predictor of return to employment. Lifetime costs range from approximately $1.8 million for motor-functional injuries to over $5.1 million for high tetraplegia at age 25.
