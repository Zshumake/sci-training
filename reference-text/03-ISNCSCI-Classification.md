# Chapter 3: International Standards for Neurological Classification of Spinal Cord Injury (ISNCSCI)

## Learning Objectives

1. Perform a complete ISNCSCI neurological examination including sensory, motor, and anorectal assessments
2. Define and correctly use ISNCSCI terminology (tetraplegia, paraplegia, dermatome, myotome, neurological level of injury)
3. Identify all 28 key sensory dermatomes and their anatomic landmarks bilaterally
4. Test the 10 key myotomes, assign MRC grades, and calculate motor index scores
5. Determine sensory, motor, and neurological levels of injury
6. Apply the AIS classification algorithm to assign impairment grades A through E
7. Define zone of partial preservation and its clinical significance
8. Describe the incomplete SCI syndromes, their mechanisms, and prognoses
9. Differentiate conus medullaris from cauda equina syndrome
10. Apply prognostic data based on AIS grade to counsel patients and families

## 3.1 Historical Background and Terminology [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 3.1.1 Evolution of the Standards

The classification of SCI has undergone substantial revision since the initial Frankel scale introduced in 1969. The American Spinal Injury Association (ASIA) published the first International Standards for Neurological Classification of Spinal Cord Injury in 1982, with subsequent major revisions in 1992, 1996, 2000, 2006, 2011, and most recently 2019. Each revision has refined the examination procedures, classification rules, and taxonomy to improve reliability and clinical utility.

The 2019 revision (eighth edition) introduced several important changes, including refined zone of partial preservation (ZPP) rules, updated guidance for classifying injuries complicated by non-SCI conditions, and inclusion of a taxonomic designation for non-traumatic SCI. The ISNCSCI worksheet, endorsed jointly by ASIA and the International Spinal Cord Society (ISCoS), remains the universally accepted standard for SCI classification.

### 3.1.2 Key Terminology Changes

In 1992, the term **tetraplegia** officially replaced **quadriplegia** to maintain etymological consistency (both Greek-derived roots versus mixed Greek-Latin). Tetraplegia refers to impairment or loss of motor and/or sensory function in the cervical segments due to damage to neural elements within the spinal canal, resulting in functional impairment in the arms, trunk, legs, and pelvic organs. Paraplegia refers to impairment or loss of motor and/or sensory function in thoracic, lumbar, or sacral segments due to damage to neural elements within the spinal canal. Paraplegia, importantly, includes conus medullaris and cauda equina injuries but not brachial plexus or peripheral nerve injuries outside the spinal canal.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Tetraplegia replaced quadriplegia in 1992 in the ISNCSCI standards. However, the term "quadriplegia" persists in lay usage and some medical literature. Board examinations will use "tetraplegia" exclusively.

### 3.1.3 Essential Definitions

- **Dermatome:** The area of skin innervated by the sensory axons within a single segmental spinal nerve (dorsal root)
- **Myotome:** The collection of muscle fibers innervated by the motor axons within a single segmental spinal nerve (ventral root)
- **Neurological level of injury (NLI):** The most caudal segment of the spinal cord with normal sensory and antigravity (3/5) motor function on both sides, provided there is normal (5/5) sensory and motor function at all rostral segments
- **Skeletal level:** The level of greatest vertebral damage on radiographic imaging (not synonymous with neurological level)
- **Complete injury:** Absence of sensory and motor function in the lowest sacral segments (S4-S5) -- defined by the absence of sacral sparing
- **Incomplete injury:** Preservation of sensory and/or motor function below the neurological level that includes the lowest sacral segments (S4-S5) -- defined by the presence of sacral sparing
- **Zone of partial preservation (ZPP):** Used only in AIS A (complete) injuries; refers to all segments below the NLI that retain some sensory or motor function

## 3.2 The ISNCSCI Examination: Overview [BOARD]

### 3.2.1 Eight Steps of the ISNCSCI Examination

The complete ISNCSCI examination follows a systematic eight-step process:

1. **Sensory examination** -- Test light touch and pin prick at all 28 key sensory dermatomes bilaterally (112 individual tests per modality)
2. **Motor examination** -- Test the 10 key muscle groups bilaterally using manual muscle testing (MRC scale 0-5)
3. **Determine sensory levels** -- Right and left sensory levels
4. **Determine motor levels** -- Right and left motor levels
5. **Determine the neurological level of injury (NLI)** -- Most rostral abnormal level across all four modalities
6. **Determine completeness** -- Assess deep anal pressure (DAP) and voluntary anal contraction (VAC); determine whether the injury is complete or incomplete based on sacral sparing
7. **Assign the AIS grade** -- Apply the classification algorithm (A through E)
8. **Determine zone of partial preservation** -- For AIS A injuries only

---

## 3.3 Sensory Examination [BOARD]

### 3.3.1 Methodology

Two sensory modalities are tested at each key point:

- **Light touch (LT):** Tested using the tip of a cotton-tipped applicator stroked lightly across the skin (less than 1 cm length). The examiner should stroke, not press, and avoid testing hairy areas where possible.
- **Pin prick (PP):** Tested using a disposable safety pin, distinguishing sharp from dull. The pin should not be reused between patients. The patient is asked to discriminate sharp from dull (not simply whether they feel it).

Each modality is scored at each key sensory point on a 3-point scale:
- **0 = Absent:** No sensation perceived
- **1 = Altered:** Impaired or abnormal sensation (includes hyperesthesia, hypoesthesia, and allodynia)
- **2 = Normal:** Normal sensation, equivalent to the reference point
- **NT = Not testable:** Used when the area cannot be tested (e.g., cast, wound, amputation)

The **face (cheek)** serves as the normal reference point. The patient is first tested on the cheek to establish what "normal" (score of 2) feels like, and all subsequent points are compared to this reference.

### 3.3.3 Maximum Sensory Scores

- Light touch: Maximum 56 per side (28 dermatomes x 2 points each), total maximum 112
- Pin prick: Maximum 56 per side, total maximum 112
- Total sensory score maximum: 224

### 3.3.4 Sensory Level Determination

The sensory level on each side is the most caudal dermatome with normal (grade 2) appreciation of both light touch AND pin prick, provided that all dermatomes above that level are also graded as normal. If the most caudal normal level is different for light touch and pin prick, the sensory level is the most rostral of the two.

## 3.4 Motor Examination [BOARD]

### 3.4.1 Key Myotomes and Index Muscles

Ten key muscle groups are tested bilaterally (five upper extremity, five lower extremity), with each muscle assigned to a single root level:

| Myotome | Index Muscle | Action Tested | Positioning for Testing |
|---------|-------------|---------------|----------------------|
| C5 | Biceps brachii (and brachialis) | Elbow flexion | Forearm supinated, elbow partially flexed |
| C6 | Extensor carpi radialis longus and brevis | Wrist extension | Wrist in neutral, forearm pronated |
| C7 | Triceps brachii | Elbow extension | Shoulder forward flexed 90 degrees, elbow flexed |
| C8 | Flexor digitorum profundus | Finger flexion (DIP of middle finger) | Hand flat, test DIP flexion of the middle finger |
| T1 | Abductor digiti minimi | Small finger abduction | Hand flat, palm down, abduct 5th digit |
| L2 | Iliopsoas | Hip flexion | Seated or supine, hip flexed past 90 degrees |
| L3 | Quadriceps femoris | Knee extension | Seated with legs over edge of bed |
| L4 | Tibialis anterior | Ankle dorsiflexion | Foot in neutral or slight plantar flexion |
| L5 | Extensor hallucis longus | Great toe extension | Foot in neutral, extend great toe at MTP |
| S1 | Gastrocnemius/soleus | Ankle plantar flexion | Foot in neutral or slight dorsiflexion |

**Table 3-2. Ten Key Myotomes and Index Muscles**

> **Clinical Pearl:** The C8 key muscle tests the flexor digitorum profundus to the MIDDLE finger (not the ring or little finger). This is a commonly tested distinction on board examinations.

### 3.4.2 Manual Muscle Testing (MRC Grading)

The Medical Research Council (MRC) scale is used for grading:

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No visible or palpable muscle contraction (total paralysis) |
| 1 | Palpable or visible contraction without joint movement |
| 2 | Full range of motion with gravity eliminated |
| 3 | Full range of motion against gravity |
| 4 | Active movement against gravity with moderate resistance |
| 5 | Normal strength (active movement against full resistance) |
| 5* | Normal strength but examiner suspects weakness (e.g., due to pain, disuse) |
| NT | Not testable (e.g., due to immobilization, contracture, severe pain) |

**Table 3-3. MRC Muscle Grading Scale**

Key testing principles:
- Each muscle group must be tested through its full available range of motion
- The patient's position must allow gravity-eliminated versus against-gravity testing
- A grade of 3 represents the critical threshold (antigravity) used for motor level determination
- Grades 4- and 4+ are sometimes used clinically but are NOT part of the formal ISNCSCI scoring
- A muscle rated 5* is scored as 5 for ISNCSCI calculations

### 3.4.3 Motor Index Score

The motor index score (MIS) provides a quantitative summary of motor function:

- **Upper Extremity Motor Score (UEMS):** Sum of MRC grades for C5-T1 bilaterally. Maximum = 50 (5 muscles x 2 sides x 5 points maximum)
- **Lower Extremity Motor Score (LEMS):** Sum of MRC grades for L2-S1 bilaterally. Maximum = 50
- **Total Motor Index Score:** UEMS + LEMS. Maximum = 100

For thoracic myotomes (T2-L1), no key muscles are designated. These segments are assumed to have a motor grade of 5 if the sensory score at that level is normal and the key muscles above and below are grade 5.

### 3.4.4 Motor Level Determination

The motor level on each side is determined by the most caudal key muscle that has a grade of at least 3/5, provided that the key muscle represented by the segment immediately above is graded at 5/5 (full strength). If the segment above is not 5/5, then the motor level moves rostrally until a segment with 5/5 strength is found with the next caudal segment at 3/5 or better.

**Special rules for motor level:**
- In segments without testable key muscles (C1-C4, T2-L1, S2-S5), the motor level is presumed to follow the sensory level if the key muscles rostral and caudal to the segment are intact
- The motor level cannot be more caudal than the most caudal segment with 5/5 strength when the next caudal key muscle is less than 3/5

> **Clinical Pearl:** The motor level rule requires grade 3/5 or greater with the segment ABOVE graded 5/5. For example, if C5 is 4/5 and C6 is 3/5, the motor level is C5 (not C6), because the segment above C6 (namely C5) is not 5/5. If C5 is 5/5 and C6 is 3/5, then the motor level IS C6.

## 3.5 Anorectal Examination [BOARD]

### 3.5.1 Deep Anal Pressure (DAP)

Deep anal pressure is tested by the examiner inserting a gloved, lubricated finger into the rectum and applying firm pressure against the rectal wall. The patient is asked whether they perceive the pressure. DAP tests the sensory innervation of the S4-S5 segments.

- Present DAP indicates sacral sensory sparing
- DAP contributes to the determination of complete versus incomplete injury

### 3.5.2 Voluntary Anal Contraction (VAC)

The examiner maintains the finger in the rectum and asks the patient to voluntarily squeeze the examiner's finger. It is important to distinguish voluntary contraction from reflex contractions (such as the bulbocavernosus reflex or rectal contractions from Valsalva).

- **Present VAC** indicates motor function preservation at S4-S5 and confirms the injury is at minimum AIS C (motor incomplete)
- VAC is the single most important determinant in distinguishing AIS B from AIS C

> **Clinical Pearl:** The presence of voluntary anal contraction (VAC) automatically classifies the patient as at minimum AIS C (motor incomplete), regardless of all other motor and sensory findings. This is because VAC represents motor function in the lowest sacral segments, fulfilling the criteria for motor preservation below the NLI in the sacral segments.

---

## 3.6 Non-Key Muscle Groups [BOARD]

When all 10 key muscles below the NLI have a grade of 0/5 but VAC is present (motor function preserved only at S4-S5), non-key muscles become important for differentiating AIS C from AIS B. The following non-key muscles are evaluated when motor function below the NLI needs to be assessed:

| Segment | Non-Key Muscle Function |
|---------|------------------------|
| C5 | Shoulder abduction (deltoid), elbow flexion (biceps, brachialis) |
| C6 | Forearm supination (supinator, biceps), wrist extension |
| C7 | Wrist flexion (flexor carpi radialis) |
| C8 | Finger extension (extensor digitorum), thumb extension |
| T1 | Finger adduction (palmar interossei), thumb opposition |
| L2 | Hip adduction (adductors) |
| L3 | Hip extension/abduction (gluteus medius) |
| L4 | Knee flexion (hamstrings) |
| L5 | Ankle eversion (peroneus longus), ankle inversion (tibialis posterior) |
| S1 | Toe flexion (flexor hallucis longus), hip extension (gluteus maximus) |

**Table 3-4. Non-Key Muscle Functions by Segment**

Non-key muscles are graded as follows for the purpose of AIS classification:
- Motor function present (any palpable or visible contraction = grade 1 or higher)
- Motor function absent (no contraction = grade 0)

Non-key muscles are assessed when needed to determine whether more than half of the key muscles (and non-key muscles) below the NLI function at grade 3 or above, which distinguishes AIS C from AIS D.

## 3.7 Neurological Level of Injury (NLI) [BOARD]

The NLI is defined as the most rostral of the four levels determined on each side:
1. Right sensory level
2. Left sensory level
3. Right motor level
4. Left motor level

The NLI is the single most cephalad of these four values. For example, if the right sensory level is C6, left sensory level is C5, right motor level is C6, and left motor level is C7, the NLI is C5.

The NLI determines the classification as tetraplegia (NLI at C1-C8) or paraplegia (NLI at T1 or below).

---

## 3.8 Sacral Sparing and Completeness [BOARD]

### 3.8.1 Definition of Sacral Sparing

Sacral sparing is the key concept that differentiates complete from incomplete SCI. An injury is classified as **incomplete** if ANY of the following are present at S4-S5:

- Sensation (light touch or pin prick) at the S4-S5 dermatome
- Deep anal pressure (DAP)
- Voluntary anal contraction (VAC)

An injury is classified as **complete** (AIS A) if ALL sacral sparing is absent at S4-S5:
- No sensation at S4-S5
- No DAP
- No VAC

> **Clinical Pearl:** Sacral sparing -- not the NLI, not the extent of injury, and not functional capacity -- defines complete versus incomplete SCI. A patient with no motor or sensory function from C5 through S3 but who has DAP would be classified as incomplete, while a patient with preserved function at multiple levels below the NLI but no sacral sparing would be classified as AIS A (complete).

### 3.8.2 Anatomic Basis of Sacral Sparing

The anatomic basis for sacral sparing in incomplete injuries reflects the laminated organization of the spinal cord tracts. Sacral fibers in the spinothalamic tract are located most laterally (peripherally) and sacral fibers in the corticospinal tract are also most lateral. Therefore, lesions that spare the peripheral rim of the cord (such as central cord lesions) may preserve sacral function even when more rostrally represented segments are impaired.

## 3.9 ASIA Impairment Scale (AIS) Classification Algorithm [BOARD]

### 3.9.1 AIS Grades

| AIS Grade | Designation | Definition |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| A | Complete | No sensory or motor function preserved in sacral segments S4-S5 |
| B | Sensory incomplete | Sensory but NOT motor function preserved below the NLI, including S4-S5. No motor function preserved more than three levels below the motor level on either side |
| C | Motor incomplete | Motor function preserved below the NLI AND more than half of key muscles below the single NLI have a muscle grade less than 3 |
| D | Motor incomplete | Motor function preserved below the NLI AND at least half of key muscles below the single NLI have a muscle grade of 3 or greater |
| E | Normal | Sensory and motor function are normal in all segments (patient had prior deficits) |

**Table 3-5. ASIA Impairment Scale**

### 3.9.2 Step-by-Step AIS Classification Algorithm

**Step 1: Is the injury complete?**
- Check for sacral sparing (LT/PP at S4-S5, DAP, VAC)
- If ALL absent at S4-S5: AIS A (Complete). Proceed to determine ZPP.
- If ANY present at S4-S5: Incomplete. Proceed to Step 2.

**Step 2: Is there motor function below the NLI?**
- Determine if there is voluntary motor function preserved in key or non-key muscles more than 3 levels below the motor level on either side
- If YES (motor function below the NLI): AIS C or D. Proceed to Step 3.
- If NO (sensory only below the NLI): AIS B (Sensory Incomplete)

**Special consideration for AIS B vs C:** If VAC is present but no key or non-key motor function is preserved more than 3 levels below the motor level on either side, the injury is classified as AIS C (because VAC represents motor function in the sacral segments).

**Step 3: Determine AIS C versus D**
- Count all key muscles below the single NLI that are testable (not NT)
- If at least half of these muscles have a grade of 3 or greater: AIS D
- If more than half have a grade less than 3: AIS C

**Step 4: AIS E determination**
- If sensory and motor function are graded as normal in all segments AND the patient previously had documented neurologic deficits attributable to SCI: AIS E
- A patient with no prior SCI history should not receive AIS E

> **Clinical Pearl:** The AIS B versus C distinction hinges on the presence of MOTOR function below the NLI. If only sensory function is preserved below the NLI (plus DAP), the classification is AIS B. If VAC is present, the classification is at minimum AIS C, because VAC represents motor function at S4-S5. The presence of VAC alone, even without any other motor function below the NLI, makes the injury AIS C.

## 3.10 Zone of Partial Preservation (ZPP) [BOARD]

### 3.10.1 Definition

The zone of partial preservation (ZPP) applies ONLY to AIS A (complete) injuries. It is defined as all segments below the NLI that have partially preserved sensory or motor function. Four ZPPs are recorded:
1. Right sensory ZPP
2. Left sensory ZPP
3. Right motor ZPP
4. Left motor ZPP

Each ZPP is recorded as the most caudal segment with some preserved function (sensory score of 1, or motor grade of 1 or higher) on that side.

### 3.10.2 Clinical Significance

The ZPP has both prognostic and documentation significance. Patients with more caudal ZPPs and greater preserved function within the ZPP tend to have higher rates of neurologic recovery. The ZPP should not be confused with the NLI; it applies only below the NLI in complete injuries.

> **Clinical Pearl:** ZPP is documented only for AIS A (complete) injuries. It is NOT used for AIS B, C, or D injuries (in incomplete injuries, the preserved function below the NLI is already captured by the AIS grade itself). The 2019 revision expanded the ZPP definition so that motor ZPP can also be documented in incomplete injuries when VAC is absent, though sensory ZPP still applies only to AIS A.

## 3.11 Prognosis by AIS Grade [BOARD]

### 3.11.1 Conversion Rates

The initial AIS grade is the single most important prognostic indicator for neurologic recovery:

| Initial AIS Grade | Conversion Rate (to Lower Grade) | Details |
|-------------------|---------------------------------|---------|
| A (Complete) | 10-15% convert to incomplete | More favorable if pin prick preserved in ZPP; conversion to C or D rare (~5-8%); higher conversion in tetraplegia vs paraplegia |
| B (Sensory Incomplete) | ~30% convert to motor incomplete (C or D) | Pin prick preservation predicts motor recovery better than light touch alone |
| C (Motor Incomplete) | 65-75% convert to D or E | Most achieve functional ambulation with rehabilitation |
| D (Motor Incomplete) | Best overall prognosis | ~75-85% achieve community ambulation |

**Table 3-6. Neurologic Conversion Rates by AIS Grade**

### 3.11.2 Pin Prick Versus Light Touch for Prognosis

Pin prick preservation below the NLI is a stronger predictor of motor recovery than light touch alone. This reflects the anatomic proximity of the lateral spinothalamic tract (carrying pin prick) to the lateral corticospinal tract in the lateral funiculus. Preservation of pin prick suggests that the lateral cord is partially intact, predicting a higher likelihood of lateral CST preservation and motor recovery.

Conversely, preservation of light touch alone (without pin prick) in AIS B injuries suggests preservation of the anterior spinothalamic tract (anterior funiculus) or dorsal columns, which are anatomically distant from the corticospinal tract and thus less predictive of motor recovery.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In AIS B injuries, preservation of PIN PRICK below the NLI predicts a significantly better motor recovery than preservation of LIGHT TOUCH alone. AIS B with pin prick preservation converts to motor incomplete (AIS C or D) in approximately 50-70% of cases, whereas AIS B with light touch only converts in approximately 10-20%.

## 3.12 Incomplete SCI Syndromes [BOARD]

### 3.12.1 Central Cord Syndrome (CCS)

**Epidemiology:** CCS is the most common incomplete SCI syndrome, accounting for approximately 50% of all incomplete injuries and approximately 9% of all traumatic SCI cases.

**Mechanism:** Typically results from hyperextension injury in elderly patients with pre-existing cervical spondylosis and spinal canal stenosis. The stenotic canal is further compromised during hyperextension by infolding of the hypertrophied ligamentum flavum anteriorly, producing cord compression. In younger patients, CCS may result from more forceful injuries (hyperextension with or without fracture).

**Pathophysiology:** The central gray matter and the innermost (medially located) white matter fibers are most vulnerable. Because corticospinal tract fibers to the upper extremities are more medially located than those to the lower extremities, and because the gray matter of the cervical enlargement (with its high density of motor neurons for the hand and arm) is most affected, upper extremity weakness predominates.

**Clinical Features:**
- Upper extremity weakness GREATER than lower extremity weakness (the hallmark)
- Variable sensory loss below the level (often a "cape-like" distribution)
- Bladder dysfunction (urinary retention) is common
- Burning dysesthesias in the upper extremities are frequent

**Recovery Pattern (in typical order):**
1. Lower extremity motor function recovers first
2. Bowel and bladder function
3. Proximal upper extremity motor function
4. Distal upper extremity (hand) motor function recovers LAST and often remains most impaired

**Prognosis:**
- Age less than 50: Better prognosis; majority achieve functional ambulation
- Age over 50: More guarded prognosis; hand function often remains significantly impaired
- Overall, approximately 50-75% achieve functional independence with ambulation
- Hand function recovery is the weakest area, especially fine motor control

> **Clinical Pearl:** In central cord syndrome, the recovery pattern follows a specific order: lower extremities first, then bowel/bladder, then proximal upper extremities, and finally the hands. Hand intrinsic function is the LAST to recover and often remains the most impaired. This recovery pattern is a high-yield board topic.

### 3.12.2 Brown-Sequard Syndrome (BSS)

**Epidemiology:** Accounts for 2-4% of all traumatic SCI.

**Mechanism:** Results from hemisection of the spinal cord. Pure hemisection is rare; most cases result from penetrating trauma (stab wounds, GSW) or, less commonly, from lateral mass fractures, disc herniations, or tumors producing asymmetric cord compression.

**Clinical Features (classic presentation):**
- **Ipsilateral to the lesion:**
  - Motor loss (lateral corticospinal tract): UMN-type weakness ipsilateral to the lesion, below the level
  - Proprioception and vibration loss (dorsal columns): Ipsilateral loss below the level
  - LMN signs at the level of the lesion (segmental anterior horn cell damage)
- **Contralateral to the lesion:**
  - Pain and temperature loss (lateral spinothalamic tract): Contralateral loss beginning 1-2 dermatomes BELOW the level of the lesion (because spinothalamic fibers cross over 1-2 segments above their level of entry)

**Recovery Pattern:**
- Ipsilateral proximal extensors recover first
- Then ipsilateral distal flexors
- Contralateral spinothalamic function recovers last

**Prognosis:**
- Best prognosis for ambulation among the incomplete syndromes
- 75-90% of patients achieve functional ambulation
- Motor recovery is typically good

> **Clinical Pearl:** Brown-Sequard syndrome produces ipsilateral motor and proprioception loss with contralateral pain/temperature loss beginning 1-2 levels BELOW the lesion. It has the best prognosis for ambulation of all incomplete SCI syndromes (75-90% ambulate).

### 3.12.3 Anterior Cord Syndrome (ACS)

**Epidemiology:** The third most common incomplete SCI syndrome.

**Mechanism:** Results from damage to the anterior two-thirds of the spinal cord, typically from:
- Anterior spinal artery occlusion (vascular, aortic surgery, dissection)
- Flexion injuries with retropulsed bone/disc fragments compressing the anterior cord
- Burst fractures with posterior fragment displacement

**Clinical Features:**
- Motor paralysis below the level (lateral corticospinal tract involvement)
- Loss of pain and temperature sensation below the level (spinothalamic tract involvement)
- PRESERVED proprioception and vibration (dorsal columns intact, supplied by posterior spinal arteries)
- Variable bowel/bladder involvement

**Prognosis:**
- Worst prognosis of all incomplete SCI syndromes
- Only 10-20% achieve functional motor recovery
- Prognosis for ambulation is poor

> **Clinical Pearl:** Anterior cord syndrome has the WORST prognosis of all incomplete SCI syndromes, with only 10-20% achieving functional motor recovery. This contrasts sharply with Brown-Sequard syndrome, which has the BEST prognosis (75-90% ambulatory).

### 3.12.4 Posterior Cord Syndrome (PCS)

**Epidemiology:** The rarest of the classical incomplete SCI syndromes, accounting for less than 1% of traumatic SCI. Due to its rarity, it was omitted from recent ISNCSCI standards documentation.

**Mechanism:** Results from damage to the posterior one-third of the spinal cord (dorsal columns). Causes include:
- Posterior spinal artery occlusion (rare)
- Vitamin B12 deficiency (subacute combined degeneration)
- Tabes dorsalis (tertiary syphilis)
- Multiple sclerosis (posterior plaque)
- Hyperextension injuries with posterior element fractures

**Clinical Features:**
- Loss of proprioception and vibration below the level
- Preserved motor function
- Preserved pain and temperature sensation
- Gait ataxia (sensory ataxia) due to loss of proprioceptive feedback
- Positive Romberg sign

**Prognosis:**
- Motor function generally preserved
- Functional prognosis depends on the degree of proprioceptive loss and compensatory strategies

## 3.13 Conus Medullaris Versus Cauda Equina Syndrome [BOARD]

### 3.13.1 Epiconus, Conus Medullaris, and Cauda Equina

Understanding the distinction between epiconus, conus medullaris, and cauda equina lesions requires knowledge of the relevant neuroanatomy:

- **Epiconus:** Spinal cord segments L4-S1 (located at approximately the T11-T12 vertebral level). Contains lower motor neuron pools for the lower extremities.
- **Conus medullaris:** Spinal cord segments S2-S5 (located at approximately the L1-L2 vertebral level). Contains sacral parasympathetic neurons, Onuf's nucleus, and the terminal segments of the cord.
- **Cauda equina:** The collection of spinal nerve roots (L2-Co1) descending within the lumbar cistern below the conus medullaris (below approximately the L1-L2 vertebral level).

### 3.13.2 Comparison Table

| Feature | Epiconus (L4-S1) | Conus Medullaris (S2-S5) | Cauda Equina (below L1-L2) |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Vertebral level | T11-T12 | L1-L2 | Below L1-L2 |
| Neuron type | UMN (cord) + LMN at level | UMN + LMN (mixed) | LMN only (nerve roots) |
| Motor deficit | LE weakness (UMN type below, LMN at level) | Typically mild motor involvement; perineal muscles affected | LE weakness (LMN type); asymmetric; often radicular |
| Sensory deficit | Saddle anesthesia + LE dermatomes | Saddle (perianal) anesthesia; symmetric | Saddle + LE dermatomes; asymmetric; often radicular |
| Reflexes | UMN signs below lesion; absent at level | BCR may be preserved or absent; mixed UMN/LMN | Areflexia (LMN); ankle jerks absent |
| Bladder involvement | Early; upper motor neuron bladder | EARLY; LMN (areflexic/flaccid) bladder | LATE; LMN bladder when it occurs |
| Bowel involvement | Early | EARLY; loss of voluntary control; flaccid sphincter | LATE |
| Pain | Less prominent | Less prominent; perianal | PROMINENT; severe, radicular, shooting |
| Onset symmetry | Symmetric | Symmetric | ASYMMETRIC |
| Sexual dysfunction | Present | Present (erectile dysfunction, anorgasmia) | Variable; often asymmetric |
| EMG findings | Denervation in myotomes L4-S1; UMN pattern below | Denervation in S2-S5 myotomes; perineal muscles | Denervation in affected root distribution; fibrillations |
| Recovery potential | Variable; limited for cord segments | Limited (cord damage) | BETTER (nerve roots can regenerate if decompressed early) |

**Table 3-8. Epiconus vs. Conus Medullaris vs. Cauda Equina Syndrome**

### 3.13.3 Key Distinguishing Features

The most important clinical distinctions are:

1. **Bladder timing:** Conus medullaris lesions produce EARLY bladder dysfunction (often the presenting complaint), whereas cauda equina lesions produce LATE bladder involvement
2. **Pain:** Cauda equina syndrome typically produces severe radicular pain, while conus medullaris syndrome is associated with less prominent pain
3. **Symmetry:** Conus medullaris produces symmetric deficits; cauda equina produces asymmetric, radicular patterns
4. **Neuron type:** Conus = mixed UMN/LMN; cauda equina = pure LMN
5. **Recovery:** Cauda equina has better recovery potential because peripheral nerve roots can regenerate, whereas damaged spinal cord tissue in the conus does not

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key distinguishing feature between conus and cauda equina is the timing of bladder dysfunction: EARLY in conus (often the presenting symptom), LATE in cauda equina. Pain is typically PROMINENT and radicular in cauda equina but less marked in conus. Cauda equina injuries are asymmetric (LMN), while conus injuries are symmetric. Cauda equina injuries have better recovery potential because nerve roots are PNS and can regenerate.

## 3.14 Complex Classification Scenarios [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 3.14.1 Non-SCI Conditions Affecting Classification

The 2019 ISNCSCI revision provided expanded guidance for handling pre-existing or concomitant conditions that affect the neurological examination. These include:

- **Pre-existing peripheral neuropathy:** May produce baseline sensory or motor deficits unrelated to SCI. Pre-existing deficits should be documented and accounted for when determining levels and AIS grade.
- **Traumatic brain injury:** Cognitive impairment may render the patient unable to cooperate with the examination. The "not testable" (NT) designation should be used, and the injury may be classified as "unable to determine."
- **Amputations:** Missing limbs cannot be tested. Motor and sensory scores for amputated segments are recorded as NT. Classification proceeds using available data.
- **Fractures and immobilization:** Segments immobilized in casts or external fixation cannot undergo manual muscle testing and are recorded as NT.
- **Chronic pain:** Severe pain may limit the patient's effort during motor testing. The 5* designation may be used if the examiner suspects that apparent weakness is due to pain rather than true neurological deficit.

### 3.14.2 Worksheet Interpretation Nuances

Several common classification pitfalls deserve emphasis:

- **Motor level in non-key muscle segments (T2-L1, S2-S5):** When the sensory level falls within a segment that lacks key muscles, the motor level is assumed to follow the sensory level if the key muscles immediately above and below are intact.
- **Asymmetric injuries:** The NLI is determined separately for each side, and the single NLI is the most rostral of the four levels. The AIS grade is determined using the single NLI.
- **NT segments:** When multiple segments are NT, classification may be "unable to determine." The examiner should attempt to test as many segments as possible and record the reason for NT.
- **AIS B versus C with VAC only:** When VAC is the only motor function below the NLI, the classification is AIS C (not B), because VAC represents motor function at S4-S5.

### 3.14.3 Timing of Examination

The initial ISNCSCI examination should ideally be performed within 72 hours of injury, after resolution of spinal shock is beginning but before significant recovery has occurred. However, the examination is valid at any time point and serial examinations are recommended to track recovery. The classification at one year post-injury is typically used as the benchmark for outcome reporting in clinical trials.

### 3.15.2 ASIA Autonomic Standards

In parallel with the ISNCSCI motor/sensory standards, the International Standards to Document Remaining Autonomic Function after Spinal Cord Injury (ISAFSCI) were developed and revised (most recently 2012). These document autonomic function in the domains of cardiovascular, respiratory, sudomotor, bladder, bowel, and sexual function. While not part of the AIS classification, autonomic function documentation provides important prognostic and management information.

## Summary

The ISNCSCI examination is the gold standard for classifying SCI severity and predicting recovery. It comprises a systematic sensory examination of 28 key dermatomes bilaterally (light touch and pin prick), motor examination of 10 key myotomes bilaterally (MRC grading), and anorectal examination (DAP and VAC). Sacral sparing at S4-S5 defines the complete/incomplete distinction, and the AIS grade (A through E) is determined by a stepwise algorithm. VAC presence automatically classifies the injury as at minimum AIS C. The motor index score maximum is 100 (UEMS 50 + LEMS 50). Pin prick preservation is a stronger predictor of motor recovery than light touch. Among incomplete syndromes, central cord syndrome is the most common (UE > LE weakness, recovery begins in LE and ends in hands), Brown-Sequard has the best prognosis for ambulation (75-90%), and anterior cord syndrome has the worst prognosis (10-20% motor recovery). Conus medullaris lesions cause early symmetric bladder dysfunction (UMN/LMN), while cauda equina produces late asymmetric deficits with prominent radicular pain (LMN) and better recovery potential.
