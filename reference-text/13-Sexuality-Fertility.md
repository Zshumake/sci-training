## 13.1 Introduction

**[BOARD]**

Sexual function, intimacy, and reproductive health are central concerns for individuals with spinal cord injury. Surveys consistently demonstrate that restoration of sexual function ranks among the highest priorities for persons with SCI -- frequently rated as more important than recovery of walking by individuals with paraplegia and as important as hand function by those with tetraplegia.

Despite its importance, sexuality after SCI has historically received inadequate attention in clinical education and rehabilitation practice. Many individuals with SCI report never having received comprehensive sexual health counseling from their rehabilitation team. This chapter provides a detailed review of the neurophysiology of sexual function, the specific effects of SCI on male and female sexual response, evidence-based treatment of sexual dysfunction, and fertility management.

## 13.2 Neuroanatomy of Sexual Function

**[BOARD]**

Understanding the neural control of sexual function is essential for predicting which aspects of sexual response will be preserved or impaired after SCI at a given level and completeness.

### Parasympathetic Pathways (S2-S4): "Point"

The parasympathetic sacral erection center resides in the intermediolateral cell column of the sacral spinal cord segments S2 through S4. Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers travel via the pelvic nerve (nervi erigentes) to the pelvic plexus and then to the cavernous nerves innervating the corpora cavernosa.

**Mechanism of erection:**
Parasympathetic activation stimulates release of nitric oxide (NO) from non-adrenergic non-cholinergic (NANC) nerve terminals and endothelial cells in the corpora cavernosa. NO activates guanylate cyclase, increasing intracellular cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP), which causes smooth muscle relaxation in the trabecular arteries and sinusoidal spaces of the corpora cavernosa. This smooth muscle relaxation leads to increased arterial inflow, sinusoidal engorgement, and venous outflow restriction (the veno-occlusive mechanism), producing penile erection.

### Sympathetic Pathways (T11-L2): "Shoot"

The sympathetic thoracolumbar center is located in the intermediolateral cell column of spinal cord segments T11 through L2. Preganglionic sympathetic fibers exit the spinal cord via the ventral roots, synapse in the sympathetic chain or prevertebral ganglia, and postganglionic fibers travel via the hypogastric nerve to the pelvic organs.

**Mechanism of ejaculation:**
Sympathetic activation via the hypogastric nerve mediates:
- Contraction of the vas deferens and seminal vesicles (emission phase -- movement of seminal fluid into the posterior urethra)
- Closure of the bladder neck (internal sphincter) to prevent retrograde ejaculation
- Coordinated contraction of the bulbospongiosus and ischiocavernosus muscles (expulsion phase -- somatic pudendal nerve, S2-S4) to propel semen through the urethra

### Somatic Pathways (S2-S4): Pudendal Nerve

The pudendal nerve (S2-S4) provides sensory innervation to the genitalia and motor innervation to the bulbospongiosus and ischiocavernosus muscles. The pudendal nerve mediates:
- Genital sensation (critical for reflexogenic erection triggering)
- Bulbocavernosus reflex
- Expulsion phase of ejaculation
- Rhythmic contractions during orgasm

### The "Point and Shoot" Mnemonic

This high-yield mnemonic summarizes the autonomic innervation of male sexual function:

- **Parasympathetic = Point (Erection):** S2-S4 via pelvic nerve leads to nitric oxide release, smooth muscle relaxation, and penile engorgement
- **Sympathetic = Shoot (Ejaculation):** T11-L2 via hypogastric nerve leads to emission (vas deferens/seminal vesicle contraction plus bladder neck closure)

## 13.3 Male Erectile Function After SCI

**[BOARD]**

### Two Types of Erections

**Reflexogenic erections** are mediated by the sacral reflex arc (S2-S4). They are triggered by direct tactile stimulation of the genitalia, with sensory afferents traveling via the pudendal nerve to the sacral cord and efferent parasympathetic output traveling via the pelvic nerve to the cavernous nerves. Reflexogenic erections do not require supraspinal input or conscious awareness.

**Psychogenic erections** are mediated by the thoracolumbar sympathetic outflow (T11-L2), modulated by descending supraspinal pathways. They are triggered by erotic cognitive stimuli (visual, auditory, olfactory, fantasy) processed in the cerebral cortex, with descending pathways modulating the T11-L2 sympathetic center, which in turn influences sacral parasympathetic outflow. Psychogenic erections require intact descending pathways from the brain to the thoracolumbar spinal cord.

### Erection Preservation by Injury Type

**Table 13.1: Erectile Function by Injury Pattern**

| Injury Type | Reflexogenic Erection | Psychogenic Erection |
|---|---|---|
| Complete UMN (above sacral segments) | Present in >90% | NOT usually present (descending pathways interrupted) |
| Incomplete UMN | Present in >90% | Present in ~50% (some descending pathways preserved) |
| Complete LMN (sacral segments destroyed) | Present in ~25% (limited, incomplete) | Present in ~25% (via preserved T11-L2 outflow if descending pathways intact) |
| Incomplete LMN | Variable | Variable |

**Key concepts:**

- UMN lesions (injury above the conus medullaris) preserve the sacral reflex arc, maintaining reflexogenic erections in the vast majority of cases. However, the interruption of descending pathways prevents psychogenic erections in complete UMN injuries.
- LMN lesions (injury directly involving the sacral segments S2-S4 or the cauda equina) damage the sacral reflex arc, severely impairing reflexogenic erections. Psychogenic erections via T11-L2 outflow may be partially preserved if descending pathways from the brain to the thoracolumbar cord remain intact.
- Reflexogenic erections in UMN injuries may occur without conscious awareness and can be triggered by any tactile genital stimulation, including catheter insertion or perineal hygiene.
- Although reflexogenic erections are common in UMN injuries, they may be insufficient in rigidity or duration for satisfactory sexual intercourse, necessitating treatment.

## 13.4 Ejaculatory Function After SCI

**[BOARD]**

Ejaculation is significantly more impaired than erection after SCI. Ejaculation is primarily a sympathetic function requiring coordinated output from the thoracolumbar sympathetic center (T11-L2), integrated somatic output via the pudendal nerve (S2-S4), and intact descending pathways.

### Ejaculation Rates by Injury Type

- **Complete UMN injuries:** Only 5 to 15 percent can ejaculate
- **Complete LMN injuries:** Approximately 18 percent can ejaculate
- **Incomplete injuries (UMN and LMN):** Higher rates of ejaculation, though still substantially reduced compared to the able-bodied population. Incomplete injuries with preserved motor function have the best ejaculatory outcomes.

The profound impairment of ejaculation is a primary contributor to male infertility after SCI and drives the need for assisted ejaculation techniques for sperm retrieval.

## 13.5 Erectile Dysfunction Treatment

**[BOARD]**

### Phosphodiesterase Type 5 (PDE5) Inhibitors

PDE5 inhibitors are the first-line treatment for erectile dysfunction (ED) in SCI. These medications inhibit the breakdown of cGMP in the cavernous smooth muscle, prolonging and enhancing the erectile response to sexual stimulation.

**Available agents:**
- **Sildenafil (Viagra):** 25 to 100 mg taken approximately 1 hour before sexual activity. The most extensively studied PDE5 inhibitor in SCI, with randomized controlled trials demonstrating significant improvement in erectile function compared to placebo.
- **Tadalafil (Cialis):** 10 to 20 mg on demand or 2.5 to 5 mg daily. Longer duration of action (up to 36 hours) provides greater flexibility in timing of sexual activity.
- **Vardenafil (Levitra):** 5 to 20 mg taken approximately 1 hour before sexual activity. Similar efficacy and pharmacokinetics to sildenafil.

**SCI-specific considerations:**
- **Autonomic dysreflexia (AD):** PDE5 inhibitors are vasodilators and may lower blood pressure. In patients at risk for AD (injuries T6 and above), PDE5 inhibitors may theoretically mitigate AD episodes triggered during sexual activity. However, the concurrent use of PDE5 inhibitors and AD treatment medications (particularly nitrates or nitroprusside) is absolutely contraindicated due to the risk of severe, life-threatening hypotension.
- **Absolute contraindication:** Concurrent use of organic nitrates (nitroglycerin, isosorbide mononitrate/dinitrate) or nitric oxide donors. This includes nitrates that may be administered emergently for AD.
- PDE5 inhibitors require some degree of preserved erectile neural pathway (they enhance the response to sexual stimulation, not generate erection independently). Patients with complete LMN injuries and absent reflexogenic erections may have a reduced response.

### Intracavernosal Injections

For patients who fail or cannot tolerate PDE5 inhibitors, intracavernosal injection of vasoactive agents is a second-line option.

**Agents:**
- **Alprostadil (prostaglandin E1, PGE1):** Directly relaxes cavernous smooth muscle independent of neural pathway integrity. Available as intracavernosal injection (Caverject) or intraurethral suppository (MUSE).
- **Combination therapy (Trimix/Bimix):** Papaverine (non-selective phosphodiesterase inhibitor) + phentolamine (alpha-adrenergic blocker) +/- alprostadil. Combination therapy often allows lower doses of individual components, reducing side effects.

**Risks:**
- **Priapism** -- Prolonged erection lasting more than 4 hours is a urologic emergency. The risk of priapism is particularly concerning in SCI patients who may not perceive penile pain. Patients must be educated to seek immediate medical attention for erections lasting more than 4 hours. Treatment involves corporal aspiration and injection of a sympathomimetic agent (phenylephrine).
- Corporal fibrosis with repeated injections
- Hematoma at the injection site
- Injection site pain (may not be perceived in insensate patients)

### Penile Vacuum Erection Devices

Vacuum erection devices (VEDs) create negative pressure around the penis, drawing blood into the corpora cavernosa. A constriction ring is then placed at the base of the penis to maintain engorgement.

- Do not require intact neural pathways -- effective regardless of injury level or completeness
- Non-invasive and reusable
- The constriction ring should not be left in place for more than 30 minutes to prevent ischemic injury
- The erection produced is often described as less natural (cool, pivots at base, venous engorgement rather than arterial)
- In patients with insensate skin, careful inspection for skin breakdown from the constriction ring is essential

### Penile Implants (Prostheses)

Penile prostheses (inflatable or semi-rigid/malleable) are a last resort for ED management, reserved for patients who fail all other treatments.

- **Inflatable prostheses:** Consist of paired intracorporal cylinders, a scrotal pump, and an abdominal fluid reservoir. Provide the most natural erection appearance and flaccidity.
- **Semi-rigid (malleable) prostheses:** Paired bendable rods implanted in the corpora cavernosa. Simpler and less expensive but produce a permanent semi-rigid state.

**SCI-specific risk:** The risk of prosthesis erosion through insensate penile skin is significantly higher in SCI patients than in the general ED population. Patients cannot detect pressure or pain from the prosthesis, leading to potential unrecognized tissue breakdown and device extrusion. The risk of erosion must be carefully weighed against the benefits, and patients must be educated in regular visual inspection of the penis for signs of erosion.

## 13.6 Male Infertility After SCI

**[BOARD]**

Male infertility after SCI results from two distinct but often coexisting problems: ejaculatory dysfunction and poor semen quality.

### Ejaculatory Dysfunction

As discussed in Section 13.4, the majority of men with SCI cannot ejaculate during sexual intercourse. This fundamental barrier to natural conception necessitates assisted ejaculation techniques for sperm retrieval.

### Poor Semen Quality

Even when ejaculation is achieved (spontaneously or through assisted techniques), semen quality in men with SCI is typically severely impaired compared to able-bodied men. The characteristic semen profile in SCI includes:

- Normal or elevated sperm count (concentration)
- Severely reduced sperm motility (both progressive and total motility)
- Reduced sperm viability
- Increased semen volume with reduced fructose concentration
- Increased presence of inflammatory cells

**Causes of poor semen quality:**

1. **Prostatic fluid stasis** -- Infrequent ejaculation leads to stagnation of prostatic secretions, creating an unfavorable biochemical environment for spermatozoa within the reproductive tract.

2. **Testicular hyperthermia** -- SCI-related impairment of thermoregulation below the injury level may elevate scrotal temperature, adversely affecting spermatogenesis. Prolonged sitting in a wheelchair further contributes to testicular heat stress.

3. **Recurrent urinary tract infections** -- Chronic bacteriuria and recurrent UTIs associated with neurogenic bladder introduce bacteria and inflammatory mediators into the seminal fluid, damaging spermatozoa.

4. **Abnormal testicular histology** -- Testicular biopsies in men with SCI commonly reveal seminiferous tubule atrophy with Sertoli cell-only pattern or maturation arrest. Seminiferous tubule atrophy is the most common finding on testicular biopsy in men with SCI. The etiology is likely multifactorial, involving vascular compromise, hormonal changes, and oxidative stress.

5. **Hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular (HPT) axis changes** -- Some studies demonstrate alterations in gonadotropin and testosterone levels after SCI, though findings are inconsistent. FSH elevation may reflect primary testicular dysfunction.

6. **Sperm antibodies** -- Antisperm antibodies are found in a significant proportion of men with SCI, potentially impairing sperm motility and fertilization capacity.

7. **Medications** -- Anticholinergics, alpha-blockers, opioids, and other medications commonly used in SCI may adversely affect spermatogenesis or sperm function.

### Leukocytospermia

**[BOARD]**

Leukocytospermia, defined as the presence of more than 1 million white blood cells (10^6) per milliliter of semen, is highly prevalent in men with SCI. Leukocytospermia is considered the single worst predictive factor for the inability of sperm to penetrate the ovum. The reactive oxygen species (ROS) produced by seminal leukocytes cause oxidative damage to sperm membranes, impairing motility, viability, and the ability to undergo the acrosome reaction necessary for oocyte penetration.

Treatment of leukocytospermia includes antibiotic therapy for concurrent genitourinary infection, anti-inflammatory agents, antioxidant supplementation, and sperm processing techniques (density gradient centrifugation, swim-up) to separate functional spermatozoa from leukocyte-contaminated seminal plasma prior to assisted reproduction.

## 13.7 Sperm Retrieval Methods

**[BOARD]**

### Penile Vibratory Stimulation (PVS) -- First-Line

PVS is the first-line method for sperm retrieval in men with SCI. A mechanical vibrator is applied to the glans penis (specifically the frenulum) to trigger the ejaculatory reflex via the pudendal nerve afferent limb.

**Key points:**
- Success rate: Approximately 86 percent in patients with injury levels at or above T10 (intact thoracolumbar ejaculation center); significantly lower (approximately 17 percent) for injuries below T10.
- Can be performed at home by the patient and/or partner after appropriate training, facilitating natural conception through intravaginal insemination.
- Preferred vibrator parameters: Amplitude of 2.5 mm or greater, frequency of 100 Hz, peak-to-peak displacement sufficient to trigger the ejaculatory reflex. Higher-amplitude devices (e.g., Ferticare or Viberect) are more effective than standard consumer vibrators.
- Semen quality obtained by PVS is superior to that obtained by EEJ, with higher motility (26.0% vs. 10.7%), viability (25.2% vs. 9.7%), and motile sperm count.
- **AD precaution:** PVS can trigger autonomic dysreflexia in patients with injuries at T6 and above. Blood pressure monitoring is mandatory during PVS in these patients. Nifedipine (10 mg sublingual) or nitroglycerin paste should be readily available for AD management. PVS should be performed with the patient in a seated or semireclined position.

### Electroejaculation (EEJ) -- Second-Line

EEJ is the second-line method when PVS fails. A rectal probe electrode delivers controlled electrical stimulation to the prostate and seminal vesicles, inducing emission.

**Key points:**
- Performed under medical supervision in a clinical setting
- Requires general anesthesia in patients with incomplete injuries who retain rectal sensation (the procedure may be painful)
- In patients with complete injuries, the procedure may be performed without anesthesia (no rectal sensation)
- AD risk is significant in patients with injuries at T6 and above; blood pressure monitoring and AD treatment must be immediately available
- Semen quality is inferior to PVS-obtained specimens
- Retrograde ejaculation is common with EEJ; bladder catheterization before and after the procedure allows collection of sperm from the bladder for use in assisted reproduction

### Surgical Sperm Retrieval -- Third-Line

When PVS and EEJ fail, surgical approaches may be used:

- **Prostate massage** -- Digital rectal massage of the prostate can sometimes produce emission of prostatic fluid containing spermatozoa.
- **Testicular sperm aspiration (TESA)** -- Percutaneous needle aspiration of spermatozoa directly from the testis.
- **Microsurgical epididymal sperm aspiration (MESA)** -- Open surgical aspiration of spermatozoa from the epididymis.
- **Percutaneous epididymal sperm aspiration (PESA)** -- Percutaneous needle aspiration from the epididymis.

Surgically retrieved sperm are typically used with intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI) due to the small number of spermatozoa obtained.

**Table 13.2: Sperm Retrieval Methods -- Summary**

| Method | Success Rate | Setting | AD Risk | Semen Quality | Preferred Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| PVS | ~86% (T10+) | Home or clinic | Yes (T6+) | Best of all methods | First-line for all |
| EEJ | ~80-90% | Clinic (medical supervision) | Yes (T6+) | Lower than PVS | PVS failures |
| Prostate massage | Variable | Clinic | Lower | Variable | EEJ failure |
| TESA/MESA/PESA | >90% | OR/clinic | Minimal | N/A (direct retrieval) | All other failures |

## 13.8 Assisted Reproductive Technology

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

When natural conception is not possible, assisted reproductive technologies (ART) offer various options based on semen quality and the female partner's reproductive status.

### Intrauterine Insemination (IUI)

IUI involves processing the semen sample (washing, concentrating) and placing the prepared sperm directly into the uterine cavity via a catheter, bypassing the cervical barrier.

- Appropriate when adequate numbers of motile sperm are obtained (typically more than 5 to 10 million total motile sperm after processing)
- Can be combined with ovarian stimulation in the female partner to increase success rates
- Multiple cycles may be needed; typical per-cycle pregnancy rates of 10 to 20 percent
- Cost-effective compared to IVF; often attempted first before progressing to more invasive and expensive options

### In Vitro Fertilization (IVF)

IVF involves ovarian stimulation, oocyte retrieval, fertilization of oocytes with processed sperm in the laboratory, and transfer of resulting embryos to the uterus.

- Indicated when sperm quality is insufficient for IUI or when IUI has failed
- Higher per-cycle success rates than IUI (approximately 30 to 50 percent depending on maternal age and other factors)
- More invasive and costly than IUI

### Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI)

ICSI involves direct injection of a single spermatozoon into the cytoplasm of an oocyte using a micromanipulation technique.

- Indicated for severe male factor infertility with very low sperm counts or motility
- Used with surgically retrieved sperm (TESA, MESA, PESA) which yield small numbers of spermatozoa
- Fertilization rates with ICSI are generally independent of sperm motility parameters, making it the preferred technique for the most severely impaired semen specimens
- Pregnancy rates are comparable to standard IVF when ICSI is used

### Fertility Counseling

Comprehensive fertility counseling for couples affected by SCI should address:

- Realistic expectations regarding natural conception potential based on injury level, completeness, and ejaculatory function
- Stepwise approach from least to most invasive fertility interventions
- Timeline considerations (female partner's age is the primary determinant of ART success)
- Financial aspects of fertility treatment (often not covered by insurance)
- Genetic counseling when indicated
- Psychological support for the emotional impact of infertility
- Options including donor sperm or adoption if ART is unsuccessful or not desired

## 13.9 Female Sexual Function After SCI

**[BOARD]**

### Neurophysiology of Female Sexual Response

Female sexual response involves complex interactions between autonomic and somatic neural pathways:

- **Genital vasocongestion (arousal):** Analogous to male erection, female genital arousal involves increased blood flow to the clitoris, labia, and vaginal walls, mediated by parasympathetic (S2-S4) pelvic nerve input.
- **Vaginal lubrication:** Vaginal transudate from increased blood flow through the vaginal epithelium. Two mechanisms:
  - **Reflexogenic lubrication:** S2-S4 parasympathetic pathway, triggered by direct genital stimulation
  - **Psychogenic lubrication:** Requires intact descending pathways from supraspinal centers to T11-L2 sympathetic outflow. Preserved T11-L2 dermatome sensation correlates with the ability to produce psychogenic vaginal lubrication.
- **Orgasm:** Involves coordinated autonomic and somatic responses including rhythmic contractions of pelvic floor musculature (pudendal nerve, S2-S4), cardiovascular changes, and subjective experience. Approximately 50 percent of women with SCI can achieve orgasm, though it may require more time and different stimulation techniques than before injury.

### Sexual Response by Injury Type

- **UMN injuries:** Reflexogenic vasocongestion from genital stimulation is preserved because the sacral reflex arc remains intact. Psychogenic arousal (from cognitive erotic stimuli) is impaired in complete UMN injuries due to interrupted descending pathways.
- **LMN injuries (S2-S5):** Reflexogenic genital arousal is impaired or absent because the sacral reflex arc is damaged. The capacity for orgasm is reduced in women with LMN S2-S5 injuries compared to those with UMN injuries.
- **Preserved T11-L2 dermatome sensation** (regardless of injury level or completeness) correlates with the ability to produce psychogenic vaginal lubrication, as this reflects intact descending pathways to the thoracolumbar sympathetic outflow.

### Orgasm

Research demonstrates that approximately 50 percent of women with SCI can achieve orgasm, though the experience may differ from pre-injury orgasm:

- Orgasms may take longer to achieve
- Stimulation of areas above the injury level (neck, ears, breasts) may become more erotically significant
- Some women describe "para-orgasms" involving intense pleasurable sensations from stimulation of areas with intact sensation near the zone of injury
- Vibrostimulation of the clitoris/genitalia may be effective for inducing orgasm, particularly in women with UMN injuries preserving the sacral reflex arc

## 13.10 Female Fertility After SCI

**[BOARD]**

### Preservation of Fertility

Female fertility is preserved after spinal cord injury. SCI does NOT affect long-term ovarian function, ovulation, or the ability to conceive, carry, and deliver a child. This is a critical counseling point: women with SCI who are sexually active and do not desire pregnancy must use contraception.

### Amenorrhea After Acute Injury

Temporary amenorrhea (cessation of menses) is common in the acute phase after SCI:

- **Incidence:** 50 to 85 percent of women experience amenorrhea after acute SCI. Higher rates are reported in cervical and high thoracic injuries.
- **Duration:** Approximately 50 percent of women return to regular menses within 6 months, and 90 percent within 12 months of injury.
- **Mechanism:** Acute amenorrhea is attributed to stress-related hypothalamic suppression (functional hypothalamic amenorrhea), similar to that seen with other forms of acute physiologic stress, critical illness, or significant weight loss.
- **Clinical significance:** The return of menses signifies restoration of ovulatory function and fertility. Contraceptive counseling should be initiated during acute rehabilitation, as pregnancy may occur once ovulation resumes.

### Contraception

Contraceptive selection in women with SCI requires consideration of several injury-specific factors:

**Table 13.3: Contraceptive Options in SCI**

| Method | Considerations in SCI |
|---|---|
| Combined oral contraceptives (estrogen + progestin) | **Avoid in the first year after injury** due to elevated DVT/PE risk. Estrogen-containing contraceptives further increase thrombotic risk. May also be avoided long-term in women with ongoing immobility or additional thrombotic risk factors. |
| Progestogen-only methods (pills, implants, injection) | Preferred hormonal contraception. No increased thrombotic risk. Depot medroxyprogesterone (Depo-Provera) injections every 3 months are convenient but may contribute to bone loss (concern given SCI-related osteoporosis). Subdermal implants (etonogestrel, Nexplanon) provide long-acting, reliable contraception. |
| Intrauterine devices (IUDs) | Effective long-acting contraception. However, IUD insertion and presence can trigger autonomic dysreflexia in women with injuries at T6 and above. Menstrual changes (heavy bleeding with copper IUD, amenorrhea with hormonal IUD) may affect AD patterns. |
| Barrier methods (condoms, diaphragm) | Condoms provide dual STI/pregnancy protection. Diaphragm use requires adequate hand dexterity for insertion, limiting its utility for women with tetraplegia. |
| Sterilization | Surgical sterilization is an option for women who have completed childbearing or definitively do not desire pregnancy. Requires informed consent and careful counseling. |

## 13.11 Pregnancy and SCI

**[BOARD]**

### Fertility and Conception

Fertility is unimpaired in women with SCI. Women with SCI can conceive naturally through sexual intercourse (assuming the male partner has adequate fertility) and carry pregnancies to term. The overall fertility rate in women with SCI is comparable to that of the general population when accounting for partnership status and contraceptive use.

### Pregnancy Complications

While fertility is preserved, pregnancy in women with SCI carries an increased risk of several complications:

1. **Pressure injuries** -- Weight gain, fluid retention, and shifting body mechanics increase pressure injury risk. More frequent skin checks and pressure redistribution are needed.
2. **Recurrent urinary tract infections** -- Already common in SCI, UTI frequency may increase during pregnancy due to urinary stasis, physiologic hydronephrosis, and immunologic changes.
3. **Increased spasticity** -- Pregnancy-related stretching of pelvic ligaments, fetal movement, and bladder distention may trigger increased spasticity. Antispasticity medications must be reviewed for teratogenicity (baclofen is category C, diazepam is category D).
4. **Decreased pulmonary function** -- Women with high thoracic or cervical injuries may experience further compromise of already-reduced respiratory capacity as the gravid uterus elevates the diaphragm.
5. **Constipation** -- Exacerbated by pregnancy-related hormonal slowing of gut motility superimposed on neurogenic bowel dysfunction.
6. **Thromboembolism** -- The risk of DVT and PE is elevated in both SCI and pregnancy. Combined, the risk is substantially increased. Prophylactic low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) may be indicated. LMWH does not cross the placenta and is compatible with breastfeeding.
7. **Anemia** -- Physiologic anemia of pregnancy may compound SCI-related anemia.

### Autonomic Dysreflexia During Labor

**[BOARD]**

AD during labor is one of the most clinically significant considerations in the management of pregnant women with SCI.

**Key points:**

- **Uterine innervation:** The uterus is innervated by T10-T12 afferents. Uterine contractions are a potent noxious stimulus below the injury level.
- **AD during labor:** Occurs in 85 to 90 percent of women with injuries at T6 and above during labor and delivery. The risk of AD during labor is present in all women with injury levels that place the uterine afferent pathways below the level of injury.
- **AD may be the ONLY sign of labor:** In women with complete SCI at T10 or above, uterine contractions cannot be consciously perceived. The onset of autonomic dysreflexia (severe hypertension, headache, flushing, sweating above the injury level, bradycardia) may be the first and only indicator that labor has commenced.
- **Treatment of choice:** Epidural anesthesia delivered to the T10 dermatome is the treatment of choice for AD during labor. The epidural blocks afferent noxious stimuli from the uterus, preventing the cascade of sympathetic hyperreflexia. The epidural should be placed early (as soon as labor is confirmed) and should continue for at least 12 hours postpartum, as uterine involution and postpartum procedures may continue to trigger AD.

### Distinguishing AD from Preeclampsia

**[BOARD]**

Both AD and preeclampsia present with hypertension during pregnancy, making differentiation essential for appropriate management.

**Table 13.4: Autonomic Dysreflexia vs. Preeclampsia**

| Feature | Autonomic Dysreflexia | Preeclampsia |
|---|---|---|
| Blood pressure pattern | Episodic; rises with contractions, resolves between contractions and with stimulus removal | Persistent; does not resolve between contractions |
| Temporal relationship | Temporally linked to noxious stimulus (contraction, bladder distention, etc.) | Persistent and progressive |
| Proteinuria | Absent | Present (>300 mg/24 hours or protein/creatinine ratio >0.3) |
| Platelet count | Normal | May be decreased (thrombocytopenia) |
| Uric acid | Normal | Elevated |
| Liver function tests | Normal | May be elevated (especially in HELLP syndrome) |
| Response to stimulus removal | Blood pressure normalizes when noxious stimulus is removed | Blood pressure persists despite stimulus removal |
| Headache | Common; severe, pounding | Common; may be persistent |
| Gestational timing | Can occur at any gestational age during labor | Typically after 20 weeks gestation |

**Clinical Pearl 13.1:** AD and preeclampsia can coexist. In a pregnant woman with SCI at T6 or above presenting with hypertension, both diagnoses should be considered. The key distinguishing feature is whether hypertension resolves between contractions (AD) or persists (preeclampsia). Laboratory testing (proteinuria, platelets, LFTs, uric acid) helps confirm or exclude preeclampsia.

### Anesthesia Considerations

- **Epidural anesthesia** is the preferred method for pain management and AD prevention/treatment during labor and delivery in women with SCI.
- **Avoid succinylcholine** (depolarizing neuromuscular blocking agent) within the first year after injury due to the risk of life-threatening hyperkalemia from denervation-related upregulation of extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors. After the first year, succinylcholine can generally be used if needed, though non-depolarizing agents are preferred.
- **Regional anesthesia** (spinal or epidural) is preferred over general anesthesia when possible to minimize respiratory complications.

### Breastfeeding

- Women with SCI can breastfeed, though those with injuries above T4 may have impaired milk let-down reflex (oxytocin release may be impaired or the efferent pathway disrupted).
- LMWH is compatible with breastfeeding (does not cross into breast milk in clinically significant amounts).
- Medications used during breastfeeding should be reviewed for safety (baclofen, oxybutynin, and gabapentin are generally considered compatible, though data are limited).

## 13.12 Psychosexual Rehabilitation

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

### Psychosexual Counseling

Comprehensive sexual rehabilitation after SCI extends far beyond the medical management of erectile dysfunction and fertility. Psychosexual counseling should address:

1. **Body image and self-esteem** -- SCI profoundly alters body image and may challenge sexual self-concept. Counseling should help individuals grieve pre-injury sexual function while developing a positive, adapted sexual identity.

2. **Communication with partners** -- Open communication about sexual needs, desires, limitations, and adaptations is essential for satisfying sexual relationships after SCI. Both the individual with SCI and their partner benefit from counseling.

3. **Exploration of alternative erogenous zones** -- Areas with preserved or enhanced sensation (above the injury level, at the zone of injury) may develop heightened erotic sensitivity. The neck, ears, chest, nipples, and other regions above the injury level may become primary sources of sexual pleasure.

4. **Positioning and adaptive techniques** -- Physical limitations (spasticity, contractures, impaired balance) may require adaptation of sexual positions. Occupational therapists and sexual health counselors can provide practical guidance on positioning, use of supportive pillows or bolsters, and timing of activities (e.g., before catheterization, after bowel program).

5. **Management of practical concerns:**
   - Bowel and bladder management before sexual activity (empty bladder, complete bowel program, secure catheter)
   - Spasticity management (stretching before activity, timing relative to antispasticity medication)
   - Skin protection (avoid prolonged pressure on insensate areas, inspect skin after activity)
   - AD management (for injuries T6 and above, maintain awareness of AD triggers during sexual activity)

6. **Sexual orientation and gender identity** -- Sexual rehabilitation must be inclusive and affirming of all sexual orientations and gender identities. LGBTQ+ individuals with SCI face additional challenges related to minority stress and may benefit from culturally competent counseling.

### The PLISSIT Model

The PLISSIT model (Permission, Limited Information, Specific Suggestions, Intensive Therapy) provides a framework for addressing sexuality in rehabilitation:

- **P -- Permission:** Creating a safe, non-judgmental environment where sexuality can be discussed. Initiating the conversation (many patients will not bring up sexuality unless asked).
- **LI -- Limited Information:** Providing basic information about the effects of SCI on sexual function, fertility, and contraception.
- **SS -- Specific Suggestions:** Offering tailored recommendations for erectile dysfunction treatment, sexual positioning, timing, bowel/bladder management, and other practical adaptations.
- **IT -- Intensive Therapy:** Referral to sexual health specialists, psychologists, or sex therapists for complex psychosexual issues, relationship difficulties, or trauma-related sexual dysfunction.

---

## Summary

Sexual function and fertility are profoundly affected by spinal cord injury, with distinct patterns determined by injury level, completeness, and the specific neural pathways involved. The "Point and Shoot" framework provides an essential organizing principle: parasympathetic pathways (S2-S4) mediate erection, while sympathetic pathways (T11-L2) mediate ejaculation. Reflexogenic erections are preserved in the vast majority of UMN injuries, while ejaculatory function is severely impaired across all injury types. PDE5 inhibitors are first-line for ED, with intracavernosal injections and vacuum devices as alternatives. Male infertility stems from both ejaculatory dysfunction and poor semen quality, with leukocytospermia identified as the single worst predictor of sperm penetration failure. PVS is the first-line sperm retrieval technique, followed by EEJ and surgical methods. Female fertility is preserved after SCI, and women can conceive and carry pregnancies safely with appropriate monitoring. AD during labor occurs in 85-90 percent of women with T6+ injuries and may be the only sign of labor; epidural anesthesia to T10 is the treatment of choice.
