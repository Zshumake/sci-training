2. Describe the vascular, biochemical, inflammatory, and apoptotic cascades of secondary injury
3. Define spinal shock and apply the Ditunno four-phase model to clinical assessment

5. Outline the prehospital and emergency department assessment and stabilization of suspected SCI

## 4.1 Primary Injury Mechanisms [BOARD]

Primary injury refers to the immediate mechanical damage to the spinal cord that occurs at the moment of impact. This damage is irreversible and initiates the cascade of secondary injury. The principal mechanisms of primary injury include:

### 4.1.1 Compression

The most common mechanism. Occurs when bone fragments, disc material, or ligamentous structures are displaced into the spinal canal, compressing the cord. Compression may be transient (as in hyperextension injuries where the cord is pinched between hypertrophied ligamentum flavum and osteophytic ridges) or sustained (as in burst fractures with retropulsed fragments).

### 4.1.2 Contusion

Contusion is the most common pathologic finding in traumatic SCI. The cord sustains a bruise-type injury from impact against displaced vertebral elements. Contusion produces central hemorrhagic necrosis of the gray matter (which has a higher metabolic demand and richer vascular supply than white matter) with relative preservation of the peripheral white matter in less severe injuries.

### 4.1.3 Distraction

Longitudinal stretching of the spinal cord beyond its elastic tolerance. This mechanism is characteristic of flexion-distraction (Chance) fractures and may occur without fracture in patients with ligamentous laxity (e.g., children, patients with Down syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis). Distraction injuries can damage blood vessels supplying the cord, producing ischemia.

### 4.1.4 Laceration and Transection

Direct tearing of cord tissue by penetrating objects (e.g., knife, bullet, bone fragment) or severely displaced vertebral elements. Complete anatomic transection is rare even in clinically complete injuries; most "complete" injuries represent severe contusion with hemorrhagic necrosis rather than true transection.

### 4.1.5 Shear

Rotational or translational forces produce shearing of neural tissue, particularly at the interface between gray and white matter. Shear injuries commonly accompany fracture-dislocations.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Contusion is the most common pathologic finding in traumatic SCI. True anatomic transection of the cord is rare. This distinction is therapeutically important because the persistence of intact (though dysfunctional) tissue in most injuries provides a potential substrate for neuroprotective and neuroregenerative therapies.

## 4.2 Secondary Injury Cascade [BOARD]

Secondary injury refers to the progressive cascade of cellular, molecular, and biochemical events that extend neural damage beyond the initial mechanical insult. This cascade begins within minutes of injury and may continue for months. Understanding the secondary injury cascade is critical because it represents the primary therapeutic target for neuroprotective interventions.

### 4.2.1 Vascular Mechanisms

**Hemorrhage:** Mechanical disruption of the intramedullary vasculature produces petechial hemorrhages in the central gray matter within minutes. These coalescent hemorrhages expand centrifugally over the first 4-8 hours, a process termed hemorrhagic necrosis.

**Vasospasm and ischemia:** Traumatic vasospasm of the anterior spinal artery and sulcal arteries, combined with microvascular thrombosis and vessel wall damage, produces ischemia in the watershed zones between the central (sulcal) and peripheral (pial) circulations. Ischemia is compounded by systemic hypotension (from neurogenic shock) and loss of autoregulatory capacity in the injured cord.

**Loss of autoregulation:** The normal ability of spinal cord vasculature to maintain constant perfusion across a range of systemic blood pressures is lost in the injured segment. Perfusion becomes passively dependent on mean arterial pressure (MAP), providing the rationale for maintaining MAP at 85-90 mmHg during the first 5-7 days.

**Reperfusion injury:** When blood flow is restored to ischemic tissue, the reintroduction of oxygen generates reactive oxygen species (ROS), paradoxically worsening cellular damage.

### 4.2.2 Biochemical Mechanisms

**Excitotoxicity:** Mechanical disruption and ischemia cause massive release of the excitatory neurotransmitter glutamate from damaged neurons and glia. Extracellular glutamate levels are markedly elevated within the first 3 hours after injury, with a possible second wave at 2-3 days. Excessive glutamate activates NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate), AMPA, and kainate receptors, producing uncontrolled calcium influx into cells.

**Calcium influx:** Intracellular calcium overload triggers multiple destructive pathways:
- Activation of calpains (calcium-dependent proteases) that degrade cytoskeletal proteins
- Activation of phospholipases that break down cell membranes
- Mitochondrial dysfunction with uncoupling of oxidative phosphorylation
- Activation of nitric oxide synthase (NOS) producing excessive NO
- Generation of free radicals

**Free radical damage:** Reactive oxygen species (superoxide anion, hydroxyl radical, hydrogen peroxide) and reactive nitrogen species (peroxynitrite, formed from NO reacting with superoxide) overwhelm endogenous antioxidant defenses (superoxide dismutase, catalase, glutathione peroxidase). Free radicals cause lipid peroxidation of cell membranes, protein oxidation, and DNA damage.

**Arachidonic acid cascade:** Phospholipase A2 activation releases arachidonic acid from membrane phospholipids. Arachidonic acid is metabolized by cyclooxygenase (producing prostaglandins and thromboxanes) and lipoxygenase (producing leukotrienes), generating additional free radicals and inflammatory mediators that promote vasoconstriction, platelet aggregation, and immune cell recruitment.

### 4.2.3 Inflammatory Response

The inflammatory response is a double-edged process: it clears debris and initiates repair, but excessive or prolonged inflammation exacerbates tissue damage.

**Timeline of inflammatory cell infiltration:**
- **Minutes to hours:** Resident microglia activate and begin secreting pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1-beta, IL-6)
- **6-12 hours:** Upregulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines continues; early neutrophil infiltration begins
- **24-48 hours:** Neutrophil infiltration peaks; neutrophils release proteases, ROS, and myeloperoxidase that damage surrounding tissue
- **3-5 days:** Neutrophils decline; monocyte/macrophage infiltration begins
- **5-7 days:** Macrophage infiltration peaks; M1 (pro-inflammatory) phenotype predominates initially, producing TNF-alpha, IL-1-beta, IL-6, and iNOS
- **7-14 days:** Transition toward M2 (anti-inflammatory, reparative) macrophage phenotype begins; M2 macrophages secrete IL-10, TGF-beta, and neurotrophic factors
- **Weeks to months:** Glial scar formation by reactive astrocytes; deposition of chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans (CSPGs) that create a physical and chemical barrier to axonal regeneration

**Glial scar:** The astroglial scar that forms around the injury site has a dual role. It seals the lesion, preventing expansion of inflammation and cellular damage into intact tissue. However, the dense meshwork of hypertrophied astrocyte processes and CSPGs creates one of the major barriers to axonal regeneration and is a target of regenerative therapies (e.g., chondroitinase ABC).

### 4.2.4 Apoptosis

Programmed cell death (apoptosis) contributes substantially to secondary tissue loss, peaking at 7-14 days post-injury. Apoptosis is particularly devastating for oligodendrocytes, the myelinating cells of the CNS. Loss of oligodendrocytes produces secondary demyelination of axons that survived the initial injury, contributing to functional deficits disproportionate to the extent of primary axonal damage.

Both intrinsic (mitochondrial pathway, mediated by cytochrome c release and caspase-9) and extrinsic (death receptor pathway, mediated by Fas ligand and caspase-8) apoptotic cascades are activated. The final common pathway converges on caspase-3, the executioner caspase.

### 4.2.5 Timeline Summary

| Phase | Time Post-Injury | Key Events |
|-------|-----------------|------------|
| Immediate | 0-2 hours | Primary mechanical damage; hemorrhage; vasospasm; glutamate release; ionic dysregulation |
| Acute | 2-48 hours | Edema peak; neutrophil infiltration (peak 24h); ongoing ischemia; excitotoxicity; lipid peroxidation |
| Subacute | 48 hours-2 weeks | Macrophage infiltration (peak 5-7d); apoptosis (peak 7-14d); Wallerian degeneration begins; demyelination |
| Intermediate | 2 weeks-6 months | Glial scar formation; CSPG deposition; continued Wallerian degeneration; syrinx formation may begin |
| Chronic | >6 months | Mature glial scar; cyst/cavity formation; ongoing demyelination; syringomyelia; neuropathic pain |

**Table 4-1. Timeline of Secondary Injury After SCI**

> **Clinical Pearl:** The secondary injury cascade provides the rationale for neuroprotective interventions. The therapeutic window for vascular and excitotoxic mechanisms is narrow (hours), while inflammatory and apoptotic targets may remain accessible for days to weeks. This temporal profile explains why ultra-early interventions (maintaining perfusion, surgical decompression) have the strongest evidence base.

## 4.3 Spinal Shock [BOARD]

### 4.3.1 Definition

Spinal shock is the temporary loss or depression of all spinal reflex activity below the level of injury. It is a physiologic phenomenon (not to be confused with neurogenic shock, which is a hemodynamic entity) characterized by areflexia, flaccidity, and loss of autonomic function below the lesion. Spinal shock does not imply structural completeness of the injury.

### 4.3.2 Ditunno Four-Phase Model

Ditunno and colleagues reconceptualized spinal shock as a four-phase process based on the sequential return of reflexes:

**Phase 1: Areflexia/Hyporeflexia (0-24 hours)**
- Complete absence of all reflexes below the level of injury
- Flaccid paralysis and areflexia
- The FIRST reflex to appear (within hours) is the delayed plantar response (flexion withdrawal to plantar stimulation)
- Pathologic cutaneous reflexes begin to emerge
- Mechanism: Loss of descending facilitation and tonic excitatory input; post-synaptic receptor hyperpolarization

**Phase 2: Initial Reflex Return (1-3 days)**
- Polysynaptic cutaneous reflexes become more prominent
- The **bulbocavernosus reflex (BCR)** typically returns during this phase (usually within 1-3 days)
- Cremasteric reflex returns
- Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) remain absent
- Mechanism: Denervation supersensitivity of post-synaptic receptors; upregulation of excitatory receptor expression

**Phase 3: Early Hyperreflexia (2 days-4 weeks)**
- DTRs begin to return and become hyperactive
- Babinski sign appears
- Detrusor hyperreflexia develops
- Early spasticity may emerge
- Mechanism: Growth of new synaptic connections (collateral sprouting); interneuron reorganization

**Phase 4: Late Hyperreflexia/Spasticity (1-12 months)**
- Full-blown spasticity develops
- Hyperactive DTRs with clonus
- Flexor and extensor spasms
- Autonomic dysreflexia emerges (in T6 and above injuries)
- Neurogenic bladder patterns become established
- Mechanism: Long-term receptor and synaptic reorganization; loss of supraspinal inhibition

### 4.3.3 Bulbocavernosus Reflex (BCR) Testing

The BCR is a polysynaptic reflex mediated through the S2-S4 segments (pudendal nerve). It is tested by:
- Squeezing the glans penis or clitoris (or tugging on an indwelling Foley catheter)
- A positive response is reflex contraction of the external anal sphincter, palpated by the examiner's finger in the rectum

**Clinical significance:** The return of the BCR (typically within 24-72 hours) signals the end of Phase 1 spinal shock. After the BCR returns, the neurological examination becomes more reliable for determining completeness. If the BCR has returned and there is still no sacral sparing, the injury is likely complete.

However, recent evidence suggests that the BCR alone should not be used as the sole determinant of spinal shock resolution, and the BCR may return while the patient remains in early phases of spinal shock.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The sequence of reflex return in spinal shock follows the order: 1) delayed plantar response (FIRST, within hours), 2) bulbocavernosus reflex (1-3 days), 3) cremasteric reflex, 4) ankle jerk, 5) Babinski sign, 6) knee jerk. The BCR typically returns within 24-72 hours and has traditionally been used to signal the end of spinal shock, after which the neurological examination for completeness is more reliable.

## 4.4 Neurogenic Shock [BOARD]

### 4.4.1 Definition and Mechanism

Neurogenic shock is a distributive shock caused by loss of sympathetic vasomotor tone below the level of injury, resulting in widespread peripheral vasodilation and venous pooling. It occurs predominantly in injuries at T6 and above because the splanchnic sympathetic outflow (T5-L2) is disrupted.

### 4.4.2 Classic Triad

1. **Hypotension:** Due to loss of vasomotor tone and arterial vasodilation below the level of injury
2. **Bradycardia:** Due to unopposed parasympathetic (vagal) tone on the heart, particularly in injuries above T1-T4 (cardioacceleratory sympathetic outflow)
3. **Warm, dry skin below the injury:** Due to cutaneous vasodilation and loss of sympathetic-mediated vasoconstriction (in contrast to the cold, clammy skin of hypovolemic shock)

### 4.4.3 Neurogenic vs. Hemorrhagic Shock

| Feature | Neurogenic Shock | Hemorrhagic Shock |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Heart rate | Bradycardia | Tachycardia |
| Skin below injury | Warm, dry, flushed | Cold, clammy, pale |
| Blood pressure | Low (vasodilation) | Low (volume depletion) |
| Mechanism | Loss of sympathetic tone | Blood loss |
| Fluid responsiveness | Partial (requires vasopressors) | Responds well to volume |
| CVP | May be low-normal | Low |
| Level of injury | T6 and above typically | Any |
| Duration | Days to weeks | Until hemorrhage controlled |

**Table 4-2. Neurogenic vs. Hemorrhagic Shock**

> **Clinical Pearl:** In the trauma patient with SCI, neurogenic and hemorrhagic shock may coexist. The clinician must NOT attribute hypotension solely to neurogenic shock without first ruling out hemorrhagic sources (especially in the setting of polytrauma). The classic teaching is: "Hypotension with bradycardia in a trauma patient with cervical SCI = neurogenic shock. Hypotension with TACHYCARDIA = hemorrhagic shock until proven otherwise."

### 4.4.4 Treatment

1. **Volume resuscitation:** Isotonic crystalloid initially; avoid over-resuscitation, which can worsen pulmonary edema
2. **Vasopressors:** If hypotension persists despite adequate volume:
   - Norepinephrine or phenylephrine (alpha-1 agonists for vasoconstriction)
   - Dopamine (at moderate doses for both vasopressor and chronotropic effects)
3. **Atropine:** For symptomatic bradycardia (blocks parasympathetic vagal tone)
4. **MAP goal:** Maintain MAP 85-90 mmHg for 5-7 days to optimize spinal cord perfusion (supported by current clinical practice guidelines)

## 4.5 Acute Management [BOARD]

### 4.5.1 Prehospital Care

- **Spinal immobilization:** Rigid cervical collar and long spine board for all patients with suspected SCI. Logroll technique for movement.
- **ABC assessment:** Airway, breathing, circulation; intubate if necessary (avoid hyperextension during intubation; use in-line stabilization)
- **Rapid transport:** To a Level I or II trauma center with neurosurgical/orthopedic spine capability
- **Avoid secondary injury:** Maintain oxygenation (SpO2 >94%), adequate blood pressure, normothermia

### 4.5.2 Emergency Department Assessment

**Imaging:**
- **CT scan (C1-C7):** First-line imaging modality for cervical spine evaluation in trauma. Superior to plain radiography for detecting fractures, particularly at the craniocervical and cervicothoracic junctions.
- **MRI:** Indicated for evaluation of soft tissue injury (disc herniation, ligamentous disruption, epidural hematoma), spinal cord parenchymal injury (contusion, edema, hemorrhage), and when there is neurologic deficit without osseous abnormality. MRI findings (intramedullary hemorrhage, cord edema extent) have prognostic value.
- **CT angiography:** May be indicated for evaluation of vertebral artery injury in cervical fractures involving the transverse foramen or facet dislocations.

### 4.5.3 Succinylcholine Avoidance

Succinylcholine, a depolarizing neuromuscular blocking agent commonly used for rapid-sequence intubation, must be avoided after 48 hours following SCI due to the risk of life-threatening hyperkalemia. Denervation supersensitivity causes proliferation of extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors across the entire muscle membrane (rather than being confined to the neuromuscular junction). When succinylcholine depolarizes these receptors, massive potassium efflux occurs, potentially triggering fatal cardiac arrhythmias.

The risk period begins approximately 48 hours post-injury and persists indefinitely (some sources suggest the risk diminishes after 6-9 months, but succinylcholine should be permanently avoided in SCI patients). Non-depolarizing agents (rocuronium, vecuronium, cisatracurium) are safe alternatives.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Succinylcholine is CONTRAINDICATED after 48 hours following SCI due to the risk of fatal hyperkalemia from denervation supersensitivity. It is safe to use within the first 24-48 hours (before receptor upregulation occurs). After 48 hours, use only non-depolarizing neuromuscular blocking agents.

### 4.5.4 Pharmacotherapy: Methylprednisolone

**NASCIS Trials:**

The National Acute Spinal Cord Injury Studies (NASCIS) represent the landmark trials for methylprednisolone sodium succinate (MPSS) in acute SCI:

- **NASCIS I (1984):** Compared low-dose vs. high-dose methylprednisolone. No significant difference between groups. No placebo control.
- **NASCIS II (1990):** Compared MPSS (30 mg/kg bolus + 5.4 mg/kg/hr for 23 hours) vs. naloxone vs. placebo. Post-hoc analysis showed modest improvement in motor scores at 6 months when MPSS was initiated within 8 hours of injury. This subgroup analysis has been widely criticized.
- **NASCIS III (1997):** Compared 24-hour MPSS vs. 48-hour MPSS vs. tirilazad mesylate. Post-hoc analysis suggested that patients treated between 3-8 hours after injury benefited from the extended 48-hour regimen, while those treated within 3 hours had equivalent outcomes with 24-hour dosing.

**NASCIS II Dosing Protocol:**
- Bolus: 30 mg/kg IV over 15 minutes
- Maintenance: 5.4 mg/kg/hr continuous infusion
- If started within 3 hours: Infuse for 23 hours total
- If started 3-8 hours: Infuse for 48 hours total
- If more than 8 hours since injury: Do NOT administer (potential harm)

**Current Guidelines:**
The AANS/CNS published updated guidelines in 2013 recommending AGAINST the routine use of methylprednisolone in acute SCI (downgraded from treatment standard to treatment option, and ultimately to a recommendation against use). The rationale includes:
- Original NASCIS findings were based on post-hoc subgroup analyses
- Modest motor improvement (approximately 4 points on the motor scale) of questionable functional significance
- Significant side effects: increased rates of wound infection, gastrointestinal hemorrhage, sepsis, pneumonia, pulmonary embolism
- No demonstrated benefit for functional outcome

Despite these guidelines, practice varies internationally, and some institutions continue to offer MPSS within the context of informed consent.

### 4.5.5 Early Surgical Decompression

**STASCIS Trial:**
The Surgical Timing in Acute Spinal Cord Injury Study (STASCIS) was a multicenter, prospective cohort study evaluating the effect of early (<24 hours) versus delayed (>24 hours) surgical decompression for cervical SCI.

Key findings:
- Patients undergoing early decompression (<24 hours) were 2.8 times more likely to achieve at least a 2-grade AIS improvement compared to those treated after 24 hours
- 19.8% of early surgery patients achieved at least a 2-grade AIS improvement vs. 8.8% in the late surgery group
- No significant difference in complication rates between groups

Current guidelines (2024 AANS/CNS update) recommend early surgical decompression within 24 hours of injury for patients with cervical SCI, citing moderate evidence supporting improved neurologic outcomes.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The AANS/CNS 2013 guidelines recommend AGAINST routine methylprednisolone use in acute SCI. The STASCIS trial supports early surgical decompression (<24 hours) for improved AIS conversion rates. The current standard of care emphasizes MAP optimization (85-90 mmHg x 5-7 days) and early decompression over pharmacotherapy.

## 4.6 Spinal Fracture Classification [BOARD]

### 4.6.1 Upper Cervical Fractures

**Jefferson Fracture (C1 Burst Fracture):**
- Mechanism: Axial load (diving, falls on vertex of skull)
- Pathology: Bilateral fractures of both the anterior and posterior arches of the atlas (C1 burst)
- Stability: Considered mechanically STABLE if the transverse ligament is intact (rule of Spence: combined lateral mass overhang <6.9 mm on open-mouth odontoid view suggests intact transverse ligament)
- Treatment: Rigid cervical collar or halo vest; rarely requires surgery unless transverse ligament is disrupted
- Neurologic injury is uncommon because the spinal canal is widest at C1

**Hangman Fracture (C2 Bilateral Pars Interarticularis Fracture):**
- Mechanism: Hyperextension with axial loading (historically from judicial hanging; now typically from MVCs or falls)
- Pathology: Bilateral fracture through the pars interarticularis (isthmus) of the axis (C2)
- Stability: Generally considered STABLE because the fracture effectively widens the spinal canal
- Classification (Levine-Edwards):
  - Type I: <3 mm displacement, no angulation (stable; collar)
  - Type II: >3 mm displacement and/or angulation (reduced in halo)
  - Type IIA: Significant angulation without displacement (traction contraindicated; halo or surgery)
  - Type III: Displaced with unilateral or bilateral facet dislocation (unstable; surgery)
- Neurologic injury is relatively uncommon due to the large canal diameter at C2

**Odontoid (Dens) Fractures:**
- Type I: Fracture of the dens tip (avulsion of alar ligament). Rare. Stable. Treated with cervical collar.
- Type II: Fracture through the base of the dens. **Most common odontoid fracture type. Highest nonunion rate** (up to 26-36% in elderly). Treatment: halo vest immobilization or anterior odontoid screw fixation; posterior C1-C2 fusion for elderly or irreducible fractures.
- Type III: Fracture through the body of C2 (extends into the vertebral body). Generally stable due to the larger fracture surface area with good cancellous bone healing. Treated with halo vest or rigid collar.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Odontoid Type II fractures are the most common odontoid fracture type and have the highest nonunion rate. In elderly patients, odontoid Type II fractures may require surgical fixation due to poor healing potential in osteoporotic bone.

### 4.6.2 Subaxial Cervical Fractures

**Cervical Burst Fractures:**
- Result from axial loading (compression) forces
- Involve disruption of the anterior and middle columns with retropulsion of bone fragments into the spinal canal
- May be stable or unstable depending on posterior column integrity

**Facet Dislocations:**
- **Unilateral facet dislocation:** Typically results from a flexion-rotation mechanism. Produces <50% anterior subluxation of the vertebral body. Usually associated with incomplete neurologic injury (radiculopathy or incomplete cord injury).
- **Bilateral facet dislocation:** Results from a flexion-distraction mechanism. Produces >50% anterior subluxation. Usually associated with complete spinal cord injury. Requires emergent reduction (closed reduction with traction if awake and cooperative, or open reduction) and surgical stabilization.

### 4.6.3 Thoracolumbar Fractures

**Denis Three-Column Model:**

Francis Denis (1983) proposed the three-column model for assessing thoracolumbar spine stability, replacing Holdsworth's two-column model:

| Column | Structures |
|--------|-----------|
| Anterior | Anterior longitudinal ligament, anterior 2/3 of vertebral body, anterior annulus fibrosus |
| Middle | Posterior 1/3 of vertebral body, posterior annulus fibrosus, posterior longitudinal ligament |
| Posterior | Pedicles, facet joints, facet capsules, laminae, ligamentum flavum, spinous processes, interspinous/supraspinous ligaments |

**Table 4-3. Denis Three-Column Model**

**Stability rule:** Disruption of two or more columns implies instability and generally requires surgical stabilization. Middle column disruption is particularly significant.

**Thoracolumbar fracture types:**
- **Compression fracture:** Anterior column failure under compression. Middle and posterior columns intact. Stable unless significant kyphosis (>30 degrees) or >50% loss of vertebral body height.
- **Burst fracture:** Anterior AND middle column failure under axial loading. Bone fragments may retropulse into the canal. Unstable; often requires surgery.
- **Chance (flexion-distraction) fracture:** Horizontal fracture from posterior to anterior, occurring at T12-L2, typically from lap seatbelt mechanism. Associated with intra-abdominal injuries in up to 50% of cases. The entire vertebra fails in tension.
- **Fracture-dislocation:** Failure of all three columns with translational displacement. Most unstable pattern; highest rate of neurologic injury.

### 4.6.4 SCIWORA (Spinal Cord Injury Without Radiographic Abnormality)

**Pediatric SCIWORA:**
- Primarily affects children due to the unique biomechanics of the pediatric spine:
  - Relatively large head-to-body ratio
  - Horizontal facet joints allowing greater translational motion
  - Elastic ligaments and joint capsules
  - Immature vertebral bodies (wedge-shaped, cartilaginous endplates)
  - Vertebral column can stretch up to 5 cm without disruption, but the spinal cord ruptures after only 5-6 mm of stretch
- The spine transiently deforms beyond the cord's elastic tolerance and then returns to normal alignment, producing cord injury without visible fracture or dislocation on plain radiographs or CT
- MRI may show cord signal abnormality (edema, hemorrhage) in the absence of osseous injury
- More severe neurologic injury and higher cervical levels in younger children (under 8 years)

**Adult SCIWORA:**
- In adults, SCIWORA typically occurs in the setting of pre-existing cervical spondylosis or stenosis
- Hyperextension mechanism in an elderly patient with a degenerative spine produces central cord syndrome
- MRI typically shows cord signal abnormality; no fracture or dislocation on CT
- With the widespread use of MRI, the concept has evolved: many cases previously classified as SCIWORA show abnormalities on MRI (disc herniations, ligamentous injury)

## 4.7 Spinal Orthoses [BOARD]

### 4.7.1 Cervical Orthoses

Cervical bracing follows a hierarchy of increasing immobilization:

| Orthosis | Motion Restriction | Primary Indication |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Soft cervical collar | Minimal (comfort only) | Cervical strain; kinesthetic reminder; not for unstable injuries |
| Hard cervical orthosis (HCO): Philadelphia, Aspen, Miami J | Moderate: limits flexion/extension ~75%, rotation ~55% | Stable midcervical injuries; post-operative step-down |
| Cervicothoracic orthosis (CTO): SOMI, Minerva, Yale | Good: limits flexion ~93% (SOMI), extension 42-60% | Mid-lower cervical fractures (C3-T1); moderate instability |
| Four-poster | Moderate-good: good flexion/extension control | Lower cervical injuries |
| Halo vest | Best: restricts ~75% of motion at C1-C2; best for upper cervical | Unstable upper cervical fractures; odontoid Type II; post-operative augmentation |

**Table 4-4. Cervical Orthosis Hierarchy**

**SOMI brace** (sternal-occipital-mandibular immobilizer): Provides excellent flexion restriction (93%) but poor extension control (42%). Best suited for injuries where flexion must be limited but the patient can be compliant with activity restrictions.

**Halo vest complications:** Pin loosening, pin site infection, skull penetration (especially in osteoporotic bone and children), ring migration, pressure injuries under the vest, difficulty with respiratory hygiene, reduced pulmonary function, dysphagia, and pin-related discomfort. In elderly patients, halo vest use has been associated with higher complication and mortality rates compared to surgical fixation.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The SOMI brace provides excellent flexion control (93%) but poor extension control (42%). Therefore, it is NOT appropriate for injuries requiring extension restriction (e.g., extension-type injuries). The halo vest provides the most rigid external immobilization, especially for the upper cervical spine, and is indicated for unstable upper cervical fractures.

### 4.7.2 Thoracolumbar Orthoses

- **TLSO (thoracolumbosacral orthosis):** Controls motion from T4 to L2. Examples include the Jewett hyperextension brace (limits flexion, used for stable compression fractures), the CASH (cruciform anterior spinal hyperextension) brace, and custom-molded TLSOs.
- **LSO (lumbosacral orthosis):** Controls motion from L3 to the sacrum. Used for lower lumbar injuries. May incorporate a thigh spica for additional rotational control.
- **General rule:** Injuries at T4-L2 require a TLSO; injuries at L3 and below require an LSO, often with a thigh spica extension.

## 4.8 Neuroprotection Research [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 4.8.1 Riluzole

Riluzole is a sodium channel blocker approved for amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) that reduces excitotoxicity by inhibiting glutamate release. The RISCIS (Riluzole in Spinal Cord Injury Study) phase III randomized controlled trial was terminated early due to slow enrollment. Preliminary results showed a trend toward improved ASIA motor scores and AIS grade improvement at 6 months in the riluzole group, but the results were not statistically significant. Riluzole remains under investigation as a potential neuroprotective agent.

### 4.8.2 Minocycline

Minocycline is a tetracycline antibiotic with demonstrated anti-inflammatory, anti-apoptotic, and anti-excitotoxic properties. It crosses the blood-brain barrier effectively. Phase II studies have shown a trend toward improved motor recovery, and a phase III trial has been conducted. Minocycline remains one of the most extensively studied repurposed neuroprotective agents in SCI, though definitive evidence of clinical efficacy is still pending.

### 4.8.3 Therapeutic Hypothermia

Modest systemic hypothermia (33 degrees Celsius) aims to reduce metabolic demand, attenuate inflammatory responses, and decrease excitotoxic damage. Intravascular cooling methods have been used in clinical trials for acute cervical SCI. A multicenter prospective trial from the University of Miami has investigated the safety and efficacy of intravascular hypothermia. While some studies suggest improved neurologic outcomes, the evidence remains insufficient to recommend routine use, and logistical challenges (specialized equipment, ICU requirements, potential coagulopathy and infection risk) limit widespread adoption.

### 4.8.4 Stem Cell Therapies

Multiple stem cell types are under investigation for SCI:

- **Neural stem/progenitor cells (NSPCs):** Can differentiate into neurons, oligodendrocytes, and astrocytes. May replace lost cells and promote remyelination.
- **Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs):** Derived from bone marrow, adipose tissue, or umbilical cord. Act primarily through paracrine mechanisms (secretion of neurotrophic factors, anti-inflammatory cytokines) rather than direct cell replacement.
- **Oligodendrocyte progenitor cells (OPCs):** Target remyelination of demyelinated but intact axons. The AST-OPC1 trial (Asterias/Lineage Cell Therapeutics) was among the first cell therapy trials in SCI.
- **Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs):** Generated from adult somatic cells; avoid ethical concerns of embryonic stem cells but carry risks of tumorigenicity.

Current evidence from phase I/II trials demonstrates safety of various cell therapies, but definitive efficacy data are lacking.

### 4.8.5 Epidural Spinal Cord Stimulation

Epidural spinal cord stimulation (eSCS) has shown remarkable potential for restoring voluntary motor function and autonomic regulation in chronic, complete SCI patients. Landmark studies have demonstrated that eSCS can enable:
- Voluntary movement of paralyzed limbs
- Independent standing and stepping
- Improved cardiovascular function
- Enhanced bladder control

The mechanism involves activation of dormant spinal circuits below the level of injury, augmented by residual supraspinal inputs that may be insufficient to generate movement independently but can be amplified by epidural stimulation. Transcutaneous spinal cord stimulation (tSCS) offers a non-invasive alternative currently under investigation.

### 4.8.6 Other Investigational Approaches

- **Anti-Nogo antibodies:** Nogo-A is a myelin-associated inhibitor of axonal regeneration. Anti-Nogo antibodies (e.g., NG-101/ATI355) block this inhibitory signal, promoting axonal sprouting and regeneration. Phase I clinical trials have been conducted.
- **Chondroitinase ABC:** An enzyme that degrades chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans (CSPGs) in the glial scar, removing a major barrier to axonal regeneration. Primarily in preclinical investigation.
- **Rho pathway inhibitors (Cethrin/VX-210):** Rho-ROCK signaling mediates growth cone collapse in response to myelin inhibitors. Phase I/II trials of intrathecal Rho pathway inhibitors showed promising safety profiles.

## Summary

Primary SCI results from mechanical forces (compression, contusion, distraction, laceration, shear), with contusion being the most common pathologic finding. The secondary injury cascade encompasses vascular (hemorrhage, vasospasm, ischemia, reperfusion injury), biochemical (glutamate excitotoxicity, calcium influx, free radicals, arachidonic acid cascade), inflammatory (neutrophils peak 24-48h, macrophages 5-7d, glial scar formation), and apoptotic (peak 7-14d, oligodendrocyte death, secondary demyelination) mechanisms. Spinal shock follows the Ditunno four-phase model: Phase 1 (areflexia, 0-24h) with delayed plantar response first, Phase 2 (initial reflex return, 1-3d) with BCR return, Phase 3 (early hyperreflexia, 2d-4wk), and Phase 4 (spasticity, 1-12mo). Neurogenic shock (T6 and above) presents with hypotension, bradycardia, and warm skin, requiring fluids, vasopressors, and MAP 85-90 mmHg for 5-7 days. NASCIS II methylprednisolone (30 mg/kg + 5.4 mg/kg/hr x 23-48h within 8h) is NOT recommended by 2013 AANS/CNS guidelines. STASCIS supports early decompression within 24 hours. Succinylcholine is contraindicated after 48 hours. Jefferson fractures are typically stable; Hangman fractures are generally stable; Odontoid Type II has the highest nonunion rate; Chance fractures involve the thoracolumbar junction with 50% associated abdominal injuries. Denis three-column theory defines thoracolumbar stability. Cervical bracing progresses from soft collar through halo vest with increasing immobilization.
