## 5.1 Pathophysiology [BOARD]

### 5.1.1 Normal Autonomic Regulation

In the intact nervous system, sympathetic preganglionic neurons in the intermediolateral cell column (IML, T1-L2) are under tonic inhibitory control from supraspinal centers, primarily the rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM), the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS), and the hypothalamus. These descending pathways travel in the lateral funiculus and maintain a regulated balance between sympathetic excitation and inhibition, permitting graded cardiovascular responses to physiologic stimuli.

When a noxious stimulus occurs below a given spinal level, afferent signals ascend to the spinal cord, activate segmental sympathetic reflexes, and simultaneously relay to supraspinal centers. The supraspinal centers modulate the sympathetic response through descending inhibitory pathways, preventing excessive sympathetic activation.

### 5.1.2 The AD Mechanism

In SCI at or above T6, the descending inhibitory pathways are interrupted at the level of the lesion. The autonomic dysreflexia cascade proceeds as follows:

**Step 1: Noxious Stimulus Below the Injury Level**
A noxious or irritating stimulus (most commonly bladder distension) below the level of injury activates peripheral sensory receptors (nociceptors, mechanoreceptors, proprioceptors).

**Step 2: Afferent Signal Transmission**
Afferent impulses travel via the dorsal roots to the spinal cord, entering at or below the level of the stimulus.

**Step 3: Massive Sympathetic Discharge**
The afferent signals activate sympathetic preganglionic neurons in the IML, particularly the splanchnic sympathetic outflow (T5-L2). Because descending inhibitory supraspinal pathways cannot cross the lesion, the sympathetic response is unopposed, uninhibited, and massively amplified. This produces widespread vasoconstriction in the splanchnic, renal, and lower extremity vascular beds below the level of injury.

**Step 4: Hypertension**
The massive vasoconstriction below the lesion produces significant systemic hypertension. The splanchnic vascular bed alone contains approximately 25% of total blood volume, and its constriction produces a dramatic rise in blood pressure.

**Step 5: Baroreceptor Response**
The hypertension is detected by arterial baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch. The baroreceptor reflex activates the parasympathetic system via the vagus nerve (CN X), producing:
- **Bradycardia** (vagal slowing of heart rate)
- **Vasodilation ABOVE the level of injury** (flushing, sweating above the lesion -- this is the portion of the body where descending inhibitory signals can reach)

**Step 6: Persistent Hypertension**
Despite the compensatory parasympathetic response, the hypertension persists because:
- The descending sympatholytic signals CANNOT cross the spinal cord lesion to reach the sympathetic neurons below it
- The vasoconstriction below the lesion continues unopposed
- The vasodilation above the lesion (a relatively small vascular territory compared to the massive constricted territory below) is insufficient to normalize blood pressure
- The parasympathetic system has limited direct vasodilatory capacity (it acts primarily on the heart and vessels above the lesion)

The result is a dangerous positive feedback loop: the noxious stimulus continues to drive sympathetic activation below the lesion, hypertension persists, and the compensatory mechanisms above the lesion are inadequate to restore normal blood pressure.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The critical anatomic threshold for AD is at or above T6 because the splanchnic sympathetic outflow (T5-L2) controls the largest vascular bed in the body. Injuries above T6 leave the entire splanchnic bed under uninhibited spinal reflex control. Injuries below T6 allow enough supraspinal control over the splanchnic bed to prevent the massive vasoconstriction that characterizes AD.

### 5.1.3 Why T6?

The T6 level is the critical threshold for AD because:
- The greater splanchnic nerve arises from T5-T9, the lesser from T10-T11, and the least from T12
- These nerves innervate the celiac, superior mesenteric, and inferior mesenteric ganglia, controlling the splanchnic vascular bed
- The splanchnic bed receives approximately 25% of cardiac output at rest
- Injuries at T6 and above leave the entire splanchnic sympathetic outflow disconnected from supraspinal inhibitory control
- The massive vasoconstrictor capacity of the splanchnic bed is sufficient to produce dangerous hypertension

Injuries at or above T6 with MORE complete lesions produce MORE severe AD because there are fewer intact descending inhibitory pathways.

## 5.2 Epidemiology [BOARD]

### 5.2.1 Susceptible Population

- **Level:** Occurs in patients with SCI at T6 and above (some sources report cases as low as T8 in rare instances)
- **Prevalence:** Affects 48-90% of patients with injuries at or above T6
- **Completeness:** More common and more severe in complete (AIS A) injuries, though it occurs in incomplete injuries as well
- **Onset:** Typically does not occur during the spinal shock phase. Onset is usually 2-4 weeks to 2 months post-injury, coinciding with the return of spinal reflexes (Ditunno Phase 3-4). However, cases have been reported as early as 4 days post-injury.
- **Chronicity:** Greater than 90% of susceptible patients experience their first episode within the first year after injury. AD continues to be a lifelong risk.

### 5.2.2 Frequency and Severity Factors

- Higher cervical injuries produce more frequent and more severe episodes
- Complete injuries (AIS A) produce more severe episodes than incomplete injuries
- Episodes tend to become more frequent and predictable over time as patients learn to recognize triggers
- Recurrent episodes are common; many patients experience multiple episodes daily

### 5.2.3 Heart Rate Variability

While bradycardia is the classically described heart rate response (due to vagal activation), tachycardia may actually occur more frequently than bradycardia in AD. The heart rate response depends on the balance between the baroreceptor-mediated vagal response and the direct sympathetic activation of the heart (if the cardioacceleratory sympathetic fibers at T1-T4 are involved in the sympathetic discharge). In injuries above T4, both bradycardia and tachycardia can occur.

## 5.3 Clinical Presentation [BOARD]

### 5.3.1 Diagnostic Criteria

The hallmark criterion for AD is an acute elevation in systolic blood pressure (SBP) of 20 mmHg or more above the patient's baseline. This threshold is particularly important because the resting baseline blood pressure in patients with SCI above T6 is typically lower than in the able-bodied population (baseline SBP is usually 90-110 mmHg). Therefore, an SBP of 130-140 mmHg may represent a dangerous hypertensive episode in this population, even though it would be considered normal or only mildly elevated in an able-bodied individual.

### 5.3.2 Signs and Symptoms

**Above the level of injury:**
- Headache (pounding, severe; the most common symptom, reported by 51-85% of patients)
- Flushing (cutaneous vasodilation above the lesion)
- Profuse sweating (diaphoresis above the lesion)
- Nasal congestion (mucosal vasodilation)
- Blurred vision
- Anxiety, apprehension, sense of impending doom

**Below the level of injury:**
- Pallor (cutaneous vasoconstriction)
- Cool skin
- Piloerection (goosebumps)

**Cardiovascular:**
- Hypertension (SBP often 200-300 mmHg in severe cases)
- Bradycardia (classic but not universal; tachycardia may occur)
- Cardiac arrhythmias

> **Clinical Pearl:** A systolic blood pressure of 150 mmHg in a patient with C5 tetraplegia may represent a hypertensive emergency. The baseline SBP in tetraplegic patients is typically 90-110 mmHg, so a reading of 150 mmHg represents a >40 mmHg elevation above baseline and should trigger the AD management algorithm. Always compare to the individual patient's baseline, not to able-bodied population norms.

## 5.4 Triggers [BOARD]

### 5.4.1 Common Triggers in Order of Frequency

The key principle is that virtually any noxious or irritating stimulus below the level of injury can trigger AD. The triggers are organized by system and frequency:

**1. Bladder (85% of episodes -- most common trigger)**
- Bladder distension (most common single trigger)
- Urinary tract infection
- Catheter blockage or kinking
- Bladder or kidney stones (nephrolithiasis/ureterolithiasis)
- Urodynamic testing or cystoscopy
- Catheterization itself
- Detrusor sphincter dyssynergia
- Catheter traction on bladder wall

**2. Bowel (second most common trigger)**
- Fecal impaction
- Bowel distension
- Digital rectal examination or stimulation
- Hemorrhoids
- Anal fissures
- Acute abdomen (appendicitis, cholecystitis, bowel obstruction)

**3. Skin**
- Pressure injuries (decubitus ulcers)
- Ingrown toenails
- Burns (including sunburn)
- Tight clothing, shoes, or stockings
- Contact with sharp or irritating objects
- Skin infections (cellulitis, abscess)

**4. Reproductive/Urogenital**
- Ejaculation (including during fertility procedures or electroejaculation)
- Sexual intercourse
- Menstruation
- Vaginal or rectal examination
- Labor and delivery (uterine contractions)
- Epididymitis or testicular torsion

**5. Musculoskeletal**
- Fractures below the level of injury (may be occult)
- Heterotopic ossification
- Muscle spasms

**6. Other**
- Deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism
- Temperature extremes (overheating, hypothermia)
- Surgical procedures below the level without adequate anesthesia
- Abdominal pathology (ulcers, gastritis)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The mnemonic for AD triggers follows a priority search order: "Bladder first, bowel second, skin third." In the emergency setting, approximately 85% of AD episodes are caused by bladder-related stimuli. ALWAYS check the bladder FIRST when managing an episode of AD.

## 5.5 Management Algorithm [BOARD]

### 5.5.1 Immediate Non-Pharmacologic Steps

The management of acute AD follows a systematic, stepwise approach. Speed is essential, as uncontrolled hypertension can lead to life-threatening complications within minutes.

**Step 1: Position the patient**
- Sit the patient UPRIGHT and lower the legs (orthostatic reduction in blood pressure)
- This maneuver alone may reduce SBP by 15-20 mmHg through venous pooling

**Step 2: Loosen or remove constrictive items**
- Remove abdominal binders
- Remove compression stockings or pneumatic compression devices
- Loosen clothing, shoes, leg bag straps
- Remove any source of external compression

**Step 3: Monitor blood pressure**
- Check BP every 2-5 minutes
- Continue monitoring until the episode resolves and BP stabilizes

**Step 4: Check the bladder FIRST (most common trigger)**
- If a catheter is in place:
  - Check for kinking, blockage, or displacement
  - If blocked, irrigate gently with a small volume (10-15 mL) of saline
  - If irrigation fails, replace the catheter
  - Use 2% lidocaine jelly as lubricant during catheterization to minimize afferent stimulation
- If no catheter is in place:
  - Catheterize the patient using 2% lidocaine jelly
  - Drain the bladder slowly (rapid decompression can cause reflex hypotension)

**Step 5: If bladder management does not resolve the episode, check the bowel**
- Apply 2% lidocaine jelly or ointment to the rectum and wait 2 minutes for topical anesthesia
- Perform a GENTLE digital rectal examination
- If stool is present, gently disimpact (use lidocaine-lubricated finger)
- If digital stimulation triggers worsening AD, STOP, apply more lidocaine, and consider pharmacologic treatment before reattempting

**Step 6: If bladder and bowel checks do not resolve the episode**
- Perform a thorough skin examination for pressure injuries, ingrown toenails, burns, or tight clothing
- Check for other potential triggers (fractures, acute abdomen, DVT/PE, UTI)

**Step 7: Pharmacologic management** (if SBP remains >150 mmHg or symptoms persist despite trigger identification and removal)

### 5.5.2 Pharmacologic Management

| Agent | Dose | Route | Onset | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|-------|-------|
| Nitroglycerin paste (2%) | 1/2 to 2 inches | Topical | 1-3 min | FIRST LINE -- easily removable if hypotension occurs; wipe off when BP normalizes |
| Nifedipine (immediate release) | 10 mg | Chew and swallow (NOT sublingual biting) | 5-10 min | Calcium channel blocker; may cause reflex tachycardia; avoid sublingual due to unpredictable absorption |
| Captopril | 25 mg | Sublingual or oral | 15-30 min | ACE inhibitor; useful if nitrates contraindicated |
| Clonidine | 0.1-0.4 mg | Oral | 30-60 min | Central alpha-2 agonist; slower onset; useful for prophylaxis |
| Prazosin | 1-2 mg | Oral | 1-3 hours | Alpha-1 blocker; slower onset; better for prophylaxis than acute management |

**Table 5-1. Pharmacologic Agents for Acute Autonomic Dysreflexia**

**ICU-level agents** for refractory or severe AD (SBP >180-200 mmHg or symptomatic despite initial measures):
- Sodium nitroprusside IV infusion (direct vasodilator; requires arterial line monitoring)
- Hydralazine IV (direct arteriolar vasodilator)
- Diazoxide IV (direct vasodilator)
- Labetalol IV (combined alpha and beta blocker)
- Phentolamine IV (alpha blocker)

### 5.5.3 Critical Pharmacologic Precautions

**AVOID beta-blockers alone:** Pure beta-blockers (propranolol, metoprolol, atenolol) are CONTRAINDICATED as sole agents in AD. Beta-blockade removes the beta-2 mediated vasodilation while leaving alpha-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed, potentially worsening hypertension. If a beta-blocker is needed for rate control, it must be combined with an alpha-blocker (labetalol, which has both alpha and beta blocking properties, is acceptable).

**Nitroglycerin paste + PDE5 inhibitor CONTRAINDICATION:** Nitroglycerin (in any form) is ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED in patients who have taken a phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor (sildenafil/Viagra, tadalafil/Cialis, vardenafil/Levitra) within the preceding 24-48 hours. The combination can produce life-threatening refractory hypotension. Many SCI patients use PDE5 inhibitors for erectile dysfunction, making this a clinically relevant drug interaction.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Nitroglycerin paste (nitropaste) is the FIRST-LINE pharmacologic agent for acute AD because it is effective, rapid-acting, and most importantly REMOVABLE. If the patient becomes hypotensive after trigger removal resolves the AD, the nitropaste can simply be wiped off the skin. However, ALWAYS ask about PDE5 inhibitor use (sildenafil, tadalafil) before applying nitropaste -- the combination is absolutely contraindicated due to risk of severe refractory hypotension.

## 5.6 Prevention [BOARD]

### 5.6.1 Patient Education

Prevention is the cornerstone of AD management. All patients with injuries at T6 and above (and their caregivers) should receive comprehensive education about:

- What AD is and why it occurs
- How to recognize the signs and symptoms
- The most common triggers and how to prevent them
- The step-by-step management protocol
- When to seek emergency medical care
- The importance of carrying an AD wallet card or medical alert identification

### 5.6.2 AD Wallet Card

Patients should carry an autonomic dysreflexia wallet card or wear medical alert identification that includes:
- Diagnosis of SCI with level and completeness
- Baseline blood pressure
- Explanation of AD for healthcare providers unfamiliar with the condition
- Step-by-step emergency management protocol
- Contact information for the patient's SCI physician

### 5.6.3 Preventive Measures by System

**Bladder:**
- Regular clean intermittent catheterization (CIC) every 4-6 hours
- Maintain catheter patency if indwelling catheter is used
- Adequate fluid intake to maintain urine output
- UTI prevention and prompt treatment
- Avoidance of overdistension (catheterize before bladder volume exceeds 400-500 mL)
- Use anticholinergics for detrusor hyperreflexia if contributing to AD

**Bowel:**
- Regular bowel program (consistent timing, typically every 1-2 days)
- Adequate fiber and fluid intake
- Use of stool softeners as needed
- Application of topical lidocaine before digital stimulation

**Skin:**
- Regular skin inspections (at minimum twice daily)
- Pressure relief every 15-30 minutes while seated
- Appropriate wheelchair cushion and mattress
- Proper-fitting clothing and footwear
- Regular nail care to prevent ingrown toenails

**Procedural:**
- Instill 2% lidocaine into the bladder or rectum before urological or anorectal procedures
- Ensure adequate anesthesia for any procedure below the level of injury
- Prophylactic antihypertensive agents before known triggers (e.g., before electroejaculation, urodynamics, cystoscopy)
- Monitor blood pressure during all procedures in susceptible patients

## 5.7 Complications [BOARD]

### 5.7.1 Life-Threatening Complications

Uncontrolled or inadequately treated AD can produce catastrophic complications:

- **Cerebrovascular accident (CVA/stroke):** Both ischemic and hemorrhagic strokes can result from the severe hypertension
- **Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH):** Rupture of intracranial aneurysms or small vessels from the acute hypertensive surge
- **Myocardial infarction:** Acute coronary syndrome from the combined effects of hypertension, tachycardia (or bradycardia), and coronary vasoconstriction
- **Seizures:** From hypertensive encephalopathy
- **Retinal hemorrhage:** From the acute pressure elevation
- **Cardiac arrhythmias:** The combination of sympathetic activation, vagal tone, and altered cardiac repolarization can produce reentrant arrhythmias, including atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, and cardiac arrest
- **Pulmonary edema:** From acute left ventricular afterload elevation
- **Death:** AD is a medical emergency; fatalities occur, particularly in patients who are unaware of the condition or in healthcare settings unfamiliar with its management

### 5.7.2 Cardiac Conduction Abnormalities

AD produces alterations in cardiac repolarization that can be detected on ECG:
- ST segment changes
- T wave inversions
- QT prolongation
- These changes predispose to reentrant arrhythmias
- Combined with the marked bradycardia from vagal activation and the direct sympathetic effects on the myocardium, the arrhythmia risk is substantial

> **Clinical Pearl:** Autonomic dysreflexia is a medical EMERGENCY. Uncontrolled episodes can result in stroke, myocardial infarction, seizure, retinal hemorrhage, cardiac arrhythmias, and death. The severity of potential complications underscores the importance of rapid recognition and systematic management. Every emergency department should have an AD management protocol, and all healthcare providers caring for SCI patients should be familiar with the condition.

## 5.8 Autonomic Dysreflexia in Pregnancy [BOARD]

### 5.8.1 AD as a Sign of Labor

In pregnant women with SCI at T6 and above, AD may be the ONLY sign of labor, as the patient may not perceive uterine contractions due to sensory loss below the injury level. The uterus is innervated by T10-T12, so uterine contractions during labor serve as the noxious stimulus that triggers AD. This has critical implications:

- Pregnant women with SCI at T6 and above must be monitored for AD during the third trimester
- Sudden onset of headache, sweating, or hypertension may herald the onset of labor
- Uterine monitoring should be initiated when AD symptoms develop

### 5.8.2 Treatment in Pregnancy

**Epidural anesthesia to T10** is the treatment of choice for labor-induced AD. The epidural blocks the afferent signals from the uterus (T10-T12) that trigger the sympathetic discharge. Key points:

- The epidural should be placed early in labor, ideally before AD becomes established
- The block must extend to at least T10 to cover the uterine afferents
- The epidural both treats existing AD and prevents further episodes during labor
- Combined spinal-epidural techniques may also be used
- General anesthesia is an alternative but less preferred because it does not specifically target the mechanism

### 5.8.3 Differentiating AD from Preeclampsia

This distinction is critically important because the management differs substantially:

| Feature | Autonomic Dysreflexia | Preeclampsia |
|---------|----------------------|--------------|
| Timing of hypertension | During contractions; resolves BETWEEN contractions | Persistent; unrelated to contraction pattern |
| Proteinuria | Absent (or pre-existing from SCI-related renal disease) | Present (>300 mg/24 hours) |
| Platelet count | Normal | Decreased (thrombocytopenia) |
| Liver enzymes | Normal | Elevated (in HELLP syndrome) |
| Uric acid | Normal | Elevated |
| Headache pattern | During episodes; resolves with BP normalization | Persistent |
| Flushing/sweating | Above lesion level | Generalized edema |
| Response to epidural | Resolves with adequate block | May partially improve but persists |
| Response to delivery | Resolves after delivery | Resolves after delivery (may persist 48h) |

**Table 5-2. Differentiating Autonomic Dysreflexia from Preeclampsia**

> **Clinical Pearl:** In pregnant women with SCI at T6 and above, AD may be the ONLY sign of labor because the patient cannot feel uterine contractions. The key differentiation from preeclampsia is that AD-related hypertension occurs WITH contractions and resolves BETWEEN them, whereas preeclampsia produces persistent hypertension. Preeclampsia is also characterized by proteinuria (>300 mg), decreased platelets, and elevated uric acid -- features absent in AD. Epidural anesthesia to T10 is the treatment of choice for labor-induced AD.

## 5.9 Silent Autonomic Dysreflexia [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 5.9.1 Definition

Silent AD refers to significant blood pressure elevations (>20 mmHg above baseline) that occur WITHOUT the typical symptoms (headache, sweating, flushing). The patient may be completely unaware of the hypertensive episode. Silent AD has been documented in:

- Up to 43% of AD episodes in some studies
- More common in patients with higher cervical injuries (greater degree of sensory loss)
- Detected during ambulatory blood pressure monitoring or routine vital sign checks
- May occur during urodynamic studies, bowel programs, or other common activities

### 5.9.2 Clinical Significance

Silent AD poses a particular danger because:
- The patient does not recognize the episode and therefore does not seek treatment
- Repeated undetected episodes may contribute to end-organ damage (cardiovascular, cerebrovascular, renal)
- Chronic episodic hypertension from silent AD may contribute to the accelerated cardiovascular disease observed in chronic SCI
- Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring may be indicated in high-risk patients to assess the frequency and severity of silent AD

### 5.9.3 Screening and Management

Regular blood pressure monitoring during activities known to trigger AD (catheterization, bowel program, transfers, bladder management) can identify patients with silent AD. When detected, the same management principles apply: identify and remove the trigger, position the patient upright, and use pharmacologic intervention if needed.

## 5.10 Boosting in Athletes [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 5.10.1 Definition

"Boosting" refers to the intentional self-induction of autonomic dysreflexia by athletes with SCI to enhance athletic performance. The hypertensive response produced by AD causes increased cardiac output, higher heart rate, and enhanced catecholamine release, which can improve exercise performance by 10-15%.

### 5.10.2 Methods

Athletes have employed various methods to induce AD, including:
- Clamping or kinking indwelling urinary catheters to produce bladder distension
- Tightening leg straps or abdominal binders excessively
- Sitting on sharp objects or testicular compression
- Drinking excessive fluids before competition to ensure bladder fullness

### 5.10.3 Prohibition

The International Paralympic Committee (IPC) has banned boosting since 1994 due to the associated health risks. Pre-competition blood pressure checks are conducted; if an athlete's SBP exceeds 160 mmHg, they are not permitted to compete until BP normalizes. Despite the ban, surveys suggest that a significant minority of athletes with SCI have attempted boosting.

### 5.10.4 Health Risks

Intentional AD induction carries all the same risks as spontaneous AD: stroke, MI, seizure, cardiac arrhythmias, and death. The practice is particularly dangerous because the athlete is engaging in vigorous physical activity while simultaneously hypertensive, amplifying the cardiovascular stress.

## 5.11 Chronic Management [COMPREHENSIVE]

### 5.11.1 Recurrent AD

Some patients experience frequent, recurrent episodes of AD that significantly impair quality of life and functional independence. Chronic management strategies include:

**Pharmacologic prophylaxis:**
- Terazosin or prazosin (alpha-1 blockers): Used for chronic prophylaxis in patients with frequent bladder-triggered episodes
- Clonidine (central alpha-2 agonist): Reduces sympathetic outflow; available as an oral or transdermal patch
- Nifedipine extended-release: For patients with frequent episodes not controlled by trigger management

**Procedural interventions:**
- Botulinum toxin injection into the detrusor muscle: Reduces detrusor overactivity and AD triggered by bladder spasms
- Sacral neuromodulation or sacral deafferentation: Reduces afferent trigger signals
- Sphincterotomy: In selected cases of detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia causing recurrent bladder-triggered AD
- Augmentation cystoplasty: For medically refractory neurogenic detrusor overactivity

**Optimization of trigger prevention:**
- Review and optimize bladder management program (switch from indwelling to intermittent catheterization if applicable)
- Optimize bowel program timing and technique
- Ensure adequate skin protection and pressure relief
- Address heterotopic ossification if symptomatic

### 5.11.2 Chronic Cardiovascular Implications

Chronic AD has implications beyond the acute episodes:
- Repeated hypertensive surges may contribute to left ventricular hypertrophy
- Chronic AD may accelerate atherosclerosis and cardiovascular disease
- The combination of chronic AD and baseline hypotension (neurogenic) creates marked blood pressure variability, which is independently associated with cardiovascular morbidity
- Quality of life is significantly impacted by fear of episodes, activity avoidance, and social isolation

### 5.11.3 Evolving Research

Current research areas in AD management include:
- Targeted molecular therapies to reduce sympathetic hyperexcitability below the lesion
- Neurostimulation approaches to modulate spinal sympathetic circuits
- Improved understanding of neuroplasticity in sympathetic pathways after SCI
- Development of wearable blood pressure monitoring systems with automated alerts for AD detection
- Investigation of the relationship between chronic AD and long-term cardiovascular outcomes

## Summary

Autonomic dysreflexia is a potentially life-threatening emergency occurring in patients with SCI at T6 and above (48-90% prevalence). The pathophysiology involves a noxious stimulus below the lesion triggering massive unopposed sympathetic discharge via the splanchnic nerves, producing vasoconstriction and hypertension below the injury. Baroreceptor-mediated vagal activation produces bradycardia and vasodilation ABOVE the lesion, but descending inhibitory signals cannot cross the lesion to suppress the sympathetic response, resulting in persistent dangerous hypertension. The diagnostic criterion is SBP >20 mmHg above baseline (typical baseline 90-110 mmHg in SCI). The number one trigger is bladder distension (85%), followed by fecal impaction. Management follows a systematic algorithm: 1) sit up and lower legs, 2) loosen clothing/binders/stockings, 3) monitor BP q2-5 min, 4) check bladder FIRST (with lidocaine), 5) check bowel (lidocaine then DRE), 6) check skin/other triggers, 7) pharmacotherapy if SBP remains >150 mmHg. Nitroglycerin paste is the first-line pharmacologic agent (removable), but is absolutely contraindicated with PDE5 inhibitor use. Avoid beta-blockers alone. Complications include stroke, SAH, MI, seizures, retinal hemorrhage, arrhythmias, and death. In pregnancy, AD may be the only sign of labor; epidural to T10 is the treatment of choice. Differentiate from preeclampsia by the intermittent pattern (resolves between contractions), absence of proteinuria, normal platelets, and normal uric acid.
