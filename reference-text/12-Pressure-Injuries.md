## 12.1 Introduction and Epidemiology

**[BOARD]**

Pressure injuries (previously termed pressure ulcers, decubitus ulcers, or bedsores) are among the most common, costly, and preventable complications of spinal cord injury. They represent a leading cause of morbidity, rehospitalization, and mortality in the SCI population, with profound effects on physical health, psychological well-being, functional independence, and health-related quality of life.

### Prevalence and Incidence

The lifetime incidence of pressure injuries in persons with SCI ranges from 25 to 85 percent depending on the study population, follow-up duration, and case definition. At any given time, approximately 20 to 30 percent of community-dwelling individuals with SCI have at least one pressure injury. The annual incidence of new pressure injuries has been estimated at 20 to 31 percent among community-dwelling SCI patients and up to 40 percent among hospitalized or institutionalized individuals.

Pressure injuries are the most common reason for rehospitalization after initial SCI rehabilitation discharge, accounting for approximately 30 percent of readmissions. The estimated cost of treating a single Stage 4 pressure injury ranges from $20,000 to over $150,000 depending on complexity, need for surgical intervention, and duration of inpatient care. The cumulative economic burden of pressure injuries in the SCI population is measured in billions of dollars annually in the United States alone.

### Mortality

Pressure injuries are associated with significantly increased mortality in SCI. Individuals with SCI who develop pressure injuries have mortality rates two to five times higher than those without. Sepsis arising from infected pressure injuries remains a leading cause of death in persons with chronic SCI. Amyloidosis from chronic pressure injury-related inflammation, though rare, represents another potentially fatal complication.

## 12.2 Pathophysiology

**[BOARD]**

Pressure injuries result from sustained mechanical loading of soft tissues, leading to ischemia, cellular death, and tissue necrosis. The pathophysiology involves the interaction of several biomechanical and physiologic factors.

### Mechanical Forces

1. **Pressure** -- External pressure exceeding capillary closing pressure (approximately 32 mmHg in normal tissue) occludes blood flow and produces tissue ischemia. However, the critical threshold varies by tissue type, with muscle being more susceptible to pressure-induced ischemia than skin. The magnitude and duration of pressure are inversely related: high pressures cause injury more rapidly than lower sustained pressures.

2. **Shear** -- Shear forces arise when the skin remains stationary on a support surface while the underlying skeleton slides relative to the skin, as occurs with bed elevation or sliding down in a wheelchair. Shear deforms blood vessels traversing the tissue planes, reducing blood flow at lower applied pressures than direct compression alone. Shear is a critical but often underappreciated contributor to pressure injury development, particularly at the sacrum and ischial tuberosities.

3. **Friction** -- Friction acts on the skin surface and can cause superficial epidermal damage (abrasion). While friction alone produces only surface injury, it potentiates the damaging effects of shear by anchoring the skin against the support surface.

### Tissue Susceptibility

Muscle tissue is more metabolically active and more susceptible to pressure-induced ischemia than skin or subcutaneous fat. This differential susceptibility explains the clinical observation of deep tissue pressure injuries (DTPI), in which muscle necrosis may be extensive while the overlying skin appears only minimally damaged. Pressure injuries may originate at the bone-muscle interface (deep injuries progressing outward) or at the skin-surface interface (superficial injuries progressing inward), with the deep-originating pattern being characteristic of sustained pressure over bony prominences.

### SCI-Specific Pathophysiology

Spinal cord injury amplifies pressure injury risk through multiple mechanisms:

- **Loss of protective sensation** -- Eliminates the normal discomfort-driven repositioning that occurs during sleep and sitting.
- **Motor paralysis** -- Prevents voluntary weight shifting and repositioning.
- **Autonomic dysfunction** -- Impairs microvascular regulation, potentially reducing the reactive hyperemia response that normally follows tissue compression.
- **Muscle atrophy** -- Denervated muscle atrophies rapidly, reducing the soft tissue padding between bony prominences and the skin surface.
- **Altered skin physiology** -- Denervated skin may exhibit changes in collagen synthesis, moisture balance, and wound healing capacity.

## 12.3 NPIAP Staging System

**[BOARD]**

The National Pressure Injury Advisory Panel (NPIAP; formerly the National Pressure Ulcer Advisory Panel, NPUAP) staging system is the most widely used classification for pressure injuries. The 2016 revision updated terminology from "pressure ulcer" to "pressure injury" and refined stage definitions. The system comprises four numerical stages plus two additional categories.

### Stage 1: Non-Blanchable Erythema of Intact Skin

Intact skin with a localized area of non-blanchable erythema, which may appear differently in darkly pigmented skin where blanching may not be visible. The presence of blanchable erythema or changes in sensation, temperature, or firmness may precede visual changes.

- Skin is intact -- no open wound or break in the epidermis
- Non-blanchable erythema persists after removal of pressure (distinguish from blanchable erythema, which is reactive hyperemia and not a Stage 1 injury)
- Area may be painful, firm, soft, or boggy
- Area may be warmer or cooler than adjacent tissue
- In darkly pigmented skin, visible erythema may not be apparent; the clinician must rely on temperature, firmness, and pain changes compared to surrounding tissue
- Stage 1 may be difficult to detect in SCI patients due to absent sensation; skin inspection must therefore rely on visual and tactile assessment

### Stage 2: Partial-Thickness Skin Loss with Exposed Dermis

Partial-thickness loss of skin with exposed dermis. The wound bed is viable, pink or red, and moist. May also present as an intact or ruptured serum-filled blister.

- The dermis is exposed but adipose (fat) tissue is NOT visible
- Granulation tissue, slough, and eschar are NOT present -- if slough or eschar is present, the injury cannot be staged as Stage 2
- The wound bed is pink, viable, and moist
- An intact or ruptured serum-filled blister represents a Stage 2 injury
- This stage should NOT be used to describe moisture-associated skin damage (incontinence-associated dermatitis), medical adhesive-related skin injury, skin tears, or other epidermal injuries unrelated to pressure/shear

### Stage 3: Full-Thickness Skin Loss

Full-thickness loss of skin in which adipose (fat) tissue is visible in the wound bed. Granulation tissue and rolled wound edges (epibole) are often present.

- Subcutaneous fat may be visible
- Bone, tendon, and muscle are NOT exposed -- if these structures are visible or directly palpable, the injury is Stage 4
- Undermining and tunneling may occur
- Fascia is not exposed
- The depth of tissue damage varies by anatomic location: areas with significant subcutaneous tissue (e.g., buttocks) may develop very deep Stage 3 injuries, while areas with minimal subcutaneous tissue (e.g., occiput, malleolus) may be quite shallow

### Stage 4: Full-Thickness Skin and Tissue Loss

Full-thickness skin and tissue loss with exposed or directly palpable fascia, muscle, tendon, ligament, cartilage, or bone in the wound bed.

- Bone, tendon, muscle, ligament, cartilage, or joint capsule is exposed or directly palpable
- Slough and/or eschar may be present on some parts of the wound bed
- Undermining and/or tunneling often occurs, sometimes extensively
- Can extend into muscle and/or supporting structures (e.g., fascia, tendon, joint capsule), making osteomyelitis possible
- The depth varies by anatomic location

### Unstageable: Obscured Full-Thickness Skin and Tissue Loss

Full-thickness skin and tissue loss in which the extent of tissue damage within the wound cannot be confirmed because it is obscured by slough or eschar.

- The wound base is covered by slough (yellow, tan, gray, green, or brown) and/or eschar (tan, brown, or black)
- The true depth cannot be determined until sufficient slough or eschar is removed to expose the base of the wound
- Once debrided, the wound will be revealed as either Stage 3 or Stage 4
- Stable eschar (dry, adherent, intact without erythema or fluctuance) on a heel or ischemic limb should NOT be removed (see heel exception below)

### Deep Tissue Pressure Injury (DTPI)

Intact or non-intact skin with localized area of persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple discoloration, or epidermal separation revealing a dark wound bed or blood-filled blister.

- Results from intense and/or prolonged pressure and shear forces at the bone-muscle interface
- Pain and temperature change often precede skin color changes
- Discoloration may appear differently in darkly pigmented skin
- The injury may evolve rapidly to reveal the full extent of tissue damage
- May resolve without tissue loss or may rapidly evolve to full-thickness injury despite optimal treatment
- The wound may evolve to be covered by thin eschar; evolution may include a thin blister over a dark wound bed
- The term "suspected" has been removed from DTPI in the 2016 revision

**Table 12.1: NPIAP Staging Summary**

| Stage | Key Defining Features | Critical Distinguishing Points |
|---|---|---|
| Stage 1 | Intact skin, non-blanchable erythema | No break in skin; may have temperature/firmness changes |
| Stage 2 | Partial-thickness, exposed dermis, NO slough | Pink wound bed; includes intact/ruptured serum blisters |
| Stage 3 | Full-thickness, fat visible, no bone/tendon/muscle exposed | Undermining/tunneling may be present |
| Stage 4 | Full-thickness, bone/tendon/muscle exposed | Often includes undermining/tunneling; osteomyelitis risk |
| Unstageable | Base obscured by slough and/or eschar | True depth indeterminate; Stage 3 or 4 underneath |
| DTPI | Deep purple/maroon discoloration or blood-filled blister | May evolve rapidly; originates at bone-muscle interface |

### The Heel Exception

**[BOARD]**

Stable eschar (dry, adherent, intact without erythema, fluctuance, or drainage) on the heel serves as a natural biologic cover and should NOT be debrided. The heel has minimal subcutaneous tissue between the calcaneus and overlying skin, and disruption of stable eschar may convert a stable wound into an open Stage 4 injury with exposed bone. However, if the eschar becomes unstable (softened, boggy, fluctuant, draining, erythematous, or malodorous), it must be debrided to allow assessment and treatment of the underlying wound.

### No Reverse Staging

**[BOARD]**

A critical principle of pressure injury staging is that there is NO reverse staging. A Stage 4 pressure injury does NOT heal to become a Stage 3, then Stage 2, then Stage 1. Deep pressure injuries heal by secondary intention through formation of granulation tissue, wound contraction, and re-epithelialization. The lost muscle, subcutaneous fat, and dermis are replaced by scar tissue, not by regeneration of the original tissue layers.

Therefore, a Stage 4 pressure injury that is healing should be documented as a "healing Stage 4 pressure injury" rather than being restaged downward. Progress is documented by measuring wound dimensions (length, width, depth), percent of wound bed covered by granulation tissue versus slough/eschar, reduction in undermining and tunneling, and advancement of wound edges.

**Clinical Pearl 12.1:** Reverse staging is a common documentation error. A healing Stage 4 pressure injury should never be recorded as Stage 3 or Stage 2 -- it is documented as a "healing Stage 4" with serial wound measurements demonstrating improvement.

## 12.4 Anatomic Locations by Body Position

**[BOARD]**

The location of pressure injuries is determined by the interface between bony prominences and support surfaces. Different body positions create different patterns of pressure distribution and corresponding patterns of injury.

### Supine Position

- **Sacrum** -- Most common location in the supine position; vulnerable to both direct pressure and shear from bed elevation
- **Occiput** -- Particularly in cervical SCI patients who cannot reposition the head; proportionally large head in pediatric patients increases risk
- **Heels** -- Calcanei are highly susceptible due to minimal soft tissue coverage; heel pressure injuries are the second most common location overall in hospitalized patients
- **Scapulae** -- Less common but occur with prolonged supine positioning, especially on firm surfaces
- **Spinous processes** -- Thoracic spinous processes in thin individuals
- **Elbows** -- Olecranon bursae

### Sitting Position (Wheelchair)

- **Ischial tuberosities** -- The most common overall location for pressure injuries in persons with SCI. The ischial tuberosities bear a disproportionate share of body weight during sitting, and the small surface area of these bony prominences creates very high interface pressures. Incidence increases with duration of sitting and inadequacy of pressure relief practices.

### Side-Lying Position

- **Greater trochanter** -- The most vulnerable site in the lateral decubitus position; minimal soft tissue coverage in atrophied limbs
- **Lateral malleolus** -- Bony prominence of the fibula at the ankle; requires padding between legs
- **Lateral knee (fibular head region)** -- Vulnerable when legs are positioned in contact

### Prone Position

- **Anterior knee (patella)** -- If positioned prone with weight on the knees
- **Chest/sternum** -- Anterior chest wall
- **Anterior superior iliac spines (ASIS)** -- In thin individuals

**Table 12.2: Pressure Injury Locations by Body Position**

| Position | Most Common Location | Other At-Risk Locations |
|---|---|---|
| Supine | Sacrum (#1 supine) | Occiput, heels, scapulae, spinous processes, elbows |
| Sitting | Ischial tuberosities (#1 overall in SCI) | Sacrum/coccyx (posterior pelvic tilt), posterior thighs |
| Side-lying | Greater trochanter | Lateral malleolus, lateral knee, ear, ribs |
| Prone | Anterior knee | Chest, ASIS, chin/face |

## 12.5 Risk Factors

**[BOARD]**

### Injury-Related Risk Factors

- **Completeness of injury** -- AIS A (motor and sensory complete) injuries carry the highest risk due to total loss of protective sensation and voluntary motor function below the injury level. The risk decreases with increasing neurologic preservation (AIS B > C > D).
- **Level of injury** -- Higher levels of injury are associated with greater risk, reflecting greater extent of paralysis, impaired respiratory function (affecting tissue oxygenation), and greater autonomic dysfunction.
- **Immobility** -- The fundamental prerequisite for pressure injury development. Complete paralysis eliminates spontaneous repositioning movements.

### Systemic Risk Factors

- **Malnutrition** -- Serum albumin less than 3.5 g/dL and prealbumin less than 15 mg/dL indicate nutritional insufficiency that impairs wound healing and tissue integrity. Protein-calorie malnutrition reduces collagen synthesis, fibroblast proliferation, and immune function.
- **Anemia** -- Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity compromises tissue oxygenation and wound healing.
- **Moisture** -- Incontinence of urine and/or stool causes maceration of the skin, reduces skin tensile strength, and increases friction coefficient. Incontinence-associated dermatitis (IAD) predisposes to pressure injury development.
- **Diabetes mellitus** -- Impairs microvascular function, immune response, and wound healing.
- **Smoking** -- Nicotine causes vasoconstriction, reduces tissue oxygenation, impairs collagen synthesis, and compromises immune function. Smoking is a strong independent risk factor for pressure injury development and recurrence.
- **Advanced age** -- Age-related changes in skin elasticity, subcutaneous fat thickness, microvascular function, and nutritional status increase susceptibility.
- **Previous pressure injury** -- A history of prior pressure injury is one of the strongest predictors of future pressure injury development. Scar tissue from healed injuries has lower tensile strength and reduced vascularity compared to native tissue, making these sites particularly vulnerable to recurrence.

### Biomechanical Risk Factors

- **Spasticity** -- Involuntary muscle spasms may cause shearing forces, dislodge positioning devices, and create uneven pressure distribution. However, some degree of spasticity may paradoxically provide a protective effect through preservation of muscle bulk.
- **Flaccidity** -- Complete flaccidity results in maximal muscle atrophy, eliminating the muscular cushion between bony prominences and skin.
- **Contractures** -- Fixed joint deformities create abnormal pressure points and complicate positioning.

### Psychosocial Risk Factors

- Depression, substance abuse, social isolation, and limited access to caregiver support or proper equipment all increase pressure injury risk through reduced adherence to preventive behaviors.

## 12.6 Risk Assessment: The Braden Scale

**[BOARD]**

The Braden Scale for Predicting Pressure Sore Risk is the most widely validated and utilized pressure injury risk assessment tool in clinical practice. It consists of six subscales, each reflecting a distinct aspect of pressure injury risk.

### Subscales and Scoring

**Table 12.3: Braden Scale Subscales**

| Subscale | Scoring Range | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| Sensory perception | 1-4 | Ability to respond meaningfully to pressure-related discomfort |
| Moisture | 1-4 | Degree to which skin is exposed to moisture |
| Activity | 1-4 | Degree of physical activity (bedfast, chairfast, walks occasionally, walks frequently) |
| Mobility | 1-4 | Ability to change and control body position |
| Nutrition | 1-4 | Usual food intake pattern and adequacy |
| Friction and shear | 1-3 | Ability to assist with position changes, degree of sliding |

- Lower scores on each subscale indicate greater risk
- Friction and shear is scored on a 1 to 3 scale (maximum 3) while all other subscales are scored 1 to 4

### Total Score Interpretation

- **Total score range:** 6 to 23
- **19-23:** No risk (not at risk)
- **15-18:** At risk (mild risk)
- **13-14:** Moderate risk
- **10-12:** High risk
- **9 or below:** Very high risk (severe risk)

### Application in SCI

The Braden Scale was developed for general hospital populations and has certain limitations in SCI. Most individuals with complete SCI will automatically score very low on sensory perception (1), mobility (1-2), and activity (1-2) subscales, clustering them in the high to very high risk categories regardless of other factors. This creates a ceiling effect that limits the discriminant validity of the Braden Scale in SCI populations. Nevertheless, the Braden Scale remains the most commonly used tool and provides a structured framework for identifying modifiable risk factors, particularly nutrition, moisture, and friction/shear.

The Spinal Cord Injury Pressure Ulcer Scale (SCIPUS) and SCIPUS-A were developed specifically for the SCI population but have not achieved the widespread adoption of the Braden Scale.

**Clinical Pearl 12.2:** In individuals with SCI, the Braden Scale is useful primarily for identifying modifiable risk factors (nutrition, moisture, friction/shear) rather than for risk stratification, since nearly all individuals with motor-complete SCI score in the high or very high risk categories based on the immobility and sensory subscales alone.

## 12.7 Prevention

**[BOARD]**

Pressure injury prevention is the single most important aspect of skin care in SCI. Prevention is far more effective, less costly, and less morbid than treatment of established injuries.

### Pressure Redistribution

**Wheelchair pressure relief:**
- **Frequency:** Every 15 to 30 minutes during sitting
- **Techniques:**
  - Wheelchair push-ups (depression lifts): Lifting the body off the seat by pushing down on the armrests or seat. Requires sufficient upper extremity strength (generally C7 or below for complete injuries). Duration should be at least 30 to 60 seconds to allow tissue reperfusion.
  - Power tilt/recline: For individuals using power wheelchairs, a tilt of at least 45 degrees for 1 to 2 minutes is recommended to effectively redistribute ischial pressure. Recline alone (backrest angle change) without tilt is less effective due to shear forces generated at the sacrum.
  - Lateral weight shifts: Shifting body weight from one ischial tuberosity to the other, though this provides incomplete unloading of the weight-bearing side.
  - Forward lean: Leaning forward over the thighs to unload the ischial tuberosities. Effective but may be difficult for individuals with poor trunk control.

**Bed repositioning:**
- Turn every 2 hours when in bed (standard nursing protocol)
- Use 30-degree lateral tilt positioning (lateral decubitus) to alternate pressure points
- Avoid positioning directly on the greater trochanter (use 30-degree tilt rather than 90-degree side-lying)
- Elevate heels off the bed using heel-suspension devices or pillows under the calves (not under the heels themselves, which concentrates pressure on the Achilles tendon)
- Limit head-of-bed elevation to less than 30 degrees when possible to minimize sacral shear

### Seating and Support Surface Selection

**Wheelchair cushions:**
- **ROHO air-cell cushions** -- Interconnected air cells conform to body contours and equalize pressure distribution. Require regular inflation checks and maintenance. Available in low-profile, mid-profile, and high-profile configurations.
- **Jay gel cushions** -- Gel-based cushions that provide contouring and pressure redistribution with a solid base for stability. May be combined with foam components (hybrid design).
- **Hybrid cushions** -- Combine foam, gel, and/or air components to optimize pressure redistribution, stability, and comfort.
- **Foam cushions** -- Contoured or flat foam cushions. Less effective for pressure redistribution than air or gel options but may be appropriate for lower-risk individuals. Standard foam cushions should generally be avoided for complete SCI.
- Cushion selection should be individualized based on pressure mapping, patient body habitus, postural stability needs, transfer method, and lifestyle requirements.

**Mattresses and bed surfaces:**
- **Alternating pressure mattresses** -- Mechanically alternate inflation and deflation of air cells to periodically unload different body regions.
- **Low-air-loss mattresses** -- Maintain a continuous flow of air through a permeable surface, reducing moisture accumulation and providing pressure redistribution.
- **Air-fluidized (Clinitron) beds** -- Warm air circulates through silicone-coated microspheres, creating a fluid-like environment that minimizes pressure and shear. Reserved for patients with extensive or multiple pressure injuries, postoperative flap care, or burns.
- Standard hospital mattresses are inadequate for individuals with SCI and should be replaced with pressure-redistributing surfaces.

### Nutritional Optimization

Adequate nutrition is essential for tissue integrity and wound healing.

- **Protein:** 1.25 to 1.5 g/kg/day for individuals at risk for or with existing pressure injuries. Protein is critical for collagen synthesis and immune function.
- **Vitamin C:** 250 to 500 mg twice daily. Vitamin C is required for collagen cross-linking and hydroxylation, and deficiency impairs wound healing.
- **Zinc:** 220 mg daily (providing approximately 50 mg elemental zinc). Zinc is a cofactor for metalloproteinases and other enzymes involved in wound repair.
- **Caloric intake:** Adequate total caloric intake to prevent catabolism and support tissue repair. Indirect calorimetry can guide individual caloric needs.
- **Monitoring:** Serial serum albumin (goal greater than 3.5 g/dL, though albumin has a long half-life of approximately 20 days and is a delayed marker) and prealbumin (goal greater than 15 mg/dL, with a shorter half-life of 2 to 3 days allowing more responsive monitoring of nutritional status).

### Moisture Management

- **Incontinence management:** Optimized neurogenic bladder and bowel programs to minimize skin exposure to urine and stool. Use of absorbent pads, barrier creams (dimethicone, zinc oxide, petrolatum-based products), and external collection devices.
- **Perspiration management:** Breathable clothing and cushion covers, moisture-wicking fabrics, and management of the thermoregulatory dysfunction that accompanies SCI.

### Skin Inspection

- **Frequency:** Twice daily -- upon arising and before bed
- **Technique:** Visual inspection of all pressure-prone areas. A long-handled mirror should be used for areas the individual cannot directly visualize (sacrum, ischial tuberosities, posterior thighs, heels).
- **Key indicators:** Non-blanchable erythema, changes in skin temperature or firmness, any break in skin integrity, moisture damage, discoloration.
- **Patient education:** Self-inspection using mirrors is a critical self-management skill taught during rehabilitation. Individuals who cannot perform self-inspection must have a reliable caregiver or partner trained in skin assessment.

**Clinical Pearl 12.3:** Heel pressure injuries can be prevented almost entirely by ensuring that heels are elevated off the bed surface at all times. Heel suspension devices or a pillow placed lengthwise under the calves (floating the heels) should be standard practice for all individuals with SCI during bed rest.

## 12.8 Wound Assessment and Management

**[BOARD]**

### Wound Assessment

Systematic wound assessment should document:

- **Location** -- Anatomic site
- **Stage** -- NPIAP stage
- **Dimensions** -- Length, width, and depth in centimeters (using a consistent measurement technique, e.g., head-to-toe orientation for length)
- **Wound bed** -- Percentage of viable tissue (granulation, epithelium) versus non-viable tissue (slough, eschar)
- **Wound edges** -- Condition (attached, rolled/epibole, undermined, tunneling with direction and depth)
- **Exudate** -- Amount (none, scant, small, moderate, large), type (serous, sanguineous, serosanguineous, purulent)
- **Periwound skin** -- Integrity, maceration, erythema, induration, callus formation
- **Signs of infection** -- Erythema, warmth, tenderness (if sensation present), purulent drainage, malodor, increasing size, delayed healing, friable granulation tissue

### Wound Cleansing

- Normal saline is the standard wound cleanser for most pressure injuries
- Low-pressure irrigation (4 to 15 psi) is recommended to effectively remove surface debris and bacteria without causing trauma to healthy granulation tissue. A 35 mL syringe with a 19-gauge needle or angiocatheter provides approximately 8 psi, which is within the recommended range.
- Cytotoxic agents (full-strength povidone-iodine, hydrogen peroxide, sodium hypochlorite/Dakin solution) should be avoided on clean granulating wounds as they damage fibroblasts and impair healing. Dilute Dakin solution (0.025%) may have a role in heavily contaminated or infected wounds for limited periods.

### Debridement

Removal of necrotic tissue is essential for wound healing, as devitalized tissue supports bacterial proliferation, produces inflammatory mediators, and acts as a physical barrier to granulation and epithelialization.

**Methods of debridement:**

1. **Sharp/surgical debridement** -- The most rapid and definitive method. Performed at the bedside (conservative sharp debridement) or in the operating room (surgical debridement) using scalpel, scissors, or curette. Indicated for extensive necrotic tissue, purulent collections, or clinical urgency. Contraindicated over exposed blood vessels or in the presence of coagulopathy (relative).

2. **Autolytic debridement** -- The body's own enzymatic processes selectively liquefy necrotic tissue. Facilitated by moisture-retentive dressings (hydrogels, hydrocolloids, transparent films) that create a moist wound environment. Slowest method but highly selective (preserves viable tissue). Appropriate for wounds with small amounts of slough and no clinical urgency.

3. **Enzymatic debridement** -- Application of exogenous proteolytic enzymes (collagenase/Santyl is the only enzymatic debriding agent currently available in the United States) to the wound bed. Collagenase selectively degrades denatured collagen in necrotic tissue. Cross-hatching of eschar before application enhances enzyme penetration. Requires a moist wound environment; avoid concurrent use of silver-containing dressings as silver inactivates collagenase.

4. **Mechanical debridement** -- Physical removal of devitalized tissue through wet-to-dry dressings (non-selective; removes both viable and non-viable tissue), wound irrigation, or hydrotherapy. Wet-to-dry dressings are non-selective and painful (in patients with sensation); they have largely been replaced by more selective methods. Pulsed lavage with suction provides controlled mechanical debridement with simultaneous irrigation.

### Moist Wound Healing

Maintaining a moist wound environment accelerates healing by:

- Promoting keratinocyte migration and re-epithelialization
- Facilitating autolytic debridement of necrotic tissue
- Supporting granulation tissue formation
- Preventing wound desiccation and eschar formation
- Maintaining growth factor and cytokine bioavailability at the wound surface

### Dressing Selection

Dressing selection is guided by wound characteristics:

**Table 12.4: Dressing Selection Guide**

| Wound Characteristic | Recommended Dressing Type | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Dry wound bed, minimal exudate | Hydrogel (amorphous or sheet) | Donates moisture, promotes autolysis |
| Moderate exudate | Foam dressings | Absorb exudate while maintaining moist environment |
| Heavy exudate | Alginate, hydrofiber | High absorptive capacity, gel when saturated |
| Shallow, clean, minimal exudate | Hydrocolloid | Moisture-retentive, autolytic debridement |
| Infected or critically colonized | Silver-containing dressings | Broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity |
| Deep wound with dead space | Wound packing (alginate rope, hydrofiber rope) | Fill dead space, prevent premature surface closure |
| Granulating wound, fragile tissue | Silicone-faced foam, contact layer | Atraumatic removal, protect delicate tissue |

### Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

Negative pressure wound therapy (vacuum-assisted closure, VAC therapy) applies controlled sub-atmospheric pressure (typically -75 to -125 mmHg) to the wound bed through a sealed foam or gauze interface connected to a suction device.

**Mechanism of action:**
- Removes excess wound exudate and reduces edema
- Promotes granulation tissue formation through mechanical stimulation (microdeformation of wound bed cells)
- Reduces wound surface area through macro-deformation (wound contraction)
- Reduces bacterial bioburden
- Enhances local blood flow to the wound margins

**Indications in SCI:**
- Stage 3 and Stage 4 pressure injuries with adequate debridement
- Preparation of wound bed for surgical flap closure
- Postoperative wound management after flap surgery (in some protocols)
- Large, exudative wounds that require frequent dressing changes

**Contraindications:**
- Unexplored fistulas to organs or body cavities
- Necrotic tissue with eschar (requires debridement first)
- Untreated osteomyelitis (relative contraindication)
- Malignancy in the wound bed
- Exposed major blood vessels (risk of hemorrhage)

## 12.9 Surgical Management

**[BOARD]**

Surgical intervention is indicated for Stage 3 and Stage 4 pressure injuries that fail to respond to conservative wound management after an adequate trial (typically 2 to 4 weeks of optimized care) or when the wound size, depth, or location makes conservative healing impractical.

### Preoperative Optimization

Before surgical repair, several conditions must be addressed:

1. **Wound bed preparation** -- Infection must be controlled, necrotic tissue debrided, and the wound bed should have healthy granulation tissue (unless the surgical plan includes excision of the entire wound).
2. **Nutritional optimization** -- Albumin greater than 3.0 g/dL (ideally greater than 3.5), prealbumin greater than 15 mg/dL, and adequate caloric and protein intake.
3. **Infection control** -- Active wound infection, osteomyelitis, and UTIs should be treated before surgery.
4. **Spasticity management** -- Uncontrolled spasticity increases the risk of flap dehiscence and must be optimized preoperatively.
5. **Smoking cessation** -- Smoking significantly impairs flap survival. A minimum of 4 to 6 weeks of smoking cessation before surgery is recommended.
6. **Medical optimization** -- Control of diabetes, treatment of anemia, optimization of bowel and bladder programs.

### Flap Types by Anatomic Location

**Table 12.5: Common Flap Procedures by Pressure Injury Location**

| Location | Flap Options | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Ischial tuberosity | Gluteus maximus myocutaneous flap; Hamstring V-Y advancement flap; Posterior thigh fasciocutaneous flap; Gracilis myocutaneous flap | Most common PI requiring surgery in SCI; musculocutaneous flaps preferred for durability; hamstring V-Y advancement preserves gluteus for future revision |
| Sacrum | Gluteus maximus rotation flap (unilateral or bilateral); Superior gluteal artery perforator (SGAP) flap | Bilateral gluteus rotation provides robust tissue bulk; preserve vascularity for potential re-advancement |
| Greater trochanter | Tensor fasciae latae (TFL) flap; Vastus lateralis myocutaneous flap; Rectus femoris flap | TFL is workhorse flap for trochanteric injuries; V-Y advancement allows re-advancement if needed |

### Postoperative Protocol

The postoperative protocol following pressure injury flap surgery is as critical as the surgery itself. A structured, graduated approach is essential to protect the flap during healing:

1. **Immediate postoperative period (weeks 0-2):**
   - **Bed rest** on an air-fluidized (Clinitron) bed for 2 to 4 weeks
   - No sitting; no pressure on the flap site
   - Wound drains typically remain in place for 5 to 10 days or until output falls below 30 mL per 24 hours
   - Strict bowel program management to minimize fecal contamination of pelvic wounds
   - Nutritional support continues
   - DVT prophylaxis

2. **Progressive sitting program (beginning approximately week 2-3):**
   - Initiate sitting at 15 minutes twice daily
   - Increase sitting time by 15 minutes every 3 days (if no signs of flap compromise)
   - **Inspect the flap after EVERY sitting session** for erythema, swelling, warmth, suture line separation, drainage, or blanching
   - If any signs of flap compromise are noted, discontinue sitting and reassess
   - Goal: gradual return to full sitting tolerance over 4 to 8 weeks

3. **Discharge and long-term care:**
   - Proper seating system with appropriate pressure-redistributing cushion
   - Reinforce pressure relief schedule and skin inspection protocols
   - Address modifiable risk factors for recurrence

### Recurrence Rates

Pressure injury recurrence following flap surgery is a major challenge, with reported rates of 30 to 60 percent depending on the series, follow-up duration, and patient population. Higher recurrence rates are associated with:

- Inadequate postoperative compliance with activity restrictions
- Persistent or unaddressed risk factors (smoking, malnutrition, poorly fitting seating equipment)
- Psychosocial factors (substance use, depression, lack of caregiver support)
- Flap design that limits options for future revision

The high recurrence rate underscores the critical importance of comprehensive patient education, risk factor modification, and long-term follow-up as integral components of surgical management.

**Clinical Pearl 12.4:** Surgical flap design should always consider the possibility of future recurrence and revision. Preserving adjacent tissue and donor sites for potential future flap procedures is a key principle of reconstructive planning in this population.

## 12.10 Complications of Pressure Injuries

**[BOARD]**

### Osteomyelitis

Osteomyelitis (infection of the underlying bone) is a frequent complication of deep pressure injuries, particularly at the ischial tuberosities, sacrum, and greater trochanters where Stage 4 injuries expose or closely approximate bone.

**Diagnosis:**

- **Probe-to-bone test** -- Inserting a sterile metal probe through the wound to palpate bone. A positive probe-to-bone test (metal probe contacts bone or gritty surface) has a positive predictive value (PPV) of approximately 89 percent for osteomyelitis in the setting of a pressure injury. This simple bedside test is among the most useful initial assessments.
- **Laboratory markers** -- Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP) are typically elevated but are nonspecific. White blood cell count may or may not be elevated.
- **Imaging:**
  - Plain radiographs may show periosteal reaction, cortical destruction, or sequestra but have limited sensitivity, particularly early in the disease course.
  - **MRI** is the imaging gold standard for osteomyelitis, with high sensitivity (approximately 90 percent) and specificity (approximately 80 percent) for detecting bone marrow edema, cortical disruption, and soft tissue involvement. However, MRI interpretation can be challenging in the setting of chronic pressure injuries due to reactive bone changes.
  - CT may be useful for surgical planning and assessment of bony architecture.
  - Nuclear medicine studies (triple-phase bone scan, WBC-labeled scans) can be adjunctive but lack specificity in the setting of chronic wounds.
- **Bone biopsy** -- The definitive diagnostic test. Percutaneous or open bone biopsy provides tissue for both histopathologic examination and microbiologic culture with sensitivities, guiding targeted antibiotic therapy. Bone biopsy should ideally be obtained through intact skin (not through the wound bed) to minimize contamination by wound flora.

**Treatment:**
- Prolonged antibiotic therapy (typically 6 to 8 weeks of pathogen-directed parenteral therapy, followed by oral suppressive therapy in some cases)
- Surgical debridement of infected bone, often combined with flap coverage
- Optimization of wound healing environment and nutritional status

### Sepsis

Pressure injuries are a leading source of septicemia in persons with SCI. Deep, heavily colonized, or infected wounds can serve as portals of entry for systemic infection, particularly in immunocompromised or malnourished individuals. Signs of systemic infection (fever, tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status, leukocytosis or leukopenia) in a patient with SCI and a pressure injury should prompt urgent wound evaluation, blood cultures, and broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy.

### Marjolin Ulcer

Marjolin ulcer refers to the malignant transformation (typically squamous cell carcinoma, SCC) that can occur in chronic, non-healing wounds or scars of long duration. The risk increases significantly in wounds present for more than 10 years.

**Key points:**
- Any chronic pressure injury that undergoes a change in character (new nodularity, raised or rolled wound edges, rapid growth, increased pain, foul-smelling exudate, hemorrhagic granulation tissue, or failure to respond to appropriate wound care) should be biopsied to evaluate for malignancy.
- SCC arising in chronic wounds tends to be more aggressive and to metastasize at higher rates than primary cutaneous SCC.
- Wide local excision with histologic margin evaluation is the standard treatment.

### Amyloidosis

Secondary (AA) amyloidosis is a rare but potentially fatal complication of chronic pressure injuries in SCI. Prolonged systemic inflammation from chronic wounds stimulates hepatic production of serum amyloid A (SAA), which can deposit as amyloid fibrils in multiple organs, particularly the kidneys (leading to nephrotic syndrome and renal failure), liver, and spleen.

- Renal amyloidosis is the most clinically significant manifestation, presenting with proteinuria, nephrotic syndrome, and progressive renal insufficiency.
- The diagnosis is confirmed by tissue biopsy (rectal, renal, or fat pad biopsy) demonstrating amyloid deposits with Congo red staining and apple-green birefringence under polarized light.
- Treatment requires elimination of the chronic inflammatory stimulus (healing or surgical repair of pressure injuries), systemic anti-inflammatory therapy, and supportive management of organ dysfunction.

## 12.11 Advanced Wound Healing Technologies

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

### Growth Factors

Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) is the most extensively studied growth factor for wound healing. Becaplermin (recombinant human PDGF-BB, Regranex) is FDA-approved for diabetic foot ulcers and has been investigated in pressure injury management, though evidence for efficacy specifically in SCI-related pressure injuries remains limited.

Other growth factors under investigation include:

- **Epidermal growth factor (EGF)** -- Stimulates keratinocyte proliferation and migration
- **Fibroblast growth factor (FGF)** -- Promotes angiogenesis and fibroblast proliferation
- **Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)** -- Enhances angiogenesis
- **Platelet-rich plasma (PRP)** -- Autologous concentrate of growth factors derived from the patient's own blood. Several small studies suggest PRP may enhance healing of chronic pressure injuries, though large randomized trials are lacking.

### Skin Substitutes

Bioengineered skin substitutes and dermal matrices offer an alternative or adjunct to traditional wound care and surgical flap closure:

- **Acellular dermal matrices** (e.g., AlloDerm, Integra) -- Provide a structural scaffold that promotes host cell ingrowth, neovascularization, and tissue regeneration. Integra is a bilayer construct consisting of a bovine collagen-glycosaminoglycan dermal template and a silicone epidermal layer. It has been used to augment wound bed preparation before skin grafting or to provide additional tissue bulk in areas of soft tissue deficiency.
- **Cellular skin substitutes** -- Contain living cells (keratinocytes, fibroblasts, or both) that produce growth factors and extracellular matrix components. Examples include Apligraf (bilayer living skin substitute) and Dermagraft (human dermal fibroblasts on a bioabsorbable scaffold). These products are primarily approved for diabetic and venous ulcers, with limited evidence in pressure injuries.

### Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBOT)

Hyperbaric oxygen therapy involves intermittent exposure to 100 percent oxygen at pressures greater than 1 atmosphere absolute (typically 2.0 to 2.4 ATA) in a pressurized chamber. HBOT increases tissue oxygen tension, promotes angiogenesis, enhances leukocyte bactericidal activity, and stimulates collagen synthesis.

Evidence for HBOT in pressure injury management:

- The evidence base for HBOT in SCI-related pressure injuries is limited and of low quality
- HBOT may have a role in enhancing healing of chronic, recalcitrant wounds that have failed conventional therapy, particularly in the setting of compromised vascularity or chronic osteomyelitis
- HBOT is not recommended as a primary or first-line treatment for pressure injuries
- Practical limitations include cost, need for specialized facilities, and logistical challenges of transporting individuals with SCI to hyperbaric chambers
- Risks include barotrauma (ear, sinus, pulmonary), oxygen toxicity (seizures), and fire hazard in the hyperbaric environment

### Electrical Stimulation

Electrical stimulation (ES) using direct current or pulsed current has demonstrated modest evidence for enhancing healing of chronic pressure injuries. The proposed mechanisms include promotion of cellular migration toward the wound, enhancement of fibroblast proliferation, stimulation of angiogenesis, and bactericidal effects. Several clinical practice guidelines conditionally recommend ES as an adjunct for pressure injuries that have not responded to conventional care.

## 12.12 Patient and Caregiver Education

**[BOARD]**

Education of patients, families, and caregivers is the foundation of long-term pressure injury prevention. Key educational components include:

1. **Understanding of risk** -- Clear explanation of why individuals with SCI are at heightened risk and the potentially life-threatening nature of pressure injuries.
2. **Skin inspection technique** -- Demonstration and return demonstration of comprehensive skin assessment using a mirror.
3. **Pressure relief practices** -- Instruction and practice in specific pressure relief techniques appropriate to the individual's functional level.
4. **Nutritional awareness** -- Understanding the role of adequate protein, calories, vitamins, and hydration in skin integrity.
5. **Equipment use** -- Proper use and maintenance of wheelchair cushions (including ROHO inflation checks), mattresses, and positioning devices.
6. **Early recognition** -- Identification of Stage 1 pressure injuries and understanding of the importance of immediate intervention (complete offloading) when early signs are detected.
7. **When to seek medical attention** -- Clear guidelines for when to contact healthcare providers regarding skin changes.

---

## Summary

Pressure injuries remain one of the most significant complications facing individuals with spinal cord injury, affecting quality of life, functional independence, and survival. The NPIAP staging system provides a standardized classification framework, with the critical principle that pressure injuries heal by secondary intention and should never be reverse-staged. Prevention through systematic pressure redistribution, appropriate seating and support surfaces, nutritional optimization, moisture management, and patient education represents the most effective strategy. When prevention fails, wound management follows principles of moist wound healing, appropriate debridement, and dressing selection matched to wound characteristics. Surgical flap repair offers definitive closure for advanced injuries but carries substantial recurrence rates of 30 to 60 percent, underscoring the lifelong nature of pressure injury risk in SCI. Vigilance for complications including osteomyelitis, sepsis, and malignant transformation is essential in the management of chronic wounds.
