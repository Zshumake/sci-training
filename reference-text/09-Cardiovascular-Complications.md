## 9.1 Introduction `[BOARD]`

Cardiovascular complications are among the most dangerous and prevalent sequelae of spinal cord injury. The disruption of autonomic nervous system pathways --- particularly the sympathetic efferents arising from the thoracolumbar cord (T1-L2) --- produces a constellation of cardiovascular disturbances that vary by injury level, completeness, and chronicity. These include orthostatic hypotension, autonomic dysreflexia, bradycardia, venous thromboembolism, and thermoregulatory failure.

### 9.1.1 Key Neurologic Levels for Cardiovascular Autonomic Function

| Level | Significance |
|---|---|
| **T1-T4** | Cardiac sympathetic outflow: sympathetic innervation to the heart arises from T1-T4; loss produces unopposed vagal tone → bradycardia |
| **T6 and above** | Autonomic dysreflexia risk: the critical threshold; injuries at T6 or above carry risk for AD and significant orthostatic hypotension |
| **T8 and above** | Thermoregulation loss: sympathetic control of sweating and cutaneous vasomotor tone is impaired; "temp-EIGHT-ture" mnemonic |
| **T1-L2** | Total sympathetic outflow: all preganglionic sympathetic neurons reside in the intermediolateral cell column at T1-L2 |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Three critical cardiovascular levels to remember: T1-T4 = cardiac sympathetic outflow (bradycardia risk), T6 = autonomic dysreflexia threshold, T8 = thermoregulation ("temp-EIGHT-ture").

## 9.2 Autonomic Dysreflexia `[BOARD]`

### 9.2.1 Definition

Autonomic dysreflexia (AD) is a potentially life-threatening syndrome of massive, uninhibited sympathetic discharge occurring in response to a noxious stimulus below the level of injury in patients with SCI at **T6 or above**. It is characterized by acute, severe hypertension (systolic blood pressure [SBP] elevation ≥20 mmHg above baseline in adults, ≥15 mmHg in adolescents, ≥15 mmHg in children) accompanied by signs and symptoms of autonomic imbalance.

### 9.2.2 Epidemiology

- Occurs in injuries at **T6 or above** (85-90% of cases)
- More common in **complete injuries** than incomplete
- Rare during the acute spinal shock phase; typically emerges after spinal shock resolution (3-6 months post-injury)
- Prevalence: Up to 70-90% of patients with cervical or high thoracic complete SCI experience at least one episode
- Frequency: Some patients experience multiple episodes daily

### 9.2.3 Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of AD involves a vicious cycle of uninhibited sympathetic activation with inadequate central modulation:

1. **Noxious stimulus below the level of injury** (e.g., bladder distension, fecal impaction) generates sensory afferent signals that ascend through the dorsal columns and spinothalamic tracts toward the brain

2. **At the spinal cord level below the injury**, these afferent signals activate a massive sympathetic reflex discharge via the splanchnic sympathetic outflow. Because the descending inhibitory pathways from the brainstem (particularly the vasomotor center) are interrupted by the spinal cord lesion, this sympathetic discharge is **uninhibited and excessive**

3. **Widespread vasoconstriction below the level of injury** occurs, driven by the unopposed sympathetic outflow acting on alpha-1 adrenergic receptors in the splanchnic and peripheral vasculature. This produces severe hypertension

4. **Baroreceptor detection:** The carotid and aortic baroreceptors detect the acute hypertension and relay this information to the brainstem vasomotor center via intact cranial nerves (CN IX and X)

5. **Attempted compensatory response:** The brainstem attempts to counteract the hypertension by: (a) increasing vagal (parasympathetic) output to the heart → **bradycardia** (though tachycardia may also occur); (b) sending descending sympathoinhibitory signals to the spinal cord. However, these descending signals **cannot pass through the spinal cord lesion** and therefore cannot reach the sympathetic neurons below the injury to inhibit the vasoconstriction

6. **Vasodilation ABOVE the level of injury only:** The compensatory vasodilation and sweating occur only above the level of injury (where descending pathways are intact), producing flushing, diaphoresis, and nasal congestion in the head, neck, and upper body --- while severe vasoconstriction persists below the level

7. **Result:** Sustained, severe hypertension with compensatory bradycardia and vasodilation confined to the territory above the lesion

### 9.2.4 Triggers

The most common triggers are distension or irritation of pelvic viscera. Bladder-related causes account for approximately 75-85% of episodes.

| Category | Specific Triggers |
|---|---|
| **Bladder** (most common, ~75-85%) | Distension (blocked catheter, missed CIC), UTI, bladder stones, catheter insertion/manipulation, cystoscopy |
| **Bowel** (~15-20%) | Fecal impaction, distension, digital stimulation, suppository insertion, hemorrhoids, anal fissure |
| **Skin** | Pressure injury, ingrown toenail, tight clothing/shoes, burns, sunburn |
| **Musculoskeletal** | Fracture (may be occult), heterotopic ossification |
| **Reproductive** | Ejaculation, labor/delivery, menstruation, vaginal examination |
| **Other** | DVT/PE, surgical procedures, diagnostic procedures below the level |

> **Clinical Pearl:** When AD occurs, think "BLADDER FIRST." Bladder distension is the most common trigger (75-85% of cases). Check the catheter for obstruction, irrigate, or perform CIC immediately.

### 9.2.5 Clinical Presentation

**Above the level of injury (compensatory responses):**
- Severe, pounding headache (most common symptom)
- Flushing (facial and upper body)
- Profuse diaphoresis (above the level of injury)
- Nasal congestion
- Blurred vision
- Piloerection above the level

**Below the level of injury (sympathetic overactivity):**
- Pallor (vasoconstriction)
- Cool, dry skin (no sweating below the level)
- Piloerection (goosebumps) below the level

**Cardiovascular:**
- Severe hypertension (SBP may exceed 250-300 mmHg)
- Bradycardia (most common, reflex vagal response)
- Tachycardia (may occur instead in some patients)

**Danger:** Uncontrolled hypertension can cause **intracranial hemorrhage, seizures, myocardial ischemia/infarction, retinal hemorrhage, pulmonary edema, and death**.

### 9.2.6 Emergency Management

Management follows a systematic approach focused on identifying and eliminating the noxious stimulus while controlling blood pressure:

**Step 1: Sit the patient up (if supine)**
- Elevating the head of the bed or sitting the patient upright produces an immediate orthostatic drop in blood pressure through venous pooling in the lower extremities
- Dangle the legs over the bedside if possible

**Step 2: Loosen all constrictive clothing, devices, and straps**
- Remove abdominal binder, leg straps, compression stockings, tight shoes
- Loosen any clothing that may be a source of cutaneous stimulation

**Step 3: Monitor blood pressure every 2-5 minutes**

**Step 4: Identify and eliminate the noxious stimulus**

- **Check the bladder FIRST:**
  - If catheterized: Check for kinks, obstruction, or blockage; irrigate with small volumes of room-temperature saline; replace the catheter if irrigation fails
  - If not catheterized: Perform CIC immediately to empty the bladder
  - Before catheterization: Apply 2% lidocaine jelly to the urethra and wait 2-3 minutes to minimize further afferent stimulation during catheter insertion

- **Check the bowel:**
  - If the bladder is not the cause: Apply lidocaine jelly to the rectum and gently perform a digital rectal exam to check for impaction
  - If stool is present: Gently disimpact (using anesthetic jelly to minimize stimulation)
  - If the bowel program triggered the episode: Stop the bowel program and manage the AD first

- **Check the skin:**
  - Inspect for pressure injuries, ingrown toenails, burns, tight clothing/devices
  - Remove any identified source of cutaneous noxious stimulation

**Step 5: Pharmacologic management (if SBP remains elevated after stimulus removal)**

| Medication | Class | Dose | Route | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nitropaste (nitroglycerin ointment) | Nitrate vasodilator | 1 inch (2.5 cm) applied to skin above level | Topical | First-line; easily removed if BP drops; onset 5-10 min |
| Nifedipine (immediate release) | Calcium channel blocker | 10 mg | PO (bite and swallow, NOT sublingual) | Rapid onset; caution with precipitous drops |
| Captopril | ACE inhibitor | 25 mg | PO/sublingual | Short-acting; useful alternative |
| Hydralazine | Direct vasodilator | 10-20 mg | IV | For severe refractory cases |
| Prazosin | Alpha-1 blocker | 1 mg | PO | Prophylactic use in recurrent AD |
| Clonidine | Alpha-2 agonist | 0.1-0.2 mg | PO | Prophylactic use |

**Step 6: If blood pressure remains dangerously elevated despite above measures**
- IV nitroprusside or labetalol drip in an ICU setting with continuous arterial monitoring
- Transfer to emergency department / intensive care if not already in a monitored setting

**Step 7: After resolution**
- Monitor for recurrence for at least 2 hours
- Document the episode and the identified trigger
- Develop a prevention plan to address the trigger

### 9.2.7 Prevention

- Reliable bladder management (CIC on schedule, catheter care)
- Regular bowel program to prevent impaction
- Skin surveillance to prevent pressure injuries
- Patient and caregiver education on recognition, triggers, and emergency response
- AD wallet card or medical alert identification
- Lidocaine jelly before bladder and bowel instrumentation
- Prophylactic medications (prazosin, nifedipine, clonidine) for patients with recurrent AD during known triggers (e.g., bowel program)

> **Clinical Pearl:** AD is a medical emergency. The immediate priorities are: (1) sit the patient up, (2) check the bladder (most common trigger), (3) check the bowel, (4) use antihypertensives if the stimulus cannot be identified or BP remains elevated. Uncontrolled AD can cause stroke, MI, or death.

### 9.2.8 AD During Pregnancy and Labor `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

AD is particularly dangerous during labor and delivery, as uterine contractions provide a continuous noxious stimulus below the level of injury. Epidural anesthesia is the treatment of choice, as it blocks the afferent limb of the reflex arc and prevents AD episodes during labor. All pregnant women with SCI at T6 or above should have an epidural placed early in labor, even if they cannot perceive contractions.

## 9.3 Orthostatic Hypotension `[BOARD]`

### 9.3.1 Definition

Orthostatic hypotension (OH) is defined as a sustained reduction in systolic blood pressure of at least 20 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure of at least 10 mmHg within 3 minutes of assuming an upright posture (or head-up tilt to ≥60 degrees).

### 9.3.2 Pathophysiology

In SCI, the mechanism of orthostatic hypotension involves disruption of the sympathetic efferent limb of the baroreceptor reflex:

1. **Loss of sympathetic outflow (T1-L2):** The sympathetic preganglionic neurons that mediate vasoconstriction reside in the intermediolateral cell column at T1-L2. SCI above these levels interrupts descending commands from the brainstem vasomotor center to these neurons

2. **Intact baroreceptor afferents:** The carotid and aortic baroreceptors still detect the drop in blood pressure and relay information to the brainstem via cranial nerves IX and X (which are intact)

3. **Interrupted efferent response:** The brainstem generates appropriate sympathoinhibitory/sympathoexcitatory commands, but these descending signals cannot traverse the spinal cord lesion to reach the sympathetic neurons below the injury. The result is an inability to vasoconstrict the splanchnic bed and lower extremity vasculature in response to orthostatic stress

4. **Venous pooling:** Blood pools in the capacitance vessels of the abdomen and lower extremities → decreased venous return → decreased cardiac output → decreased blood pressure

5. **Compensatory tachycardia:** Heart rate may increase (via intact vagal withdrawal and some cardiac sympathetic activation above the lesion), but this is insufficient to maintain adequate blood pressure in the face of massive venous pooling

### 9.3.3 Clinical Presentation

- Lightheadedness, dizziness
- Syncope or presyncope
- Visual dimming or blurring
- Pallor
- Nausea
- Generalized weakness and fatigue
- Cognitive impairment ("brain fog")

Symptoms are triggered by upright positioning (>60 degrees from horizontal) and may occur during tilt table sessions, transfers to wheelchair, or prolonged sitting.

### 9.3.4 Management

**Nonpharmacologic Measures:**

| Intervention | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Immediate repositioning (Trendelenburg / lower head) | Restores venous return by gravity |
| Elastic (graduated compression) stockings | Reduces venous pooling in lower extremities |
| Abdominal binder | Compresses the splanchnic vascular bed (the largest venous capacitance bed), reducing pooling |
| Tilt table accommodation | Gradual, progressive increase in tilt angle over days/weeks; allows adaptation |
| Increase oral fluid intake | Expands intravascular volume |
| Slow positional changes | Allows time for compensatory mechanisms |
| Avoidance of large meals | Postprandial splanchnic vasodilation worsens OH |

**Pharmacologic Management:**

| Medication | Class | Dose | Mechanism | Key Side Effects |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Salt tablets (NaCl) | Volume expander | 1 g QID (with meals and HS) | Increases intravascular volume via sodium/water retention | Hypertension (especially supine), edema |
| Midodrine | Alpha-1 adrenergic agonist | 2.5-10 mg TID | Direct vasoconstriction of arterioles and venous capacitance vessels | Supine hypertension, piloerection, urinary retention |
| Fludrocortisone | Synthetic mineralocorticoid | 0.05-0.2 mg daily | Enhances renal sodium and water reabsorption → expands plasma volume | Hypokalemia, supine hypertension, edema, CHF |
| Droxidopa | Norepinephrine prodrug | 100-600 mg TID | Converted to norepinephrine peripherally → vasoconstriction | Supine hypertension, headache, dizziness |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The abdominal binder is a dual-purpose device in SCI: it improves respiratory function when upright (supports diaphragm mechanics) AND helps prevent orthostatic hypotension (compresses the splanchnic vascular bed). However, remember to remove it when supine.

### 9.3.5 Relationship Between OH and AD

An important clinical paradox exists: as orthostatic hypotension improves over time (through gradual adaptation of autonomic reflexes and development of spasticity-mediated vascular tone), patients may begin to develop episodes of autonomic dysreflexia. The same injury that causes loss of vasomotor control (OH) also sets the stage for uninhibited sympathetic discharge (AD) once reflex pathways below the lesion become hyperexcitable.

## 9.4 Deep Vein Thrombosis and Pulmonary Embolism `[BOARD]`

### 9.4.1 Risk and Epidemiology

Venous thromboembolism (VTE) --- encompassing deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE) --- is one of the most dangerous acute complications of SCI. Without prophylaxis, DVT occurs in up to 40-100% of acute SCI patients (depending on detection method and timing). The highest risk period is the **first 2 weeks** after injury, with elevated risk persisting for 8-12 weeks.

**Pulmonary embolism** is the **leading cause of preventable death** in acute SCI.

### 9.4.2 Virchow's Triad in SCI

All three components of Virchow's triad are present in acute SCI, explaining the extraordinarily high VTE risk:

| Component | Mechanism in SCI |
|---|---|
| **Venous stasis** | Paralysis eliminates the skeletal muscle pump in the lower extremities; immobility prevents ambulation |
| **Hypercoagulable state** | Acute traumatic injury triggers a systemic inflammatory and procoagulant response; elevated Factor VIII, fibrinogen, and platelet activation |
| **Endothelial/vessel injury** | Direct trauma at the time of injury; may also occur from venous stasis and inflammatory mediators |

### 9.4.3 DVT Prophylaxis

The Consortium for Spinal Cord Medicine Clinical Practice Guidelines recommend the following approach (Consortium for Spinal Cord Medicine, 2016):

**Pharmacologic Prophylaxis:**

- **Low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH)** is preferred over unfractionated heparin (UFH)
  - **Enoxaparin** 30 mg BID or 40 mg daily (both regimens are equally effective and safe)
  - Alternative: Dalteparin 5,000 units daily
- **Initiate within 72 hours** of injury (once hemostasis is secured and no active hemorrhage)
- **Duration: minimum 8-12 weeks** (some guidelines recommend 12 weeks or until the patient is mobilized, whichever is longer)
- LMWH is associated with significantly fewer DVT and PE events and a trend toward less major bleeding compared to low-dose unfractionated heparin (LDUH)

**Mechanical Prophylaxis:**

- **Sequential compression devices (SCDs):** Intermittent pneumatic compression of the calves and/or thighs; recommended in combination with pharmacologic prophylaxis
- **Graduated compression stockings (GCS):** Passive compression of the lower extremities; less effective than SCDs alone but used adjunctively
- Mechanical prophylaxis alone is insufficient and should not be used as the sole method unless anticoagulation is contraindicated

**IVC Filter:**

- Indicated when anticoagulation is **contraindicated** (active hemorrhage, recent surgery with bleeding risk, hemorrhagic conditions)
- Retrievable IVC filters are preferred; should be removed when anticoagulation can be safely resumed
- IVC filters prevent PE but do not prevent DVT; they should be used as a bridge until pharmacologic prophylaxis can be initiated

### 9.4.4 DVT Diagnosis

- **Duplex ultrasonography** of the lower extremities is the standard screening and diagnostic test
- Screening duplex ultrasound is recommended at admission and periodically during the high-risk period (e.g., weekly for the first 2-4 weeks), though universal screening protocols vary by institution
- **D-dimer** is unreliable in acute SCI (elevated in most trauma patients regardless of DVT presence)
- Clinical signs (leg swelling, warmth, erythema) may be absent or unreliable in patients without sensation

### 9.4.5 Pulmonary Embolism

**Clinical Presentation in SCI:**

PE may present atypically in SCI patients. Classic symptoms that depend on sensation (pleuritic chest pain) may be absent in patients with high thoracic or cervical injuries. The clinician must maintain a high index of suspicion for atypical presentations:

- Unexplained **tachycardia** (may be the only sign)
- **Hypoxia** (SpO2 decline)
- **Autonomic dysreflexia** (PE as a noxious visceral stimulus in patients at T6 or above)
- Dyspnea (if the patient can perceive it)
- Cardiovascular collapse in massive PE

**Diagnosis:**

- CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) is the diagnostic standard
- V/Q scan if CTPA is contraindicated
- Echocardiography for right ventricular strain in hemodynamically unstable patients

**Treatment:**

- Systemic anticoagulation (heparin infusion → transition to oral anticoagulant)
- Thrombolysis for massive PE with hemodynamic instability
- Surgical embolectomy or catheter-directed therapy for refractory cases

> **Clinical Pearl:** PE is the leading cause of preventable death in acute SCI. Classic presentation may be absent (no chest pain below the level of injury). Unexplained tachycardia, hypoxia, or autonomic dysreflexia may be the only clues. Maintain a high index of suspicion.

## 9.5 Bradycardia and Cardiac Rhythm Disturbances `[BOARD]`

### 9.5.1 Pathophysiology

The heart receives sympathetic innervation from **T1-T4** via the cardiac accelerator nerves. These sympathetic fibers increase heart rate (positive chronotropy), contractility (positive inotropy), and conduction velocity (positive dromotropy). The heart also receives parasympathetic innervation from the **vagus nerve (CN X)**, which slows heart rate (negative chronotropy).

In cervical SCI (particularly above T1), the cardiac sympathetic outflow is interrupted while vagal innervation remains intact. This produces **unopposed vagal tone**, resulting in:

- **Resting bradycardia** (heart rate 40-60 bpm is common in acute cervical SCI)
- **Exaggerated vagal responses** to stimuli that further increase vagal tone (e.g., tracheal suctioning, which stimulates vagal afferents)
- **Risk of asystole** with vigorous suctioning or other vagal provocations

### 9.5.2 Clinical Features

- Most prominent in **cervical SCI** (above T1), particularly **complete injuries**
- Usually most severe in the **first 2-6 weeks** after injury
- Typically **self-resolving** over weeks to months as autonomic reflexes adapt
- **Suctioning** is a common precipitant of acute bradycardia or asystole in the ICU setting

### 9.5.3 Management

**Symptomatic Bradycardia (HR <40-44 bpm with hypotension or hemodynamic compromise):**

| Intervention | Details |
|---|---|
| Atropine | 0.5-1.0 mg IV; blocks muscarinic (vagal) receptors on the SA node; may repeat every 3-5 min (max total dose 3 mg) |
| Temporary transcutaneous pacing | For refractory bradycardia unresponsive to atropine |
| Temporary transvenous pacing | If transcutaneous pacing is inadequate or long-term pacing is anticipated |
| Preoxygenation before suctioning | Reduces the risk of vagal-mediated bradycardia during tracheal suctioning |
| Limit suction duration | Keep each suction pass to <10-15 seconds to minimize vagal stimulation |

**Permanent pacemaker:** Rarely needed; most bradycardia in SCI resolves spontaneously. Considered only for persistent, symptomatic bradycardia refractory to pharmacologic management.

### 9.5.4 Succinylcholine Contraindication

**Succinylcholine** is a depolarizing neuromuscular blocking agent used for rapid-sequence intubation. In acute SCI, it can be used safely within the first **48 hours** of injury. However, after 48 hours, **succinylcholine is CONTRAINDICATED** due to the risk of **life-threatening hyperkalemia**.

**Mechanism:** Following denervation of skeletal muscle (as occurs in SCI), acetylcholine receptors proliferate across the entire muscle membrane (upregulation of extrajunctional receptors). When succinylcholine --- a depolarizing agent --- activates these widely distributed receptors, massive simultaneous depolarization releases potassium from a vastly larger membrane surface area than normal, producing acute, severe hyperkalemia that can cause cardiac arrest.

The risk begins approximately **48 hours** after injury and persists indefinitely in patients with SCI. Non-depolarizing agents (rocuronium, vecuronium, cisatracurium) are safe alternatives.

> **Clinical Pearl:** NEVER administer succinylcholine to a patient with SCI beyond 48 hours post-injury. Extrajunctional acetylcholine receptor upregulation causes massive potassium efflux upon depolarization, leading to potentially fatal hyperkalemia. This is a classic board examination topic.

## 9.6 Thermoregulatory Dysfunction `[BOARD]`

### 9.6.1 Pathophysiology

Thermoregulation requires intact sympathetic pathways to control two key effector mechanisms: cutaneous vasomotor tone (vasoconstriction to conserve heat, vasodilation to dissipate heat) and sweating (evaporative heat loss). These sympathetic pathways travel from the hypothalamus through the brainstem and spinal cord to exit at multiple thoracolumbar levels.

In SCI above **T8** ("temp-EIGHT-ture"), the hypothalamic thermoregulatory center loses access to the sympathetic effectors below the level of injury. The patient:

- **Cannot sweat below the level of injury** (cannot dissipate heat)
- **Cannot vasoconstrict below the level of injury** (cannot conserve heat)
- **Cannot shiver below the level of injury** (cannot generate heat)

The result is **poikilothermia** --- the body temperature tends to equilibrate with the ambient environmental temperature, similar to a "cold-blooded" organism. In a cold environment, the patient becomes hypothermic; in a warm environment, the patient becomes hyperthermic.

### 9.6.2 Clinical Implications

- Patients with cervical and high thoracic SCI are vulnerable to both hypothermia and hyperthermia
- Environmental temperature control is essential (climate-controlled living environment, appropriate clothing and blankets)
- Exercise and physical activity may produce dangerous overheating because the patient cannot dissipate heat through sweating below the level

### 9.6.3 Differential Diagnosis of Fever in SCI

Fever in a patient with SCI demands a systematic evaluation. Infection is always the first consideration, but several SCI-specific causes must be included in the differential.

| Cause | Key Features |
|---|---|
| **UTI** | Most common cause of fever in SCI; pyuria + bacteriuria + symptoms |
| **Pneumonia** | Second most common; productive cough (or retained secretions), infiltrate on CXR |
| **Wound/pressure injury infection** | Erythema, drainage, warmth around wound/ulcer |
| **Poikilothermia / "Quad fever"** | Temperature elevation without an identifiable infection; diagnosis of exclusion; typically in cervical SCI; environmental exposure history |
| **DVT/PE** | Low-grade fever; leg swelling (DVT), tachycardia/hypoxia (PE) |
| **Heterotopic ossification** | Periarticular warmth, swelling, elevated alkaline phosphatase |
| **Drug fever** | Temporal relationship with medication initiation; diagnosis of exclusion |

**"Quad fever"** refers to temperature elevation in cervical SCI patients without an identifiable infectious source, attributed to the thermoregulatory dysfunction of tetraplegia. It is a **diagnosis of exclusion** --- infection must be ruled out before attributing fever to poikilothermia.

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Temp-EIGHT-ture" --- thermoregulatory dysfunction occurs with injuries above T8. When a patient with SCI presents with fever, always rule out infection first (UTI, pneumonia, wound infection). "Quad fever" is a diagnosis of exclusion.

## 9.7 Long-Term Cardiovascular Disease Risk `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 9.7.1 Accelerated Cardiovascular Disease

Persons with SCI have a significantly elevated risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD) compared to the age-matched able-bodied population. CVD has emerged as the leading cause of death in chronic SCI, surpassing respiratory causes in many long-term follow-up studies. This accelerated CVD risk is driven by a combination of factors:

- **Physical inactivity and deconditioning:** SCI limits exercise capacity, reducing cardiorespiratory fitness and energy expenditure
- **Unfavorable lipid profiles:** Reduced HDL cholesterol is particularly common; LDL and triglyceride elevations are also prevalent
- **Insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes:** Prevalence of glucose intolerance and diabetes is significantly elevated in SCI compared to the general population
- **Obesity:** Body composition changes include increased adiposity (particularly visceral fat) despite potentially normal body weight, due to reduced lean muscle mass
- **Chronic inflammation:** Elevated C-reactive protein and inflammatory markers are common in chronic SCI

### 9.7.2 Metabolic Syndrome in SCI

Metabolic syndrome --- the clustering of central obesity, dyslipidemia, hypertension, and insulin resistance --- is highly prevalent in the SCI population. However, standard diagnostic criteria may underestimate its prevalence because:

- **BMI cutoffs are inaccurate:** The traditional BMI overweight threshold of ≥25 kg/m2 underestimates adiposity in SCI because muscle atrophy reduces body weight while fat mass increases. A **BMI ≥22 kg/m2** has been proposed as the equivalent overweight cutoff in SCI (Laughton et al., 2009)
- **Waist circumference norms** developed for the able-bodied population may not be applicable to seated individuals with altered body habitus

### 9.7.3 Cardiovascular Disease Prevention

- **Exercise:** Adapted exercise programs (arm ergometry, wheelchair sports, functional electrical stimulation cycling) should be encouraged; guidelines recommend at least 20 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous intensity aerobic exercise twice weekly and strength training twice weekly (Martin Ginis et al., 2018)
- **Lipid management:** Screen lipid profiles regularly; statin therapy per standard CVD prevention guidelines
- **Glucose monitoring:** Regular screening for diabetes/prediabetes (fasting glucose, HbA1c)
- **Weight management:** Use SCI-specific BMI cutoffs (≥22 as overweight); dietary counseling
- **Blood pressure management:** Treat hypertension per standard guidelines, recognizing the complexity of labile blood pressure in SCI (OH vs. AD vs. hypertension)
- **Smoking cessation:** Strongly recommended

### 9.7.4 Takotsubo (Stress) Cardiomyopathy `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

Takotsubo cardiomyopathy (stress-induced cardiomyopathy or "broken heart syndrome") has been reported in acute cervical SCI, likely due to the catecholamine surge associated with the acute injury and autonomic disruption. It presents with acute heart failure, wall motion abnormalities (apical ballooning), and elevated troponin, mimicking acute myocardial infarction but with normal coronary arteries. Management is supportive, and recovery is typically complete.

### 9.7.5 QT Prolongation `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

QT prolongation on electrocardiogram has been reported in acute cervical SCI, attributed to autonomic imbalance (unopposed vagal tone, loss of cardiac sympathetic innervation). This may increase the risk of torsades de pointes and sudden cardiac death. Medications that further prolong the QT interval (certain antiarrhythmics, antibiotics such as fluoroquinolones, psychiatric medications) should be used with caution and with ECG monitoring in acute cervical SCI patients.

## 9.8 Summary Table: Cardiovascular Complications by Injury Level `[BOARD]`

| Complication | Level Threshold | Mechanism | Key Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bradycardia | T1-T4 (cardiac sympathetics) | Unopposed vagal tone | Atropine; temporary pacing |
| Autonomic dysreflexia | T6 and above | Uninhibited sympathetic discharge | Sit up; find/remove stimulus; antihypertensives |
| Orthostatic hypotension | T6 and above | Loss of sympathetic vasoconstriction | Binder; stockings; midodrine; fludrocortisone |
| Thermoregulation loss | T8 and above | Loss of sweating/shivering/vasomotor below level | Environmental control; "quad fever" as DDx |
| DVT/PE | All levels (highest in complete) | Virchow's triad | LMWH prophylaxis 8-12 weeks; SCDs |
