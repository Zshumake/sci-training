## 10.1 Introduction `[BOARD]`

Spasticity is one of the most common and clinically significant sequelae of spinal cord injury, affecting an estimated 65-78% of persons with chronic SCI. It is a component of the upper motor neuron (UMN) syndrome and arises from the disruption of descending pathways that normally modulate segmental spinal reflex activity. While spasticity can be disabling --- interfering with function, causing pain, disrupting sleep, and predisposing to contractures --- it can also be beneficial, assisting with transfers, maintaining muscle mass, and supporting venous return. Effective management requires careful assessment of each patient's specific pattern of spasticity, its functional impact, and its relationship to underlying noxious stimuli.

---

## 10.2 Definition `[BOARD]`

### 10.2.1 The Lance Definition (1980)

The most widely cited definition of spasticity was proposed by Lance in 1980:

**"Spasticity is a motor disorder characterized by a velocity-dependent increase in tonic stretch reflexes (muscle tone) with exaggerated tendon jerks, resulting from hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex, as one component of the upper motor neuron syndrome."**

Key elements of this definition:

1. **Velocity-dependent:** The resistance to passive stretch increases with the velocity of stretch. Slow passive stretch may encounter little or no resistance, while rapid stretch elicits a catch or clonus. This velocity dependence distinguishes spasticity from rigidity (which is velocity-independent, as seen in Parkinson disease) and from contracture (which is fixed resistance regardless of velocity).

2. **Tonic stretch reflexes:** The hyperexcitability involves both the tonic (sustained) and phasic (brisk) components of the stretch reflex.

3. **Exaggerated tendon jerks:** Deep tendon reflexes (muscle stretch reflexes) are hyperactive, often with clonus.

4. **One component of the UMN syndrome:** Spasticity does not exist in isolation; it is one of several manifestations of UMN damage.

### 10.2.2 The Upper Motor Neuron Syndrome

The UMN syndrome comprises both **positive phenomena** (release phenomena reflecting hyperexcitable segmental reflexes) and **negative phenomena** (loss of function reflecting loss of descending motor commands).

| Positive Phenomena | Negative Phenomena |
|---|---|
| Spasticity (velocity-dependent tone) | Weakness (paresis/paralysis) |
| Hyperreflexia | Loss of dexterity and fine motor control |
| Clonus | Fatigability |
| Babinski sign (extensor plantar response) | Loss of selective motor control |
| Flexor spasms | Impaired motor planning |
| Extensor spasms | |
| Mass reflex patterns | |
| Clasp-knife phenomenon | |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Spasticity is only one component of the UMN syndrome. The negative phenomena (weakness, loss of dexterity) are often MORE functionally disabling than the positive phenomena (spasticity, spasms). Treatment that reduces spasticity may unmask the underlying weakness, potentially worsening function.

## 10.3 Pathophysiology `[BOARD]`

### 10.3.1 Normal Motor Control

In the intact nervous system, the stretch reflex is modulated by descending supraspinal pathways that exert predominantly inhibitory control over spinal interneurons and alpha motor neurons. The primary descending inhibitory pathways include:

- **Dorsal reticulospinal tract** (medullary reticular formation): Major source of inhibition to flexor reflex afferents and segmental interneurons
- **Corticospinal tract:** Provides both excitatory and inhibitory input to spinal motor circuitry; its loss contributes to disinhibition of segmental reflexes

Other descending pathways (vestibulospinal, medial reticulospinal) are predominantly excitatory to extensor motor neurons. The balance between descending inhibition and excitation maintains normal tone and reflex gain.

### 10.3.2 Effect of Spinal Cord Injury

When the spinal cord is injured, the descending inhibitory pathways (particularly the dorsal reticulospinal and corticospinal tracts) are interrupted. This produces:

1. **Loss of descending inhibition** → segmental reflex arcs become disinhibited
2. **Increased alpha motor neuron excitability** → lower threshold for activation by Ia afferent (muscle spindle) input
3. **Interneuronal reorganization** → changes in spinal interneuron circuits (including altered presynaptic inhibition, altered reciprocal inhibition, and changes in recurrent inhibition via Renshaw cells)
4. **Denervation supersensitivity** → spinal neurons become hypersensitive to residual neurotransmitter input
5. **Sprouting and new synaptic connections** → aberrant connections may form, contributing to abnormal reflex patterns

The net result is hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex: when a spastic muscle is stretched, the Ia afferent signal is amplified by the disinhibited spinal circuitry, producing an exaggerated motor response --- the "velocity-dependent increase in tonic stretch reflexes" described by Lance.

### 10.3.3 Types of Spasms

**Flexor spasms:** Involuntary flexion of the hip, knee, and ankle in response to noxious or non-noxious stimuli (cutaneous or proprioceptive). Mediated by the flexor withdrawal reflex arc. More common in incomplete injuries and with noxious stimuli (e.g., pressure injury, UTI).

**Extensor spasms:** Involuntary extension of the hip and knee with plantar flexion of the ankle. May occur spontaneously or in response to postural changes (e.g., transitioning from sitting to supine). More common in complete injuries. Extensor spasms can be functionally useful (assisting standing and transfers) but can also cause falls if they occur unexpectedly.

**Clonus:** Rhythmic, involuntary oscillation of a joint (most commonly the ankle) in response to sustained passive stretch. Results from the repetitive activation and relaxation of the stretch reflex at a frequency of 5-8 Hz. Sustained clonus (>10 beats) is always pathologic and indicates significant UMN dysfunction.

## 10.4 Assessment `[BOARD]`

### 10.4.1 Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS)

The Modified Ashworth Scale is the most widely used clinical tool for grading spasticity. It assesses resistance encountered during passive movement of a limb through its range of motion at a standardized velocity.

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| **0** | No increase in muscle tone |
| **1** | Slight increase in tone, manifested as a catch and release OR minimal resistance at the end of the range of motion (ROM) |
| **1+** | Slight increase in tone, manifested as a catch, followed by minimal resistance throughout the remainder (less than half) of the ROM |
| **2** | More marked increase in tone through most of the ROM, but the affected part is easily moved |
| **3** | Considerable increase in tone; passive movement is difficult |
| **4** | Affected part is rigid in flexion or extension |

**Limitations:** The MAS does not distinguish spasticity (velocity-dependent neural component) from contracture (fixed mechanical component). It has limited inter-rater reliability, particularly at grades 1 and 1+. It does not capture spasm frequency or functional impact.

### 10.4.2 Penn Spasm Frequency Scale

The Penn Spasm Frequency Scale is a patient self-report measure of spasm frequency.

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| **0** | No spasms |
| **1** | Spasms induced only by stimulation (not spontaneous) |
| **2** | Infrequent spontaneous spasms, occurring less than once per hour |
| **3** | Spontaneous spasms occurring more than once per hour |
| **4** | Spontaneous spasms occurring more than 10 times per hour |

A companion severity scale (1 = mild, 2 = moderate, 3 = severe) can be added to characterize the intensity of spasms.

### 10.4.3 Tardieu Scale

The Tardieu Scale is a more sophisticated assessment tool that addresses the critical limitation of the MAS by differentiating the **dynamic (spastic/neural) component** of tone from the **static (contracture/mechanical) component**.

**Method:**

The joint is passively moved at two different velocities:

1. **Slow velocity (V1):** The joint is moved as slowly as possible through its full passive range. The angle achieved represents **R2** --- the full passive ROM. Any limitation at R2 reflects **fixed contracture** (structural shortening of muscle, tendon, or joint capsule).

2. **Fast velocity (V3):** The joint is moved as fast as possible. The angle at which a "catch" (sudden increase in resistance) occurs represents **R1** --- the angle of the dynamic (spastic) response. This catch represents the velocity-dependent neural component of increased tone.

**Interpretation:**

- **R2 - R1 = Dynamic component** (the portion of tone attributable to spasticity, which is potentially reversible with antispasticity treatment)
- **Large R2-R1 gap:** Most of the resistance is from spasticity (dynamic/neural) → likely to respond well to pharmacologic or interventional treatment
- **Small R2-R1 gap:** Most of the resistance is from contracture (static/mechanical) → will NOT respond to antispasticity medications; requires stretching, serial casting, or surgical intervention

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Tardieu Scale is clinically superior to the MAS because it separates spasticity (treatable with medications) from contracture (requires mechanical intervention). A large R2-R1 gap means the patient has primarily spasticity and will likely respond to pharmacotherapy. A small gap means fixed contracture dominates and medications alone will be insufficient.

### 10.4.4 Additional Assessment Measures

- **Spinal Cord Assessment Tool for Spastic Reflexes (SCATS):** Standardized assessment of clonus, flexor spasms, and extensor spasms specific to SCI
- **Visual Analog Scale (VAS) for spasticity:** Patient self-report of spasticity severity on a 0-10 scale
- **Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS):** Patient-centered outcome measure linking spasticity treatment to individually defined functional goals

## 10.5 Beneficial Versus Harmful Spasticity `[BOARD]`

Not all spasticity is pathologic. The decision to treat spasticity must weigh potential benefits against the functional cost of reducing tone.

### 10.5.1 Beneficial Effects of Spasticity

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Assists with transfers | Extensor tone in the lower extremities helps support the body during pivot transfers |
| Maintains muscle mass | Tonic contraction provides a low-level exercise stimulus that preserves muscle bulk |
| Preserves bone density | Muscle forces transmitted to bone reduce the rate of disuse osteoporosis |
| Supports standing | Extensor spasticity may allow standing in a standing frame or with orthotics |
| Aids venous return | Rhythmic spasms and tonic muscle contraction function as a "muscle pump," reducing venous stasis and potentially lowering DVT risk |
| Maintains limb volume | Prevents severe muscle atrophy and associated cosmetic and functional concerns |

### 10.5.2 Harmful Effects of Spasticity

| Harm | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Interferes with function | Spasms disrupt ADLs, wheelchair propulsion, transfers, and self-care |
| Causes pain | Sustained muscle contraction and spasms can be intensely painful |
| Disrupts sleep | Nocturnal spasms cause frequent awakenings and poor sleep quality |
| Predisposes to contractures | Prolonged muscle shortening leads to fixed joint deformities |
| Causes skin breakdown | Scissoring of legs, friction from spasms, difficulty with positioning |
| Impairs hygiene | Adductor spasticity impairs perineal access; hand spasticity impairs grip release |
| Increases caregiver burden | Difficulty with dressing, bathing, transfers, and positioning |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Always ask: "Is this patient's spasticity helping or hurting them?" Reducing spasticity in a patient who uses extensor tone for transfers may worsen their functional independence. Treatment should target harmful spasticity while preserving beneficial tone.

## 10.6 The "Red Flag" of Sudden Spasticity Increase `[BOARD]`

A sudden, unexpected increase in spasticity in a patient with previously stable tone is a **red flag** that demands investigation for a new **noxious stimulus** below the level of injury. The list of potential triggers overlaps substantially with the triggers for autonomic dysreflexia:

| Category | Noxious Stimulus |
|---|---|
| Genitourinary | UTI, bladder distension, bladder stones, catheter-related irritation |
| Skin | Pressure injury (decubitus ulcer), ingrown toenail, skin laceration, burn |
| Musculoskeletal | Occult fracture (often lower extremity, without pain awareness), heterotopic ossification |
| Gastrointestinal | Constipation, fecal impaction, hemorrhoids, appendicitis |
| Neurologic | Syringomyelia (post-traumatic syrinx formation), tethered cord |
| Other | Tight clothing, orthotic malalignment, DVT |

> **Clinical Pearl:** A sudden increase in spasticity is NOT just "worsening spasticity" --- it is a clinical alarm. The first response should be a systematic search for noxious stimuli: check the bladder, bowel, skin, and musculoskeletal system. Treating the underlying cause often resolves the spasticity increase without any change in antispasticity medications.

## 10.7 Stepwise Management Approach `[BOARD]`

Spasticity management follows a hierarchical, stepwise approach:

### Step 1: Remove Noxious Stimuli
- Evaluate and treat UTI, bladder stones, pressure injuries, ingrown toenails, occult fractures, fecal impaction
- This step is always first and may be all that is needed

### Step 2: Nonpharmacologic Interventions
- **Stretching and range of motion (ROM):** Daily passive and active-assisted ROM through full available range; prevents contracture development and temporarily reduces tone
- **Positioning:** Proper wheelchair seating, bed positioning, and use of positioning devices to maintain muscle length
- **Splinting and orthoses:** Resting hand splints, ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs), and knee immobilizers to maintain joint position and prevent contracture
- **Standing programs:** Standing frame, tilt table, or standing wheelchair; weight-bearing through the lower extremities provides a sustained stretch that reduces lower extremity spasticity
- **Cryotherapy/hydrotherapy:** Cold application or warm water immersion may temporarily reduce tone

### Step 3: Oral Medications
- Systemic pharmacotherapy (see Section 10.8)

### Step 4: Focal Treatments
- Botulinum toxin injection (see Section 10.9)
- Phenol/alcohol nerve blocks (see Section 10.10)

### Step 5: Intrathecal Baclofen Pump
- For generalized, refractory spasticity (see Section 10.11)

### Step 6: Surgical Interventions
- Tendon lengthening, selective dorsal rhizotomy, tenotomy (see Section 10.12)

## 10.8 Oral Medications `[BOARD]`

### 10.8.1 Baclofen

**Mechanism of Action:** Baclofen is a structural analog of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) that acts as an agonist at **GABA-B receptors**. GABA-B receptors are located presynaptically on primary afferent terminals (Ia afferents) and postsynaptically on spinal interneurons and motor neurons. Activation of presynaptic GABA-B receptors inhibits calcium influx and reduces neurotransmitter (glutamate) release from Ia afferents. Activation of postsynaptic GABA-B receptors increases potassium conductance, hyperpolarizing the motor neuron and reducing its excitability.

**Dosing:**
- Starting dose: 5 mg TID (three times daily)
- Titrate gradually: Increase by 5 mg per dose every 3-7 days
- Maximum dose: **80 mg/day** (20 mg QID) --- though some practitioners use higher doses with careful monitoring
- Divided doses throughout the day; higher dose at bedtime if nocturnal spasms are predominant

**Side Effects:**
- Sedation and drowsiness (most common dose-limiting side effect)
- Weakness (particularly problematic in patients who rely on extensor tone)
- Dizziness
- Nausea
- Cognitive impairment
- Crosses the blood-brain barrier **poorly** when administered orally (which limits efficacy but also limits CNS side effects compared to intrathecal delivery)

**Critical Safety Issue --- WITHDRAWAL:**
- Baclofen must **NEVER be discontinued abruptly**
- Abrupt withdrawal can precipitate a life-threatening syndrome resembling malignant hyperthermia or serotonin syndrome: **fever, altered mental status, rebound severe spasticity, rigidity, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, multi-organ failure, and death**
- Always taper baclofen gradually over 1-2 weeks when discontinuing
- This withdrawal risk applies to both oral and intrathecal baclofen (see Section 10.11)

> **Clinical Pearl:** NEVER stop baclofen abruptly. Baclofen withdrawal can cause a life-threatening syndrome of fever, rigidity, seizures, rhabdomyolysis, and death. Always taper gradually over 1-2 weeks.

### 10.8.2 Tizanidine

**Mechanism of Action:** Tizanidine is a centrally acting **alpha-2 adrenergic agonist**. It reduces spasticity by enhancing presynaptic inhibition of motor neurons at the spinal cord level. Alpha-2 agonism in the spinal cord reduces excitatory amino acid release from interneurons, decreasing the excitability of alpha motor neurons.

**Dosing:**
- Starting dose: 2 mg TID (or at bedtime if sedation is prominent)
- Titrate gradually: Increase by 2-4 mg per dose every 3-7 days
- Maximum dose: **36 mg/day** (divided TID-QID)

**Side Effects:**
- Sedation (common; may be therapeutically useful for nocturnal spasms)
- Dry mouth
- Dizziness
- Hypotension (alpha-2 agonism reduces central sympathetic outflow)
- **Hepatotoxicity:** Monitor liver function tests (LFTs) at baseline, at 1, 3, and 6 months, and periodically thereafter. Discontinue if AST/ALT rise to >3 times the upper limit of normal

**Critical Drug Interaction:**
- **Ciprofloxacin** (and other CYP1A2 inhibitors, including fluvoxamine) significantly increases tizanidine blood levels by inhibiting its hepatic metabolism. Concomitant use of tizanidine with ciprofloxacin is **contraindicated** due to the risk of severe hypotension, sedation, and toxicity.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Tizanidine and ciprofloxacin must NOT be used together. Ciprofloxacin inhibits CYP1A2, dramatically increasing tizanidine levels and risking severe hypotension and sedation. This is clinically relevant because fluoroquinolones are commonly prescribed for UTI in SCI patients who may already be on tizanidine.

### 10.8.3 Dantrolene Sodium

**Mechanism of Action:** Dantrolene is unique among antispasticity agents because it acts **peripherally** --- directly on skeletal muscle rather than in the central nervous system. It binds to the **ryanodine receptor (RyR1)** on the sarcoplasmic reticulum of skeletal muscle fibers, inhibiting the release of calcium from intracellular stores. Without adequate calcium release, the actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling that produces muscle contraction is diminished, resulting in weakened muscle force generation.

**Dosing:**
- Starting dose: 25 mg daily
- Titrate gradually: Increase by 25 mg every 4-7 days
- Maximum dose: **400 mg/day** (divided BID-QID)

**Side Effects:**
- Generalized muscle weakness (directly reduces contractile force in ALL muscles, including non-spastic ones; this limits its utility in patients with borderline strength)
- Drowsiness
- Diarrhea
- **Hepatotoxicity:** The most serious adverse effect; fatal hepatitis has been reported. Monitor LFTs at baseline and periodically. Risk is higher in females, patients >35 years, patients taking other hepatotoxic medications, and at doses >400 mg/day

**Unique Feature:** Dantrolene is the **only oral antispasticity medication that acts peripherally** (at the muscle level). All others act centrally (in the brain or spinal cord). This peripheral action means it does not cause sedation or cognitive effects at typical doses but does cause generalized weakness.

### 10.8.4 Diazepam

**Mechanism of Action:** Diazepam is a benzodiazepine that acts as an agonist at **GABA-A receptors** in the brain and spinal cord. It enhances the effect of GABA by increasing the frequency of chloride channel opening, producing hyperpolarization and inhibition of neuronal activity. In the spinal cord, it enhances presynaptic inhibition of spinal reflexes.

**Dosing:**
- Starting dose: 2 mg BID-TID
- Titrate as needed: 2-10 mg BID-QID
- Particularly useful for nocturnal spasms (given at bedtime)

**Side Effects:**
- Sedation (significant; dose-limiting)
- Cognitive impairment
- Physical dependence with chronic use (withdrawal seizures if discontinued abruptly)
- Respiratory depression (particularly in combination with opioids or other CNS depressants)
- Tolerance with chronic use

**Role:** Diazepam is less commonly used as a first-line agent due to sedation and dependence but remains useful for **nighttime spasms** where sedation is desirable rather than adverse.

### 10.8.5 Gabapentin

**Mechanism of Action:** Gabapentin is an anticonvulsant that binds to the alpha-2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels, reducing presynaptic calcium influx and thereby decreasing excitatory neurotransmitter release. Although developed as a GABA analog, it does not directly interact with GABA receptors.

**Dosing:**
- Starting dose: 100-300 mg TID
- Titrate: Increase by 100-300 mg per dose every 3-7 days
- Typical effective dose: 900-3600 mg/day (divided TID)

**Side Effects:**
- Sedation, dizziness
- Peripheral edema
- Weight gain

**Role:** Gabapentin is not a primary antispasticity agent but has a dual benefit in SCI patients who have both spasticity and **neuropathic pain**. Its antispasticity effect is modest compared to baclofen or tizanidine, but it may allow reduction in doses of primary antispasticity medications.

### 10.8.6 Clonidine

**Mechanism of Action:** Clonidine is an **alpha-2 adrenergic agonist** (similar mechanism to tizanidine). It reduces spasticity by enhancing presynaptic inhibition at the spinal cord level. Available as oral tablets or transdermal patch.

**Dosing:**
- Oral: 0.05-0.1 mg BID
- Transdermal patch: 0.1-0.3 mg/day (changed weekly)

**Side Effects:**
- Hypotension (dose-limiting, particularly problematic in SCI patients already prone to orthostatic hypotension)
- Sedation
- Dry mouth
- Rebound hypertension if discontinued abruptly

**Role:** Second-line agent; the transdermal patch provides steady-state drug delivery and may be useful for patients who have difficulty with oral medication schedules.

### 10.8.7 Oral Medication Summary Table

| Medication | Site of Action | Receptor/Target | Starting Dose | Max Dose | Key Side Effects | Monitoring |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Baclofen | CNS (spinal cord) | GABA-B agonist | 5 mg TID | 80 mg/day | Sedation, weakness; WITHDRAWAL SEIZURES | Never stop abruptly |
| Tizanidine | CNS (spinal cord) | Alpha-2 agonist | 2 mg TID | 36 mg/day | Sedation, hypotension, hepatotoxicity | LFTs; avoid ciprofloxacin |
| Dantrolene | Peripheral (muscle) | Ryanodine receptor | 25 mg daily | 400 mg/day | Weakness, hepatotoxicity | LFTs |
| Diazepam | CNS (brain + cord) | GABA-A agonist | 2 mg BID | 40 mg/day | Sedation, dependence, respiratory depression | Taper slowly |
| Gabapentin | CNS | Alpha-2-delta Ca channel | 100 mg TID | 3600 mg/day | Sedation, edema, weight gain | Renal dosing |
| Clonidine | CNS (spinal cord) | Alpha-2 agonist | 0.05 mg BID | 0.4 mg/day | Hypotension, sedation | Avoid abrupt discontinuation |

## 10.9 Botulinum Toxin `[BOARD]`

### 10.9.1 Mechanism of Action

Botulinum toxin (BoNT) is produced by the anaerobic bacterium Clostridium botulinum. It acts at the presynaptic terminal of the neuromuscular junction (NMJ) to block the release of acetylcholine (ACh), producing a chemically mediated, reversible denervation of the injected muscle.

The molecular mechanism involves cleavage of **SNARE (Soluble NSF Attachment Protein Receptor) complex proteins**, which are essential for the fusion of ACh-containing synaptic vesicles with the presynaptic membrane:

- **Botulinum toxin type A** (onabotulinumtoxinA/Botox, abobotulinumtoxinA/Dysport, incobotulinumtoxinA/Xeomin): Cleaves **SNAP-25** (Synaptosome-Associated Protein of 25 kDa)
- **Botulinum toxin type B** (rimabotulinumtoxinB/Myobloc): Cleaves **synaptobrevin (VAMP --- Vesicle-Associated Membrane Protein)**

Without intact SNARE proteins, ACh vesicles cannot fuse with the presynaptic membrane, ACh is not released into the synaptic cleft, and the muscle fiber is not activated. The result is focal muscle weakness/paralysis at the injection site.

### 10.9.2 Clinical Pharmacology

| Parameter | Details |
|---|---|
| Onset of action | 24-72 hours |
| Peak effect | 2-6 weeks |
| Duration of effect | 3-6 months (average 12-16 weeks for type A) |
| Recovery mechanism | Axonal sprouting creates new NMJs; eventually original NMJs recover and sprouts retract |
| Antibody formation | Can develop with repeated injections, leading to secondary treatment failure; risk increased by high doses, frequent injections, and booster doses |

### 10.9.3 Clinical Application in SCI

Botulinum toxin is used for **focal spasticity** --- spasticity that is problematic in specific muscle groups rather than generalized throughout the body. Common targets in SCI include:

| Muscle Group | Indication | Typical Dose (onabotulinumtoxinA) |
|---|---|---|
| Biceps/brachialis | Elbow flexor spasticity | 100-200 units per muscle |
| Forearm flexors | Wrist/finger flexor spasticity | 50-100 units per muscle |
| Hip adductors | Scissoring, hygiene difficulty | 75-300 units per muscle |
| Hamstrings | Knee flexor spasticity | 100-300 units per muscle |
| Gastrocnemius/soleus | Ankle plantar flexor spasticity | 100-300 units per muscle |
| Tibialis posterior | Foot inversion | 50-200 units |

**Injection guidance:** Electromyographic (EMG) guidance, electrical stimulation, or ultrasound guidance improves accuracy of needle placement within the target muscle.

**Maximum dose per session:** Generally 400-600 units of onabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) per session across all injection sites. Doses are NOT interchangeable between formulations (Botox, Dysport, Xeomin, Myobloc have different potency units).

### 10.9.4 Side Effects

- Excessive weakness in the injected muscle (intended effect but may be over-pronounced)
- Diffusion to adjacent muscles causing unintended weakness
- Pain at injection site
- Flu-like symptoms
- Dysphagia (with cervical injections)
- Antibody formation with repeated use (secondary nonresponse)
- Systemic botulism (extremely rare at therapeutic doses)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Botulinum toxin type A cleaves SNAP-25; type B cleaves synaptobrevin (VAMP). Both prevent ACh release at the NMJ. The clinical effect is focal, reversible muscle weakness lasting 3-6 months. It is the treatment of choice for focal spasticity in specific muscle groups.

## 10.10 Phenol and Alcohol Nerve Blocks `[BOARD]`

### 10.10.1 Mechanism

Phenol (5-7% aqueous solution) and ethyl alcohol (45-100%) are chemical neurolytic agents that produce focal destruction of nerve tissue. When injected in proximity to a peripheral nerve, they denature protein and disrupt nerve conduction, producing a chemical denervation of the muscles supplied by that nerve.

### 10.10.2 Clinical Application

| Application | Target | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Obturator nerve block | Obturator nerve | Hip adductor spasticity causing scissoring, impaired perineal hygiene |
| Musculocutaneous nerve block | Musculocutaneous nerve | Elbow flexor spasticity |
| Motor point block | Intramuscular motor points | Focal spasticity in accessible muscles |
| Tibial nerve block | Tibial nerve | Plantar flexor spasticity (equinovarus) |

### 10.10.3 Advantages Over Botulinum Toxin

- Lower cost
- Immediate onset (vs. 24-72 hours for BoNT)
- Useful for large muscle groups where high BoNT doses would be required (and expensive)
- No antibody formation

### 10.10.4 Disadvantages and Complications

- **Dysesthesia:** The most feared complication; occurs when phenol is injected into a **mixed sensorimotor nerve** (containing sensory fibers), producing painful burning, tingling, or causalgia in the nerve's sensory distribution. This risk is minimized by targeting **pure motor nerves** or **motor branches** (e.g., motor branch of the obturator nerve) rather than mixed nerves
- Duration: 3-6 months (similar to BoNT, though some blocks last longer)
- Technical difficulty: Requires EMG or nerve stimulator guidance for accurate localization
- Risk of permanent nerve damage with repeated injections

> **Clinical Pearl:** Phenol neurolysis is most safely applied to pure motor nerves or motor branches. Injection into mixed sensorimotor nerves risks producing dysesthesia --- a painful complication that can be more disabling than the original spasticity.

## 10.11 Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB) Pump `[BOARD]`

### 10.11.1 Rationale

Oral baclofen crosses the blood-brain barrier poorly, which limits its efficacy at tolerable doses and produces systemic side effects (sedation, cognitive effects) before adequate spinal cord levels are achieved. Intrathecal delivery via an implanted pump bypasses the blood-brain barrier entirely, delivering baclofen directly to the cerebrospinal fluid bathing the spinal cord.

**Intrathecal baclofen is approximately 100 times more potent** than oral baclofen because it achieves therapeutic concentrations directly at the GABA-B receptors on spinal interneurons and motor neurons, with minimal systemic absorption and vastly fewer CNS side effects.

### 10.11.2 Patient Selection

ITB is indicated for **severe, generalized spasticity** that:
- Has failed maximal oral pharmacotherapy (inadequate response or intolerable side effects)
- Significantly impairs function, comfort, or care
- Is not amenable to focal treatments alone (botulinum toxin, phenol blocks)

### 10.11.3 The ITB Trial

Before permanent pump implantation, a diagnostic trial is performed to confirm that the patient will respond to intrathecal baclofen.

**Protocol:**
- **Bolus injection:** 50 micrograms (mcg) of baclofen administered intrathecally via lumbar puncture
- **Monitoring:** Clinical assessment of spasticity at 1, 2, 4, 6, and 8 hours post-injection using the Modified Ashworth Scale or other validated measures
- **Positive response:** A reduction of at least **1 point on the MAS** (or clinically significant reduction in spasm frequency/severity) sustained for **4-8 hours**
- If the 50 mcg dose is ineffective, the trial can be repeated with doses of 75 mcg and then 100 mcg on subsequent days

### 10.11.4 Pump Implantation

- A programmable pump (SynchroMed II, Medtronic) is implanted **subcutaneously** in the abdominal wall (lower abdominal quadrant)
- A catheter is tunneled subcutaneously from the pump to the lumbar intrathecal space
- The pump reservoir is refilled percutaneously every 1-6 months (depending on dose and concentration)
- The pump is programmed externally to deliver a continuous basal rate, with or without supplemental bolus doses
- Starting dose: typically 50-100 mcg/day intrathecally
- Dose is titrated over weeks to months to achieve optimal spasticity control

### 10.11.5 ITB Pump Withdrawal --- MEDICAL EMERGENCY

**ITB withdrawal is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY** that can be fatal if not recognized and treated promptly. It occurs when intrathecal baclofen delivery is abruptly interrupted, most commonly due to:

- Catheter malfunction (kink, dislodgement, fracture)
- Pump failure (battery depletion, mechanical malfunction)
- Failure to refill the pump reservoir on schedule
- Programming error

**Clinical Presentation:**

The withdrawal syndrome develops over hours and can progress rapidly to life-threatening severity:

| Early Signs | Late/Severe Signs |
|---|---|
| Increased spasticity (rebound) | Severe rigidity, opisthotonus |
| Pruritus (often the first symptom) | High fever (hyperpyrexia) |
| Agitation, restlessness | Altered mental status, delirium |
| Tachycardia | Seizures |
| Diaphoresis | Rhabdomyolysis |
| | Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) |
| | Multi-organ failure |
| | Death |

The syndrome closely resembles **neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS)** and **malignant hyperthermia**, and shares pathophysiologic features with **serotonin syndrome** (loss of GABA-B-mediated presynaptic inhibition of serotonin release may produce excessive serotonin activity).

**Emergency Management:**

| Intervention | Details |
|---|---|
| Restore ITB delivery | Troubleshoot pump and catheter immediately; bolus dose via pump or side port if accessible |
| Oral baclofen | 10-20 mg every 6 hours (escalate rapidly; oral baclofen is far less potent than ITB but provides partial replacement) |
| Benzodiazepines | Diazepam 5-10 mg IV/PO every 6-12 hours for spasticity, agitation, and seizure prevention |
| Cyproheptadine | 4-8 mg PO every 6-8 hours; a serotonin antagonist that may mitigate the serotonergic component of the withdrawal syndrome |
| IV fluids | Aggressive hydration to prevent renal failure from rhabdomyolysis |
| ICU admission | Continuous monitoring; cooling measures for hyperpyrexia |
| Dantrolene | May be considered for severe rigidity (acts peripherally to reduce muscle contraction) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** ITB pump withdrawal is a medical emergency with mortality if untreated. The triad of rebound spasticity, fever, and altered mental status in a patient with an ITB pump should trigger immediate intervention: restore ITB delivery, administer oral baclofen + benzodiazepines + cyproheptadine, aggressive IV hydration, and ICU admission.

### 10.11.6 ITB Pump Overdose

ITB overdose produces the opposite clinical picture:

- **Profound flaccidity** (loss of all muscle tone)
- **Respiratory depression** (central hypoventilation)
- **Excessive drowsiness progressing to coma**
- Hypotension, bradycardia

**Management:** Supportive care (airway management, mechanical ventilation if needed), reduction or cessation of ITB delivery, physostigmine (controversial, may partially reverse CNS depression). Most cases resolve within 24-48 hours with pump dose reduction.

## 10.12 Surgical Interventions `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 10.12.1 Tendon Lengthening / Release

Surgical lengthening of tendons (e.g., Achilles tendon lengthening, hamstring lengthening, adductor tenotomy) is performed when spasticity has produced **fixed contracture** that cannot be corrected by pharmacologic or conservative measures. The procedure restores joint range of motion and functional positioning.

**Indications:**

- Fixed contracture interfering with function, positioning, seating, or hygiene
- Failed conservative management (stretching, serial casting, pharmacotherapy)
- R2-R1 gap on Tardieu Scale is small (predominantly contracture, not dynamic spasticity)

### 10.12.2 Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy (SDR)

SDR involves selective sectioning of the dorsal (sensory) nerve rootlets at the lumbosacral level. By interrupting the afferent limb of the stretch reflex arc (the Ia afferents from muscle spindles), SDR permanently reduces the excitatory input driving the hyperexcitable motor neurons, thereby reducing spasticity.

**Technique:** Under intraoperative electrophysiological monitoring, individual dorsal rootlets at L1-S2 are stimulated. Rootlets that produce abnormal EMG responses (sustained, spread to multiple myotomes, or failure to habituate) are identified as contributing to spasticity and are selectively cut. Rootlets producing normal responses are preserved to maintain residual sensory function.

**Advantages:**
- Permanent reduction in spasticity (does not require ongoing medication or pump management)
- Reduces tone without the sedation and systemic effects of oral medications

**Disadvantages:**
- Irreversible (cannot "undo" the rhizotomy)
- Loss of sensory input from the cut rootlets (may affect proprioception)
- Does not address contracture (may be combined with orthopedic procedures)
- Primarily used in cerebral palsy; less commonly performed in SCI

### 10.12.3 Serial Casting

Serial casting is a conservative orthopedic technique in which a joint (most commonly the ankle or elbow) is positioned in a cast at the maximum achievable range of motion. The cast is changed every 5-7 days, with each successive cast positioning the joint at a slightly greater range. This progressive, sustained stretch remodels shortened soft tissues (muscle, tendon, joint capsule) and gradually restores range of motion.

**Indications:** Moderate contracture with some remaining dynamic (spastic) component; may be combined with botulinum toxin injection (which reduces the dynamic component) to optimize casting outcomes.

**Complications:** Pressure injury under the cast (particularly in insensate skin), skin maceration, heterotopic ossification exacerbation, DVT (from immobilization).

## 10.13 Spasticity Management in Special Situations `[BOARD]`

### 10.13.1 Spasticity and Function

Before initiating any antispasticity treatment, the clinician must assess the **functional impact** of spasticity. Key questions include:

- Does the patient use extensor spasticity to assist with standing or transfers?
- Does the patient rely on flexor tone for hand grip (tenodesis)?
- Will reducing spasticity unmask weakness that worsens function?
- Can the treatment be targeted (focal) rather than systemic to preserve beneficial tone?

### 10.13.2 Spasticity and Pain

Spasticity and neuropathic pain frequently coexist in SCI and share some common pathophysiologic mechanisms (central sensitization, neuroplastic changes in the spinal cord). Medications that address both conditions (gabapentin, pregabalin) may provide dual benefit.

### 10.13.3 Spasticity and Sleep

Nocturnal spasms are a major cause of sleep disruption in SCI. A bedtime dose of diazepam or baclofen may be specifically beneficial for nocturnal symptoms. Tizanidine, with its sedating properties, is also useful at bedtime.

## 10.14 Integrated Spasticity Management Algorithm `[BOARD]`

```
SUDDEN INCREASE IN SPASTICITY
    |
    v
STEP 1: Search for and treat noxious stimulus
    (UTI, skin breakdown, fracture, constipation, ingrown toenail, syrinx)
    |
    v  (if no noxious stimulus or spasticity persists after treatment)
STEP 2: Nonpharmacologic interventions
    (Stretching, ROM, positioning, splinting, standing program)
    |
    v  (if insufficient)
STEP 3: Oral medications
    First-line: Baclofen (GABA-B agonist)
    Alternatives: Tizanidine (alpha-2 agonist), Dantrolene (peripheral, ryanodine receptor)
    Adjuncts: Diazepam (nighttime), Gabapentin (if concurrent neuropathic pain)
    |
    v  (if focal spasticity predominates or oral meds insufficient)
STEP 4: Focal treatments
    Botulinum toxin (for focal targets)
    Phenol/alcohol blocks (for large muscles or nerve targets)
    |
    v  (if generalized spasticity refractory to oral medications)
STEP 5: Intrathecal baclofen pump
    Trial (50 mcg bolus → positive = ≥1 MAS point reduction for 4-8 hrs)
    Permanent pump implantation
    |
    v  (if contracture has developed or all else fails)
STEP 6: Surgical interventions
    Tendon lengthening, selective dorsal rhizotomy, serial casting
```
