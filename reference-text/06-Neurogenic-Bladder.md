## 6.1 Introduction `[BOARD]`

Neurogenic bladder dysfunction is among the most consequential sequelae of spinal cord injury (SCI), affecting virtually every patient with a clinically significant lesion. The priorities in managing neurogenic lower urinary tract dysfunction, in descending order, are: (1) preservation of upper urinary tract function, (2) prevention of urinary tract infection, (3) achievement of efficient bladder emptying, (4) avoidance of indwelling catheterization when possible, (5) patient concordance with the management plan, and (6) minimization of pharmacologic burden when feasible (Panicker et al., 2015). Before the modern era of intermittent catheterization and urodynamic surveillance, renal failure was the leading cause of death in persons with SCI. Although mortality from renal causes has declined dramatically, urologic complications remain a principal source of morbidity, rehospitalization, and diminished quality of life.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The hierarchy of bladder management priorities places preservation of renal function above all other goals, including continence and convenience.

## 6.2 Neuroanatomy of Micturition `[BOARD]`

### 6.2.1 Central Pathways

Normal micturition requires coordinated interaction between cortical, brainstem, and spinal centers.

**Cerebral Cortex and Frontal Lobe.** The medial prefrontal cortex and anterior cingulate gyrus exert tonic inhibition over the sacral micturition center during the storage phase. This cortical inhibition allows the bladder to fill without triggering involuntary detrusor contraction. Damage to frontal lobe structures (e.g., from stroke, tumor, or traumatic brain injury) results in loss of voluntary inhibition and produces detrusor overactivity with preserved sphincter coordination --- the patient experiences urgency and urge incontinence but does not develop detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD).

**Pontine Micturition Center (PMC) / Barrington's Nucleus.** Located in the dorsolateral pons, the PMC serves as the master "switch" that coordinates voiding. When the cortex releases its inhibitory hold and permits micturition, the PMC simultaneously activates parasympathetic efferents to contract the detrusor and inhibits the external urethral sphincter (EUS) via the pudendal nucleus (Onuf's nucleus) in the sacral cord. This reciprocal coordination ensures that detrusor contraction and sphincter relaxation occur in synchrony. Loss of PMC influence --- as occurs in suprapontine lesions --- results in involuntary detrusor contractions but with preserved coordination (no DSD). In contrast, spinal cord lesions below the pons but above the sacral cord disconnect the PMC from the sacral center, producing DSD, the hallmark of suprasacral neurogenic bladder (de Groat et al., 2015).

> **Clinical Pearl:** The PMC coordinates detrusor contraction with sphincter relaxation. When the spinal cord is injured between the PMC and the sacral center, this coordination is lost, resulting in detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia.

**Sacral Micturition Center.** The sacral micturition center resides in the intermediolateral cell column at S2-S4 and contains the parasympathetic preganglionic neurons that drive detrusor contraction. Onuf's nucleus, located in the ventral horn at the same spinal levels, contains the somatic motor neurons innervating the EUS via the pudendal nerve.

### 6.2.2 Peripheral Pathways

Three peripheral nerve systems control lower urinary tract function. Their actions can be distilled into a clinically memorable framework: **"Sympathetics Store, Parasympathetics Pee."**

**Parasympathetic Innervation (S2-S4, Pelvic Nerve).** Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers arise from the intermediolateral column at S2-S4 and travel via the pelvic nerve to ganglia located in or near the bladder wall. Postganglionic fibers release acetylcholine, which acts on muscarinic M3 receptors on detrusor smooth muscle to produce contraction. Activation of this pathway is the primary mechanism of voiding. Concurrently, parasympathetic activation relaxes the internal urethral sphincter (IUS) via nitric oxide release, facilitating bladder outlet opening.

**Sympathetic Innervation (T11-L2, Hypogastric Nerve).** Preganglionic sympathetic fibers originate from the intermediolateral column at T11-L2 and synapse in the inferior mesenteric ganglion and pelvic plexus. Postganglionic fibers reach the bladder via the hypogastric nerve and exert a dual storage function: (1) alpha-1 adrenergic receptors at the bladder neck and IUS mediate smooth muscle contraction, increasing outlet resistance; (2) beta-3 adrenergic receptors on the detrusor body mediate smooth muscle relaxation, promoting bladder accommodation during filling. The net sympathetic effect is to promote urine storage.

**Somatic Innervation (S2-S4, Pudendal Nerve).** The pudendal nerve carries somatic motor fibers from Onuf's nucleus at S2-S4 to the EUS, which is composed of striated (skeletal) muscle. Voluntary contraction of the EUS provides continence during times of increased abdominal pressure (e.g., coughing, straining) and can be used to abort an impending void. The pudendal nerve also carries sensory afferents from the urethra and perineum.

### 6.2.3 Urethral Sphincter Anatomy

| Feature | Internal Urethral Sphincter (IUS) | External Urethral Sphincter (EUS) |
|---|---|---|
| Muscle type | Smooth muscle | Striated (skeletal) muscle |
| Innervation | Sympathetic (T11-L2, hypogastric nerve) | Somatic (S2-S4, pudendal nerve) |
| Receptor | Alpha-1 adrenergic | Nicotinic cholinergic |
| Control | Involuntary | Voluntary |
| Function | Tonic contraction maintains continence at rest | Voluntary contraction guards against leakage |

### 6.2.4 Receptor Pharmacology

| Receptor | Location | Nerve | Neurotransmitter | Action | Clinical Agonist/Antagonist |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| M3 muscarinic | Detrusor body | Pelvic (parasympathetic) | Acetylcholine | Detrusor contraction (voiding) | Oxybutynin (antagonist) |
| Alpha-1 adrenergic | Bladder neck, IUS | Hypogastric (sympathetic) | Norepinephrine | IUS/bladder neck contraction (storage) | Tamsulosin (antagonist) |
| Beta-3 adrenergic | Detrusor body | Hypogastric (sympathetic) | Norepinephrine | Detrusor relaxation (storage) | Mirabegron (agonist) |
| Nicotinic cholinergic | EUS | Pudendal (somatic) | Acetylcholine | EUS contraction (continence) | --- |

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Sympathetics Store, Parasympathetics Pee" --- the sympathetic system promotes storage through alpha-1-mediated bladder neck contraction and beta-3-mediated detrusor relaxation; the parasympathetic system promotes voiding through M3-mediated detrusor contraction.

## 6.3 Classification of Neurogenic Bladder by Lesion Level `[BOARD]`

### 6.3.1 Upper Motor Neuron (Suprasacral) Bladder

Lesions above the sacral micturition center (above the conus medullaris, typically above T12-L1 vertebral level) interrupt descending pathways from the PMC to the sacral center. The sacral reflex arc remains intact, resulting in reflex detrusor contractions that are involuntary and uncoordinated with sphincter activity.

**Key Features:**

- **Detrusor hyperreflexia (detrusor overactivity):** Involuntary detrusor contractions during filling that the patient cannot inhibit
- **Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD):** Simultaneous contraction of the detrusor and EUS during attempted voiding, present in up to 85% of patients with suprasacral lesions; produces functional obstruction and dangerously elevated intravesical pressures
- **Small functional bladder capacity** due to frequent involuntary contractions
- **High intravesical pressures** during both storage and voiding
- **"Failure to store"** as the primary clinical problem
- **Risk of vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) and hydronephrosis** due to chronically elevated pressures

The combination of DSD and detrusor overactivity creates a "hostile bladder" --- one that generates dangerously high pressures that can be transmitted retrograde to the upper tracts, causing ureteral dilation, VUR, hydronephrosis, and ultimately renal parenchymal damage if untreated.

### 6.3.2 Lower Motor Neuron (Sacral/Infrasacral) Bladder

Lesions at or below the sacral micturition center (conus medullaris or cauda equina) destroy the parasympathetic preganglionic neurons and/or the sacral reflex arc.

**Key Features:**

- **Detrusor areflexia:** Absent or severely diminished detrusor contractions
- **Lax (denervated) sphincter:** Reduced tone at both IUS and EUS
- **Large bladder capacity** because the bladder fills passively without reflex contraction
- **Low intravesical pressures** during storage (generally bladder-safe pressures)
- **"Failure to empty"** as the primary clinical problem
- **Overflow incontinence:** Leakage occurs when intravesical pressure exceeds the diminished urethral closure pressure, typically at large volumes

Although upper tract damage is less common with LMN bladder (because pressures are generally low), chronic urinary retention predisposes to urinary tract infection and bladder overdistension.

### 6.3.3 Mixed Patterns

In clinical practice, many patients exhibit mixed UMN and LMN features, particularly those with injuries near the conus medullaris (T12-L2 vertebral levels) or with incomplete injuries. Urodynamic studies, rather than neurological level alone, are essential for characterizing the specific type of dysfunction and guiding treatment (Stoffel et al., 2017).

| Parameter | UMN (Suprasacral) Bladder | LMN (Sacral/Infrasacral) Bladder |
|---|---|---|
| Detrusor activity | Hyperreflexic (overactive) | Areflexic (underactive) |
| Sphincter tone | Spastic; DSD in up to 85% | Flaccid, denervated |
| Bladder capacity | Reduced | Increased |
| Intravesical pressure | High (dangerous) | Low (generally safe) |
| Primary problem | Failure to store | Failure to empty |
| Incontinence type | Reflex incontinence | Overflow incontinence |
| Upper tract risk | High (VUR, hydronephrosis) | Lower (unless chronic retention) |
| Bulbocavernosus reflex | Present (intact sacral arc) | Absent (damaged sacral arc) |

## 6.4 Spinal Shock Bladder `[BOARD]`

During the period of spinal shock --- which may last days to weeks following acute SCI --- the bladder is areflexic regardless of the neurological level of injury. This occurs because all reflex activity below the level of injury is temporarily suppressed.

### 6.4.1 Acute Phase Management

- **Indwelling (Foley) catheter** is placed during the acute resuscitation and intensive care phase to monitor urine output and prevent overdistension
- An indwelling catheter is appropriate in the acute setting when hemodynamic monitoring and fluid management are paramount

### 6.4.2 Transition to Intermittent Catheterization

The transition from indwelling catheter to clean intermittent catheterization (CIC) should be initiated when:

- Urine output has stabilized to less than **100 mL/hour** (reflecting resolution of post-injury diuresis)
- The patient is off intravenous fluids
- Medical stability permits catheter changes

**Practical considerations for initiating CIC:**

- Goal catheterization volumes should be **less than 500 mL** per catheterization
- Fluid intake should be restricted to **2-3 liters per day** (approximately 1800-2400 mL oral intake) to maintain catheterization volumes within target range
- Catheterization is performed every **4-6 hours** (typically 4-6 times daily)
- Fluid intake should be distributed evenly throughout the day, with restriction of fluids after the evening catheterization to avoid nocturnal overdistension

> **Clinical Pearl:** The indwelling catheter is a bridge --- not a destination. Transition to CIC should occur as soon as urine output stabilizes below 100 mL/hour and IV fluids are discontinued. Every additional day of indwelling catheterization increases the risk of catheter-associated UTI and urethral complications.

## 6.5 Catheterization Methods `[BOARD]`

### 6.5.1 Clean Intermittent Catheterization (CIC)

CIC is the **gold standard** for bladder management in SCI and is recommended as the first-line approach by the Paralyzed Veterans of America (PVA) Consortium for Spinal Cord Medicine and international guidelines (Panicker et al., 2015; Stoffel et al., 2017).

**Technique:**

- Clean (not sterile) technique in the community setting
- Catheter size typically 12-16 French
- Frequency: every 4-6 hours (4-6 times daily), adjusted to keep volumes below 500 mL
- Duration per catheterization: approximately 5-10 minutes

**Functional Requirements:**

- Patients with C7 or higher motor function can typically perform CIC independently
- Patients at C6 level may perform CIC with adaptive devices (tenodesis grip, catheter holders)
- Patients at C5 or above generally require attendant-assisted catheterization

**Advantages:**

- Mimics normal fill-and-empty cycle
- Lowest rate of UTI compared to indwelling methods
- Preserves urethral integrity
- Allows bladder cycling (potentially maintains capacity)
- Associated with higher rate of bladder control recovery (Zheng et al., 2024)

### 6.5.2 Catheter Comparison

| Feature | CIC | Indwelling Urethral (Foley) | Suprapubic Catheter | Condom (External) Catheter |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Infection risk | Lowest | Highest | Lower than urethral indwelling | Low (no instrumentation) |
| Urethral complications | Minimal | Stricture, erosion, fistula | None (bypasses urethra) | None |
| Independence level | C6-C7 with adaptations | Any level | Any level | Males only; requires adequate penile length |
| Bladder cancer risk | Baseline | Elevated after 10+ years | Elevated after 10+ years | Baseline |
| Stone risk | Low | High (catheter as nidus) | Moderate | Low |
| Upper tract risk | Low (if volumes controlled) | High (bypasses storage) | Moderate | Dependent on residuals |
| Continence | Between catheterizations (may leak) | Continuous drainage | Continuous drainage | Collects leakage |
| Patient acceptance | High if can perform | Low satisfaction | Better than urethral Foley | Good if continent |

> **Clinical Pearl:** CIC is associated with more than twice the rate of bladder control recovery within one year compared to indwelling catheterization. It should be offered as the preferred method whenever feasible.

### 6.5.3 Indwelling Urethral (Foley) Catheter

Indwelling urethral catheters are associated with the highest complication rates, including catheter-associated UTI, urethral erosion, stricture, bladder calculi, and squamous cell carcinoma of the bladder with prolonged use (>10 years). They are appropriate for short-term use during acute care but should be transitioned to CIC or other management as soon as possible.

### 6.5.4 Suprapubic Catheter

Suprapubic catheters bypass the urethra and are placed percutaneously into the bladder through the lower abdominal wall. They are associated with fewer urethral complications than indwelling urethral catheters and lower UTI rates. They may be appropriate for patients who cannot perform CIC and for whom an indwelling catheter is unavoidable. However, long-term indwelling catheterization (including suprapubic) still carries elevated risks for calculi and bladder cancer.

### 6.5.5 Condom (External) Catheter

Condom catheters are an option for males with adequate penile length who have reflex voiding or stress incontinence. They collect urine externally without instrumentation of the lower urinary tract. They are appropriate only when post-void residual volumes are acceptable and bladder pressures are safe. Complications include skin maceration, penile ischemia (if applied too tightly), and UTI if used with an occluded drainage system.

## 6.6 Urodynamic Evaluation `[BOARD]`

Urodynamic studies (UDS) are the cornerstone of neurogenic bladder assessment, providing objective data on bladder storage and voiding function that cannot be inferred from neurological examination alone.

### 6.6.1 Components of Urodynamic Testing

**Cystometrogram (CMG):** Measures intravesical pressure during controlled bladder filling. Identifies detrusor overactivity (involuntary contractions during filling), reduced compliance (pressure rise disproportionate to volume infused), and sensory thresholds.

**Electromyography (EMG):** Records activity of the pelvic floor and EUS during filling and voiding. Increased EMG activity during detrusor contraction (when it should normally relax) indicates DSD.

**Pressure-Flow Study:** Measures detrusor pressure and flow rate during voiding. Identifies obstruction (high pressure, low flow --- as seen with DSD) versus underactive detrusor (low pressure, low flow).

**Detrusor Leak Point Pressure (DLPP):** The intravesical pressure at which urine leaks through the urethra in the absence of a detrusor contraction or abdominal straining. This is a critical safety parameter.

**Post-Void Residual (PVR):** Volume remaining in the bladder after voiding or catheterization attempt. Elevated PVR (>100-200 mL) suggests incomplete emptying.

### 6.6.2 Normal Bladder Parameters

| Parameter | Normal Value |
|---|---|
| Total bladder capacity | 400-700 mL |
| First sensation of filling | 100-200 mL |
| Normal desire to void | 200-300 mL |
| Strong desire to void | 300-400 mL |
| Urgency | 400-500 mL |
| Functional capacity | Voided volume + PVR |

### 6.6.3 Urodynamic Findings by Lesion Type

| UDS Parameter | UMN (Suprasacral) | LMN (Sacral/Infrasacral) |
|---|---|---|
| Detrusor contractions | Involuntary, high-pressure | Absent |
| Bladder capacity | Reduced | Increased |
| Compliance | May be reduced | Usually preserved initially |
| EMG during voiding | Increased (DSD) | Decreased or absent |
| Leak point pressure | Often elevated (>40 cmH2O) | Usually low |
| Flow pattern | Intermittent (DSD) | Absent or very low flow |

### 6.6.4 The 40 cmH2O Threshold

In 1981, McGuire and colleagues published a landmark study demonstrating that among 42 patients with myelodysplasia followed for a mean of 7.1 years, none with detrusor leak point pressures below 40 cmH2O developed vesicoureteral reflux (VUR), whereas 68% of those with pressures above 40 cmH2O did (McGuire et al., 1981). Ureteral dilation was observed in 81% of patients with DLPP exceeding this threshold.

This finding established **40 cmH2O** as the clinical danger threshold for upper urinary tract damage and remains one of the most widely cited benchmarks in neurourology. However, subsequent research has clarified that DLPP should not be used as the sole urodynamic parameter, because other factors --- including duration of elevated pressure, bladder compliance, and presence of VUR --- also contribute to upper tract deterioration (Swatesutipun et al., 2022).

> **Clinical Pearl:** A detrusor leak point pressure exceeding 40 cmH2O is associated with a high risk of vesicoureteral reflux and upper tract damage. This threshold, established by McGuire et al. in 1981, remains the benchmark for identifying a "hostile bladder" requiring intervention.

## 6.7 Pharmacotherapy `[BOARD]`

### 6.7.1 Anticholinergic / Antimuscarinic Agents

These medications block M3 muscarinic receptors on the detrusor, reducing involuntary contractions and increasing bladder capacity. They are first-line pharmacotherapy for UMN (overactive) bladder.

**Oxybutynin**

- Mechanism: Nonselective antimuscarinic with direct antispasmodic properties on smooth muscle
- Dosing: Immediate release 5 mg BID-TID; extended release (XR) 5-30 mg daily; topical gel or transdermal patch available
- Side effects: Dry mouth (most common), constipation, blurred vision, cognitive impairment (crosses blood-brain barrier), heat intolerance
- Efficacy: Reduces involuntary detrusor contractions, increases bladder capacity, and decreases incontinence episodes
- Caution: Cognitive side effects are particularly concerning in elderly patients and those with brain injury; XR formulations and topical preparations have fewer anticholinergic side effects

**Tolterodine**

- Mechanism: Competitive muscarinic antagonist with relative bladder selectivity
- Dosing: Immediate release 2 mg BID; extended release 4 mg daily
- Side effects: Similar to oxybutynin but with fewer CNS effects (lower blood-brain barrier penetration)
- Advantage: Better tolerated than oxybutynin in many patients

**Solifenacin, Darifenacin, Fesoterodine, Trospium**

- Additional antimuscarinic agents with varying receptor selectivity and pharmacokinetic profiles
- Trospium is a quaternary amine that does not cross the blood-brain barrier, making it preferred in patients at risk for cognitive effects
- Darifenacin is M3-selective, theoretically reducing cardiac (M2) side effects

### 6.7.2 Beta-3 Adrenergic Agonist

**Mirabegron**

- Mechanism: Selective beta-3 adrenergic receptor agonist; activates beta-3 receptors on the detrusor to promote smooth muscle relaxation during filling, thereby increasing bladder capacity and compliance
- Dosing: 25-50 mg daily
- Side effects: Hypertension, nasopharyngitis, urinary tract infection; notably **no anticholinergic side effects** (no dry mouth, constipation, or cognitive effects)
- Evidence in neurogenic bladder: Prospective randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trials have demonstrated significant improvements in volume at first detrusor contraction, bladder compliance, and patient-reported outcomes in patients with neurogenic detrusor overactivity from SCI (Krhut et al., 2018). Real-world data confirm sustained benefit with low adverse event rates
- Role: Increasingly used as first-line therapy or as add-on to antimuscarinics; particularly advantageous in patients who cannot tolerate anticholinergic side effects

**Vibegron**

- Mechanism: Beta-3 adrenergic agonist similar to mirabegron
- Dosing: 75 mg daily
- Advantage: Does not inhibit CYP2D6 (fewer drug interactions than mirabegron)

### 6.7.3 Alpha-1 Adrenergic Antagonist

**Tamsulosin**

- Mechanism: Selective alpha-1A adrenergic receptor blocker; relaxes smooth muscle at the bladder neck and proximal urethra, reducing outlet resistance
- Dosing: 0.4 mg daily
- Side effects: Orthostatic hypotension, dizziness, retrograde ejaculation
- Role: Used in neurogenic bladder to facilitate emptying by reducing bladder outlet resistance; may be combined with anticholinergics (antimuscarinic reduces storage pressure while alpha-blocker reduces outlet resistance)

### 6.7.4 Botulinum Toxin A (OnabotulinumtoxinA) Intradetrusor Injection

- Mechanism: Blocks presynaptic release of acetylcholine at the neuromuscular junction by cleaving SNAP-25, a SNARE complex protein essential for synaptic vesicle fusion; also inhibits release of ATP and substance P from urothelial afferents
- Dosing: **200 units** injected into the detrusor muscle at 20-30 sites (sparing the trigone) via cystoscopy; FDA-approved for neurogenic detrusor overactivity
- Onset: 1-2 weeks; peak effect at 4-6 weeks
- Duration: 6-9 months (average 8-9 months between injections)
- Efficacy: Reduces mean urinary incontinence episodes by approximately 21 per week (vs. 9 for placebo); significantly increases maximum cystometric capacity and reduces maximum detrusor pressure (Cruz et al., 2011; Ginsberg et al., 2012)
- Side effects: Urinary tract infection (34-48%), urinary retention requiring CIC (if not already catheterizing), hematuria
- Indication: Refractory neurogenic detrusor overactivity that has not responded to oral pharmacotherapy

### 6.7.5 Pharmacotherapy Summary Table

| Medication | Class | Mechanism | Dosing | Key Side Effects | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Oxybutynin | Antimuscarinic | M3 antagonist | 5 mg BID-TID or XR 5-30 mg/day | Dry mouth, constipation, cognitive | First-line for overactive detrusor |
| Tolterodine | Antimuscarinic | Muscarinic antagonist | 2 mg BID or ER 4 mg/day | Fewer CNS effects than oxybutynin | First-line alternative |
| Mirabegron | Beta-3 agonist | Beta-3 receptor agonist | 25-50 mg daily | Hypertension; no anticholinergic SEs | First-line or add-on |
| Tamsulosin | Alpha-1 blocker | Alpha-1A antagonist | 0.4 mg daily | Orthostatic hypotension, ejaculatory dysfunction | Reduces outlet resistance |
| OnabotulinumtoxinA | Neurotoxin | Cleaves SNAP-25 | 200 U intradetrusor | UTI, retention | Refractory overactivity |
| Desmopressin | Vasopressin analog | V2 receptor agonist | 0.1-0.4 mg PO HS | Hyponatremia | Nocturnal polyuria |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Mirabegron offers a genuinely different mechanism (beta-3 agonism) with no anticholinergic burden, making it an ideal agent for patients who experience dry mouth, constipation, or cognitive effects from antimuscarinics.

## 6.8 Urinary Tract Infection `[BOARD]`

### 6.8.1 Epidemiology

UTI is the **most common medical complication** of SCI and the **number one cause of rehospitalization** in the SCI population. Patients with SCI experience an average of 2-3 symptomatic UTIs per year, with incidence varying by bladder management method (lowest with CIC, highest with indwelling catheter).

### 6.8.2 Diagnosis: Symptomatic UTI vs. Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

The diagnosis of UTI in the SCI population requires the simultaneous presence of three elements (Consortium for Spinal Cord Medicine, 2006):

1. **Pyuria:** Greater than 10 white blood cells (WBC) per mm3 (or per high-power field in uncentrifuged specimen)
2. **Significant bacteriuria:**
   - Greater than 100,000 (10^5) colony-forming units (CFU)/mL from a clean-catch specimen
   - Greater than 100 (10^2) CFU/mL from a catheterized specimen
3. **Signs and/or symptoms** attributable to UTI, which in the SCI population may include:
   - Fever, chills, rigors
   - New or worsening incontinence
   - Increased spasticity
   - Autonomic dysreflexia (in patients with injuries at T6 or above)
   - Cloudy or malodorous urine
   - Suprapubic discomfort or flank pain (if sensation present)
   - Malaise, lethargy
   - Hematuria

**Asymptomatic bacteriuria (ASB)** --- the presence of bacteria in the urine without accompanying symptoms --- is **extremely common** in the SCI population (prevalence 50-90% in those performing CIC) and should **NOT be treated with antibiotics**. Treatment of ASB promotes antibiotic resistance, increases the risk of Clostridium difficile infection, and does not reduce the incidence of symptomatic UTI (Nicolle et al., 2019).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Asymptomatic bacteriuria is the rule, not the exception, in SCI patients performing CIC. Treating a positive urine culture in the absence of symptoms is not only unhelpful but harmful --- it drives antibiotic resistance without clinical benefit.

### 6.8.3 Prevention

- CIC with clean technique (lowest infection rates of any catheterization method)
- Adequate fluid intake (2-3 L/day)
- Regular, complete bladder emptying (volumes <500 mL)
- Avoidance of indwelling catheters when possible
- Cranberry products have shown inconsistent benefit in randomized trials
- Methenamine hippurate may reduce UTI incidence in selected patients
- Antibiotic prophylaxis is generally not recommended for routine CIC due to resistance concerns

## 6.9 Vesicoureteral Reflux `[BOARD]`

### 6.9.1 Pathophysiology

In the normal bladder, the distal ureter traverses the bladder wall at an oblique angle through the detrusor muscle before opening at the ureteral orifice. This oblique intramural course creates a valve mechanism: as the bladder fills and the wall stretches, the intramural ureter is compressed, preventing retrograde flow of urine from the bladder to the kidney.

In neurogenic bladder, chronically elevated intravesical pressures (particularly from DSD and reduced compliance) cause detrusor wall hypertrophy. This hypertrophy alters the ureteral course from oblique to more perpendicular, rendering the vesicoureteral junction (VUJ) incompetent. The result is VUR --- retrograde flow of urine (often infected) from the bladder to the upper tracts.

### 6.9.2 Consequences

- Hydronephrosis and hydroureter
- Recurrent pyelonephritis
- Renal scarring and parenchymal loss
- Progressive renal insufficiency
- Historically the leading cause of death in SCI patients before the era of CIC and urodynamic surveillance

### 6.9.3 Prevention and Management

- Maintain detrusor leak point pressure below 40 cmH2O
- CIC with or without anticholinergics to reduce intravesical pressure
- Botulinum toxin intradetrusor injection for refractory cases
- Augmentation cystoplasty for refractory high-pressure bladder (see Section 6.12)
- Annual renal ultrasound for surveillance

## 6.10 Bladder Calculi `[BOARD]`

### 6.10.1 Epidemiology and Pathogenesis

Bladder stones are the **second most common urologic complication** of SCI. The risk is strongly associated with the use of indwelling catheters, which serve as a nidus for stone formation and promote chronic bacteriuria.

**Mechanism:** Urease-producing organisms --- particularly **Proteus mirabilis, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Klebsiella species** --- split urea into ammonia and carbon dioxide, alkalinizing the urine. In an alkaline environment, magnesium ammonium phosphate (struvite) and calcium phosphate (apatite) precipitate, forming "infection stones" or struvite calculi. These stones can grow rapidly and encase an indwelling catheter, contributing to catheter malfunction and recurrent infection.

### 6.10.2 Prevention

- Avoidance of indwelling catheters when possible
- Regular catheter changes (every 4 weeks for indwelling catheters)
- Adequate fluid intake to maintain dilute urine
- Prompt treatment of symptomatic UTI
- Acidification of urine (theoretical benefit, limited evidence)

### 6.10.3 Treatment

- Cystolitholapaxy (endoscopic fragmentation and removal) is first-line
- Open cystolithotomy for very large stones
- Treatment of underlying UTI
- Catheter change or conversion from indwelling to CIC to prevent recurrence

## 6.11 Bladder Cancer `[BOARD]`

### 6.11.1 Risk and Histology

SCI patients, particularly those with long-term (>10 years) indwelling catheterization, have a significantly elevated risk of bladder cancer. The risk is estimated at 16-28 times that of the general population in some studies (Groah et al., 2002; Subramonian et al., 2004).

A distinctive feature of bladder cancer in the SCI population is the high proportion of **squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)**, which accounts for approximately 40-50% of bladder cancers in chronically catheterized SCI patients --- in stark contrast to the general population, where more than 90% of bladder cancers are transitional cell (urothelial) carcinoma. This shift toward SCC is attributed to chronic irritation, inflammation, and squamous metaplasia caused by long-term catheter-associated mucosal trauma and recurrent infection.

### 6.11.2 Surveillance

- Annual cystoscopy has been recommended by some experts for patients with indwelling catheters for more than 5-10 years, though consensus guidelines are lacking
- Urine cytology has low sensitivity for SCC
- Vigilance for hematuria, recurrent UTI, and changes in bladder function

> **Clinical Pearl:** Squamous cell carcinoma --- not transitional cell carcinoma --- is the predominant histologic type of bladder cancer in SCI patients with long-term indwelling catheters. Clinicians should maintain high vigilance after 10 or more years of catheterization.

## 6.12 Long-Term Urologic Surveillance `[BOARD]`

### 6.12.1 Recommended Monitoring

| Study | Frequency | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Renal ultrasound | Annually | Screen for hydronephrosis, renal calculi, renal atrophy |
| Urodynamic studies | Baseline, then periodic (annually or with changing symptoms) | Assess pressures, compliance, DSD; guide pharmacotherapy |
| Serum creatinine | Annually | Monitor renal function (note: may underestimate dysfunction) |
| Creatinine clearance (24-hour urine or calculated) | Periodic | More sensitive than serum creatinine for detecting early decline |
| Urine culture | Only if symptomatic | Diagnose UTI; do NOT screen asymptomatic patients |
| Cystoscopy | Consider after 5-10 years of indwelling catheter | Screen for malignancy |

### 6.12.2 Serum Creatinine Limitations

In SCI patients, serum creatinine may be misleadingly low due to reduced muscle mass (the primary source of creatinine). Therefore, a "normal" serum creatinine does not exclude significant renal impairment. **Creatinine clearance** (either 24-hour urine collection or calculated using the Cockcroft-Gault or CKD-EPI formula, with appropriate adjustment for reduced muscle mass) is a more sensitive indicator of early renal deterioration.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Do not be reassured by a "normal" serum creatinine in a patient with SCI. Reduced muscle mass lowers creatinine production, masking renal impairment. Creatinine clearance is a more sensitive measure of kidney function in this population.

## 6.13 Augmentation Cystoplasty `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 6.13.1 Indications

Augmentation cystoplasty is considered for patients with refractory high-pressure, low-compliance bladders that fail to respond to maximal pharmacotherapy (anticholinergics, mirabegron, botulinum toxin) and who are at ongoing risk for upper tract damage.

### 6.13.2 Technique

The procedure involves detubularization of a segment of bowel (most commonly ileum, less often sigmoid colon or stomach) and patching it onto the bivalved bladder to increase capacity and improve compliance. The interposition of bowel mucosa disrupts the coordinated detrusor contraction, effectively abolishing high-pressure overactivity.

### 6.13.3 Outcomes and Complications

- Significant improvement in bladder capacity and compliance
- Reduction or elimination of VUR and high-pressure episodes
- Patients invariably require CIC postoperatively (no spontaneous voiding)
- Complications: mucus production (bowel mucosa), metabolic acidosis (ileal segment absorbs chloride and excretes bicarbonate), recurrent UTI, bladder calculi (mucus nidus), and a small risk of malignancy at the bowel-bladder anastomosis (long-term surveillance required)

## 6.14 Sacral Neuromodulation and Electrical Stimulation `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 6.14.1 Sacral Anterior Root Stimulation (Brindley Procedure)

The Finetech-Brindley sacral anterior root stimulator involves implantation of electrodes on the S2-S4 anterior (motor) nerve roots, allowing electrical stimulation to produce coordinated detrusor contraction on demand. To prevent DSD during stimulated voiding, the procedure is combined with posterior (sensory) sacral rhizotomy, which eliminates reflex detrusor overactivity and DSD by interrupting the afferent limb of the sacral reflex arc.

**Outcomes:** Improved bladder emptying, continence between stimulations, and reduced UTI rates. The posterior rhizotomy component abolishes reflex erections and sacral sensation, which must be discussed with the patient preoperatively.

### 6.14.2 Sacral Neuromodulation (InterStim)

Sacral neuromodulation via implanted electrodes at the S3 foramen modulates sacral nerve activity and has been used primarily for non-neurogenic overactive bladder. Its role in neurogenic bladder (including SCI) remains an area of active investigation, with some evidence of benefit in incomplete injuries.

---

## 6.15 Vanilloid Receptor Agonists `[COMPREHENSIVE]`

### 6.15.1 Capsaicin and Resiniferatoxin

Capsaicin and resiniferatoxin are vanilloid receptor (TRPV1) agonists that, when instilled intravesically, initially stimulate and then desensitize C-fiber afferents in the bladder wall. These unmyelinated C-fibers become the dominant afferent pathway driving reflex detrusor overactivity after SCI (replacing the normally dominant A-delta fibers).

**Rationale:** By desensitizing C-fibers, vanilloid agonists reduce involuntary detrusor contractions, increase bladder capacity, and decrease incontinence episodes.

**Resiniferatoxin (RTX):** An ultrapotent capsaicin analog (approximately 1,000 times more potent) that produces desensitization with less of the initial burning/irritation associated with capsaicin. Clinical trials have shown improvements in bladder capacity and reduction in incontinence, but the results have been inconsistent, and neither agent has achieved widespread clinical adoption. Both remain largely investigational in the context of SCI-related neurogenic bladder.

## 6.16 Special Considerations `[BOARD]`

### 6.16.1 Autonomic Dysreflexia and the Bladder

Bladder distension is the **single most common trigger** of autonomic dysreflexia (AD) in patients with injuries at T6 or above. Any bladder management failure --- blocked catheter, missed catheterization, overdistension --- can precipitate a potentially life-threatening episode of AD. (See Chapter 9 for a detailed discussion of AD management.)

### 6.16.2 Pregnancy and Neurogenic Bladder

Pregnant women with SCI and neurogenic bladder require close urologic monitoring. Increased urine production, mechanical compression of the bladder by the gravid uterus, and altered pharmacokinetics necessitate adjustments to the catheterization schedule and medication regimen. Anticholinergics are generally avoided during pregnancy due to limited safety data; CIC frequency may need to increase to accommodate increased urine volumes.

### 6.16.3 Aging and Neurogenic Bladder

The aging SCI population faces compounding risks from age-related changes in bladder function (reduced compliance, prostatic hypertrophy in males) superimposed on neurogenic dysfunction. Ongoing urodynamic surveillance is particularly important in this group.

## 6.17 Integrated Bladder Management Algorithm `[BOARD]`

**Step 1: Acute Phase (Spinal Shock)**
- Indwelling catheter for monitoring
- Transition to CIC when urine output <100 mL/hr, off IV fluids

**Step 2: Characterize Bladder Dysfunction**
- Urodynamic study (CMG, EMG, pressure-flow, DLPP)
- Classify as UMN (overactive/DSD) or LMN (areflexic)

**Step 3: Initiate Bladder Program**
- CIC every 4-6 hours (gold standard for both UMN and LMN)
- Fluid management: 2-3 L/day, distributed evenly
- Goal volumes: <500 mL per catheterization

**Step 4: Pharmacotherapy (UMN/Overactive Bladder)**
- First-line: Antimuscarinic (oxybutynin, tolterodine) OR mirabegron
- Combination therapy if monotherapy insufficient
- Add tamsulosin if outlet resistance contributes to poor emptying

**Step 5: Refractory Cases**
- Botulinum toxin 200 U intradetrusor
- Re-evaluate with urodynamics after treatment

**Step 6: Surgical Options (if all else fails)**
- Augmentation cystoplasty
- Sacral anterior root stimulation
- Urinary diversion (last resort)

**Ongoing: Surveillance**
- Annual renal US
- Periodic UDS
- Creatinine clearance
- Cystoscopy if indwelling catheter >5-10 years
